The Cloze Procedure in Reading Comprehension Assessment: From Taylor's 1953 Innovation to Contemporary Masked Language Modeling

This comprehensive report traces the historical evolution, theoretical foundations, empirical validation, and contemporary applications of the cloze procedure—a methodology for assessing reading comprehension that has remained remarkably influential in educational assessment for over seventy years. Originated by Wilson L. Taylor in 1953 as a tool for measuring text readability, the cloze procedure has undergone significant theoretical refinement, empirical scrutiny, and computational reinvention, culminating in its recognition as functionally equivalent to the masked language modeling techniques now central to modern natural language processing systems like BERT and GPT. This report examines how the cloze test emerged from information-theoretic principles, became institutionalized in educational assessment contexts, encountered substantial construct validity challenges, generated rich psycholinguistic research into word prediction and reading behavior, and ultimately found unexpected validation in computational linguistics through the parallel development of masked language models. The evidence presented here demonstrates that while the cloze procedure's status as a unitary measure of language proficiency remains contested, its value as a window into predictive language processing—both human and computational—has only deepened with recent advances in cognitive neuroscience and artificial intelligence.

The Conceptual Origins and Information-Theoretic Foundations of the Cloze Procedure

The cloze procedure entered the research literature as a deliberate innovation designed to address perceived limitations of existing readability measurement techniques[1]. Wilson L. Taylor, working at the University of Illinois in 1953, introduced this psychological tool with the explicit goal of measuring "the effectiveness of communication" by deleting selected words from text and asking readers to restore the missing elements[1]. Taylor's fundamental insight was that readers engaged in gap-filling necessarily demonstrated their understanding of language patterns and semantic relationships within extended discourse; thus, their success rate at completion could serve as an empirical measure of textual comprehensibility. Rather than relying on algorithmic formulas that counted syllables and sentence length—as existing readability measures like Flesch and Dale-Chall did—the cloze procedure would engage actual human comprehenders in a task that captured the interactive complexity of real reading[1]. This methodological choice proved revolutionary because it moved assessment away from surface features of the text itself toward the actual cognitive processing that readers performed.

The theoretical foundation of the cloze procedure rested fundamentally on principles derived from Gestalt psychology and information theory[1][20]. Taylor explicitly drew on the Gestalt concept of "closure," the perceptual tendency to complete incomplete patterns, arguing that readers naturally attempted to restore mutilated language patterns to their complete forms[1]. However, beyond this psychological principle, Taylor anchored the procedure in information-theoretic concepts that would later prove prescient for understanding both human cognition and machine learning. The notion that language constitutes patterned sequences that readers could probabilistically complete connected directly to the statistical properties of language that Claude Shannon had begun systematically investigating in his foundational work on information theory[1]. While Taylor's original 1953 formulation did not explicitly employ Shannon entropy calculations, the conceptual equivalence was implicit: the cloze task asked readers to estimate which words should follow given contexts, a fundamentally probabilistic operation that information theory could formalize[4].

Two theoretical strands particularly shaped early cloze research[1]. First, the concept of "total language context" encompassed the idea that comprehension emerged from the interaction of semantic, syntactic, and pragmatic constraints operating simultaneously across language sequences[1]. Rather than measuring isolated linguistic competencies—phoneme recognition, grammar rules, vocabulary definitions—the cloze procedure inherently sampled from all these sources simultaneously, as readers drew upon whatever contextual information would help them predict missing words. Second, drawing from Charles Osgood's work on "dispositional mechanisms," Taylor recognized that readers possessed probabilistic expectations about language continuity, what Osgood characterized as the psychological readiness or preparedness to produce certain linguistic forms in given contexts[1]. The cloze test measured precisely these dispositional mechanisms—the actual language patterns that skilled readers anticipated during comprehension.

The relationship between deletion rate and gap complexity illustrates how early cloze researchers grappled with information-theoretic principles. When every fifth word was deleted, for instance, readers encountered gaps distributed evenly throughout the text, creating a uniform sampling of the language. Yet subsequent research demonstrated that deletion rate profoundly affected task difficulty, which modern information theory can explain precisely: the entropy of possible word continuations varies substantially depending on context strength[4]. Information entropy quantifies the degree of uncertainty or "surprise" associated with different gap locations; gaps occurring after high-predictability contexts (such as "peanut _____" where "butter" is nearly certain) involve lower entropy and thus represent easier gaps, while gaps in semantically or syntactically ambiguous contexts involve higher entropy and thus greater difficulty[4]. This insight—that entropy could serve as a proxy for gap complexity—did not emerge until 2019 in Maltaev and colleagues' computational analysis, yet it was conceptually implicit in Taylor's original approach[4].

Mechanical Implementation and Theoretical Sophistication: How Cloze Procedure Operates

The cloze procedure's mechanics, despite their apparent simplicity, embodied genuine theoretical sophistication about language processing[1]. In Taylor's original conception, the researcher selected a text passage, mutilated it by deleting selected words, and presented this modified version to readers who attempted to restore the missing elements[1]. A "cloze unit" was defined as any single occurrence of a successful completion—when a reader produced a word at a blank that matched the original text[1]. The procedure generated "a considerable number of cloze units" across a passage, which then underwent statistical analysis to determine how frequently readers' restorations matched the original text. When a substantial percentage of readers produced the identical word originally present in the text, this demonstrated strong mutual understanding between writer and reader; when few readers produced the original word, this indicated contextual ambiguity or comprehension difficulty[1].

Yet the procedure incorporated flexibility that allowed investigators to target specific linguistic phenomena. Taylor noted that researchers need not follow mechanistic deletion patterns; they could instead employ "rational deletion" whereby only certain word types—nouns, main verbs, or adjectives—were systematically removed[3]. This selective deletion allowed assessment of whether readers comprehended particular grammatical categories or semantic domains. Furthermore, scoring procedures admitted variation: the "exact word" method required precise matches to the original text, while "acceptable answer" methods permitted synonyms or semantically equivalent completions[24][30]. This flexibility in both deletion and scoring would later prove both advantageous and problematic, as it meant research findings could vary dramatically depending on methodological choices[11][11].

The procedure also demonstrated theoretical sophistication in how it balanced multiple language-processing demands. Unlike discrete-point tests that isolated individual linguistic skills, the cloze procedure inherently integrated multiple sources of information[36]. Readers could not rely solely on syntactic knowledge when filling blanks; they necessarily invoked semantic associations, discourse coherence understanding, vocabulary knowledge, and pragmatic reasoning about what interpretations made sense in context[11]. For researchers investigating language proficiency holistically, this integration represented a profound advantage: the cloze procedure captured the unified, simultaneous operation of multiple language systems that characterize authentic comprehension[36]. For researchers seeking to diagnose specific deficits or isolate particular linguistic competencies, this same integration represented a confound—the procedure could not determine whether errors reflected syntactic misunderstanding, vocabulary limitations, or discourse comprehension failures[11].

Theoretical Expansion: Oller's Pragmatic Expectancy Grammar and the Integration Thesis

The 1970s and 1980s witnessed substantial theoretical expansion of cloze procedure concepts, most prominently through John Oller's influential pragmatic expectancy grammar framework[8][11][36]. Oller (1979) proposed a theoretical model arguing that language proficiency constituted fundamentally a unified, integrative system rather than a collection of discrete, separable component skills[8][11][36]. From this perspective, cloze tests represented not mere readability assessment tools but rather integrative instruments capable of measuring underlying language competence directly[8][29]. Pragmatic expectancy grammar, as Oller articulated it, described the psychological mechanisms by which comprehenders processed sequential language elements while respecting "the normal contextual constraints of that language" and relating these linguistic sequences to extralinguistic contexts through pragmatic mappings[8]. Cloze tests, in this theoretical framework, directly engaged these pragmatic expectancy mechanisms: readers had to extract what a writer intended to communicate, then predict which linguistic forms would cohere with that intention within the sentence or discourse being processed[8].

This pragmatic theoretical perspective fundamentally reframed what cloze tests purportedly measured. Rather than assessing isolated reading skills, cloze procedures ostensibly tapped into the integrated underlying competence that Oller conceptualized as the foundation of all language use[11][36]. The theory predicted that cloze test scores should correlate substantially with diverse measures of language proficiency—listening comprehension, speaking ability, writing quality, grammatical knowledge—precisely because all these abilities drew upon the same underlying pragmatic expectancy system[8][11][29]. Research findings initially appeared to support this integration thesis: numerous studies reported moderate to high correlations between cloze test performance and other measures of language proficiency, with some researchers finding cloze test scores predicted performance on listening comprehension, reading comprehension, writing assessments, and Foreign Service Institute oral interviews[11][11]. Based on these correlations, cloze tests gained adoption as integrative proficiency measures in high-stakes contexts, most notably in the Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL)[32].

However, Oller's integration thesis, while theoretically elegant and initially empirically promising, would eventually encounter substantial challenges. The theoretical claim that a single underlying pragmatic expectancy grammar could account for all language performance was difficult to square with evidence that readers apparently used different processing mechanisms in different contexts[11][11]. Furthermore, the methodology most suitable for testing Oller's theory—correlational analysis—proved inadequate to resolve what constructs cloze tests actually measured[11]. When more sophisticated statistical approaches, particularly structural equation modeling, were applied to cloze test data beginning in the 1990s, more complex pictures emerged, suggesting that cloze tests measured lexical and grammatical knowledge at the sentential level rather than unified pragmatic competence[11][11]. Despite these theoretical complications, Oller's framework remained influential in establishing cloze procedures as serious assessment instruments worthy of institutional adoption.

Educational Institutionalization: From Research Tool to Standardized Assessment

Beginning in the 1960s and accelerating through the 1980s, the cloze procedure underwent institutionalization as an assessment tool within educational systems. Research conducted with elementary school students demonstrated that cloze test performance could identify instructional reading levels comparable to traditional informal reading inventories[9]. Teachers and reading specialists found the procedure appealing: it required no specialized equipment, could be administered to entire classrooms simultaneously, and produced quantitative scores amenable to statistical analysis. The procedure proved particularly valuable in second language learning contexts, where researchers sought to assess whether English language learners had developed sufficient command of linguistic patterns and vocabulary to comprehend academic texts[7][29].

The standardization of cloze methodology crystallized in the 1970s and 1980s, with broad agreement emerging that "mechanical deletion" of every fifth word with standardized blank length represented the standard implementation[9]. This methodological standardization facilitated comparison across studies and contexts; it also simplified administration and scoring for educational practitioners. However, research established early that even seemingly minor variations—whether every fifth or every seventh word was deleted, whether deletion occurred from all word categories or only content words, whether scoring accepted synonymous answers or required exact-word matches—significantly affected test difficulty, reliability, and validity estimates[11][11][47]. This methodological sensitivity created ongoing tension: strict standardization enabled comparison but potentially obscured the flexibility that made cloze procedures useful for targeting specific instructional objectives[33].

The adoption of cloze procedure variants in major standardized tests exemplified its institutionalization. The Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL) incorporated cloze-type items into its assessment battery, with the multiple-choice variant (where readers selected missing words from provided options rather than generating them) proving especially popular for large-scale testing contexts[32][24]. Research examining whether multiple-choice cloze items measured the same constructs as free-response cloze tests yielded mixed findings: some evidence suggested moderate correlations between these variants, while other evidence indicated they assessed somewhat different skills, with multiple-choice cloze perhaps being more sensitive to lexical knowledge and free-response cloze more dependent on syntactic pattern recognition[32]. The prevalence of cloze variants in standardized tests documented the procedure's acceptance as a legitimate and practical reading assessment tool, even as theoretical questions about what it measured remained unresolved.

Construct Validity Debates: What Does the Cloze Procedure Actually Measure?

Despite decades of research, the fundamental question "What does the cloze procedure measure?" remained contentious through the 1980s and 1990s[11][11]. Research findings revealed dramatically inconsistent reliability estimates, ranging from a low of 0.31 to a high of 0.96 across different studies[11][11]. These enormous variations suggested that reliability was not a fixed property of cloze tests but rather depended heavily on how they were constructed and scored. Similarly, while validity correlations with other language measures were often moderate to high, the variability across studies was problematic: some research found strong associations between cloze test scores and oral proficiency, while other research found these associations weak or absent[11][11].

More troubling for the pragmatic integration thesis were findings suggesting that cloze tests primarily measured lexical and grammatical knowledge at relatively local, sentence-level contexts rather than the broad pragmatic competence Oller had theorized[11]. A particularly influential study by Bachman (1985) established that when deletion rates changed substantially—say, from every fifth word to every tenth word—cloze test scores and their correlations with external criteria changed dramatically as well[11]. If the cloze test genuinely measured some stable underlying pragmatic competence, Bachman argued, it should not be so sensitive to methodological parameters like deletion frequency. Instead, the sensitivity to deletion frequency suggested that cloze tests were measuring something more narrowly dependent on the immediate textual context, likely lexical-semantic and morphosyntactic form rather than broad discourse comprehension[11]. Research by Saito (2003) applying structural equation modeling to the Examination for the Certificate of Proficiency in English (ECPE) found that "the cloze section of the ECPE measures the form and meaning of grammar" at "sentential and suprasentential levels rather than overall language proficiency"[11]. The pragmatic integration thesis, once promising, appeared to overstate what cloze procedures could accomplish.

The scoring methodology debate illuminated further construct validity questions[24][30][11]. Brown (1980) identified four prominent scoring approaches: exact-word matching, acceptable answers, Clozentropy (which weighted acceptable answers based on native speaker norming), and multiple-choice formats[24]. Research comparing these approaches found high correlations among them—Laing's study reported 0.92 correlations between exact and acceptable scoring for full test scores[30]—suggesting they measured essentially the same underlying constructs. Yet this raised an uncomfortable question: if accepting synonyms produced essentially identical results to requiring exact-word matches, why did the original Cloze passages contain particular words at all? The high correlation between exact and acceptable scoring suggested readers might be relying primarily on part-of-speech information and broad semantic appropriateness rather than the specific lexical content Taylor had emphasized[30]. This realization prompted scholars to question whether cloze procedures were primarily assessing grammatical pattern recognition and semantic category matching rather than the fine-grained comprehension of authored meaning that readability assessment nominally aimed to evaluate[11][11].

Furthermore, research increasingly documented affective and contextual variables affecting cloze performance that had little to do with reading comprehension per se. Test anxiety, time pressure, English language proficiency (for non-native speakers), and prior knowledge of text topics all influenced cloze test scores in ways that confounded interpretation[11]. A reader might comprehend a passage perfectly well but produce few exact-word matches on a cloze test due to time constraints, unfamiliarity with particular synonymous vocabulary items, or performance anxiety. These validity threats suggested that cloze tests measured not purely reading comprehension but rather a complex combination of comprehension ability, vocabulary breadth, processing speed, and affective state under test conditions.

Psycholinguistic Investigation: Eye-Tracking, Predictability Norms, and Brain Responses

Parallel to the educational application and validity debates, psycholinguists increasingly employed cloze procedures to investigate fundamental mechanisms of language comprehension. The cloze task proved exceptionally valuable for psycholinguistic research because it provided quantitative estimates of word predictability—what psychologists termed "cloze probability," the proportion of comprehenders who completed a sentence fragment by producing a particular target word[6][6]. By collecting cloze data from groups of participants, researchers could quantify how predictable or surprising specific words were in context, then use these predictability estimates as independent variables predicting eye-movement patterns, reading times, and event-related potential (ERP) components in actual reading[6][6][15][49].

Cloze probability norms proved remarkably powerful for predicting reading behavior. Numerous studies documented that words with high cloze probability—those that many participants predicted—were read faster than words with low cloze probability[6][6]. This predictability effect appeared robustly in self-paced reading studies where participants controlled reading pace by pressing a button to reveal words sequentially, and in eye-tracking studies where participants read naturally while eye position was monitored[6][6]. Furthermore, cloze probability predicted neural responses to words: the N400 component, a negative-going event-related potential peaking around 400 milliseconds after word onset, was smaller for high-predictability words than for low-predictability words, reflecting easier semantic integration of predictable words into discourse meaning[6][39][42]. This psychoneural finding provided independent validation that cloze probability tapped into genuine predictive mechanisms operating during comprehension[6][39][42].

However, research progressively revealed important dissociations between cloze probability and other measures of word predictability derived from computational language models and corpus statistics[17][15]. Smith and Levy (2011) demonstrated that cloze probabilities systematically differed from normative corpus statistics, with human participants showing consistent biases—they underestimated low-probability words' likelihood and overestimated the likelihood of semantically appropriate words even when corpus data indicated they were less probable[17]. These systematic deviations suggested that human comprehenders did not simply compute probabilistic expectations matching corpus statistics; instead, they employed heuristic strategies that prioritized semantic plausibility and contextual fit over precise statistical correspondence[17][49].

More recently, large language models (LLMs) have provided new windows into the comparison between human cloze performance and computational word prediction. Research by Holtzman and colleagues (2024) found that Large Language Models like LLaMA, when used to generate word predictability estimates, provided better fit to eye-movement data than traditional cloze probability estimates[15]. When researchers simulated reading using the OB1-reader model of eye movement control—providing it with either cloze-derived or LLM-derived predictability estimates—models using LLM predictions achieved lower root mean square error (RMSE) than models using cloze estimates[15]. Furthermore, different language models (GPT-2, LLaMA) produced different predictability estimates, with more sophisticated language models (LLaMA) generally providing better fits to observed eye-movement measures[15]. These findings suggested that while human cloze performance and machine language models both captured important aspects of word predictability, they did not capture identical aspects: language models appeared to capture aspects of linguistic context that human readers might not consciously process or that influenced reading times through different mechanisms than conscious word prediction[15].

Recent research has also clarified the relationship between word predictability and surprisal theory—the influential hypothesis that processing difficulty is determined by surprisal (negative log probability) of incoming words[49]. Large-scale analysis of six reading datasets by Hahn and colleagues (2023) employing advanced nonlinear regression methods found evidence supporting logarithmic rather than linear relationships between predictability and processing difficulty[49]. This logarithmic effect is theoretically significant because it aligns with probabilistic inference accounts of comprehension: according to such accounts, readers update probability distributions over possible interpretations as words arrive, and processing difficulty reflects the information-theoretic cost (KL divergence) of such updates[49]. The logarithmic relationship means that the processing cost of a word is proportional to surprisal—that is, proportional to how much the word changes the reader's probability distribution over interpretations—rather than varying linearly with surprise[49]. This theoretical development positioned cloze-derived predictability estimates as one window (albeit an imperfect one) into the information-theoretic principles governing language comprehension.

The Computational Turn: Masked Language Modeling as Algorithmic Cloze

The emergence of deep learning in natural language processing created an unexpected convergence between cloze procedure methodology and cutting-edge machine learning approaches. In 2018, Google researchers introduced BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers), a transformer-based language model trained using masked language modeling (MLM)[18][38][41]. The MLM training objective was, fundamentally, a computational implementation of the cloze procedure: the model randomly masked approximately fifteen percent of tokens in input text sequences, then trained bidirectional transformers to predict the masked tokens based on surrounding context[14][18][38]. During training, BERT learned to generate probability distributions over the entire vocabulary for each masked position, thus learning to estimate what words were likely to follow or precede given contexts[18][38].

This computational cloze procedure proved extraordinarily effective for pretraining language models. By training on massive unlabeled text corpora—BERT was trained on the entire English Wikipedia plus the BooksCorpus dataset—transformer models learned robust bidirectional representations of language context[14][18]. The self-attention mechanism in transformers allowed the model to weight differently the relevance of different context words when predicting masked tokens, thus capturing complex long-range dependencies and semantic relationships that simpler statistical models could not[14][18][38]. After pretraining with MLM objectives, these models could be fine-tuned for diverse downstream tasks—text classification, named entity recognition, question answering—and achieved state-of-the-art performance[18][38]. The success of masked language modeling demonstrated empirically what Oller and Taylor had theorized: that predicting words from context engaged fundamental linguistic competencies that transferred to diverse language understanding tasks[14][18][38].

Subsequent developments in transformer-based language models refined MLM approaches while maintaining the core cloze-like training objective. RoBERTa (Liu et al., 2019) trained on substantially more data than BERT—160 GB of text versus BERT's approximately 100 GB—and found that further scaling of MLM training improved performance[14]. DeBERTa introduced improved transformer architectures that further enhanced context understanding[14]. XLM-RoBERTa extended MLM pretraining to cross-lingual contexts, training on Wikipedia in multiple languages and Common Crawl multilingual data[14]. Across all these variants, the fundamental training approach remained constant: mask words and train models to predict them, a procedure directly analogous to human cloze task performance[14][22]. The convergence between psycholinguistic cloze procedures and computational masked language modeling suggested deep structural parallels between human and machine approaches to language comprehension.

However, important differences also emerged between how humans and machines approached cloze-like tasks[14][18]. Human readers, when completing sentences, generated single word continuations based on intuitive expectations[6][6]. Language models, by contrast, generated probability distributions over all possible continuations, thus quantifying multiple plausible completions and their relative likelihood[18]. Human cloze performance reflected offline conscious word production, potentially involving strategic processes and deliberate consideration[17]. Language model training occurred through iterative error correction across millions of examples, optimizing model weights to minimize the cross-entropy loss between predicted and actual word identities[18]. Yet despite these mechanistic differences, the end result was similar: both approaches learned to estimate contextual word probability, to recognize linguistic patterns, to extract semantic relationships, and to integrate information across extended contexts[14][18]. The fact that transformer language models trained on MLM achieved exceptional performance on language understanding tasks provided striking validation for the core theoretical premise underlying cloze procedures: that predicting words from context engages the fundamental mechanisms of language comprehension.

Modern Implementations: Standardized Tests, Automatic Generation, and Adaptive Assessment

Contemporary assessment practices continue to employ cloze procedure variants while simultaneously developing increasingly sophisticated computational approaches to automatic test generation. The Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL) maintained cloze-like items in its reading comprehension section, recognizing that these items effectively assessed vocabulary knowledge and contextual understanding[45]. The Examination for the Certificate of Proficiency in English (ECPE) incorporated a dedicated cloze section, despite ongoing questions about what it measured, because cloze items demonstrated reasonable psychometric properties when evaluated with appropriate statistical techniques[11]. Multiple-choice variants of cloze procedures proliferated in educational assessment because they simplified large-scale scoring: administrators could use optical scanning or digital systems to record responses, whereas free-response cloze tests required manual scoring subject to human error and interpretation[32].

The advent of transformer-based language models created new possibilities for automatically generating cloze test items with controlled difficulty levels. Rather than requiring human test designers to manually select words to delete and develop plausible distractors, researchers developed systems that could analyze passages, identify candidates for deletion based on computational metrics, and generate sets of plausible incorrect options (distractors) using language model outputs[20][37][20]. In 2022, Chiang and colleagues presented CDGP (Cloze Distractor Generation based on Pre-trained Language Models), which used RoBERTa's conditional probability estimates to rank potential distractor words according to how plausible they would be at masked positions[37]. More recent work by Shen and colleagues (2024) developed the Personalized Cloze Test Generation (PCGL) Framework, which utilizes large language models to generate cloze test items tailored to individual learners' proficiency levels, enabling adaptive assessment that dynamically adjusts difficulty to match each student's abilities[21].

Beyond distractor generation, computational approaches advanced cloze test construction itself. Holtzman and colleagues (2024) created the nCloze method, which formalized pedagogically motivated objectives—plausibility, incorrectness, and distinctiveness—as conditional probability distributions from masked language models, then used optimization algorithms (simulated annealing) to jointly generate sets of distractors maximizing these objectives[20][20]. Critically, they validated their automatically generated tests with human participants, demonstrating that nCloze-generated tests had stronger correlation with teacher-created comprehension tests than state-of-the-art neural methods and greater internal consistency[20][20]. This validation work was essential: it demonstrated that computational cloze generation was not merely an engineering convenience but could produce pedagogically sound assessment instruments comparable to or better than traditional human-designed tests[20][20].

These modern developments represented a full-circle return to information-theoretic foundations. Entropy-based approaches directly addressed Taylor's intuition that gap complexity should be quantifiable: by computing entropy of conditional probability distributions at masked positions, researchers could predict which gaps would prove difficult for learners[4]. Machine learning approaches to distractor generation leveraged the same information-theoretic principles that made BERT's masked language modeling effective: by sampling words from probability distributions conditioned on context, systems could generate plausible-but-incorrect options that felt semantically reasonable but did not match the original author's intended expression[20][37][20]. The computational implementations thus vindicated Taylor's 1953 insight: that word prediction from context could be formalized quantitatively, and that systematic analysis of gap-filling behavior could reveal much about both human language comprehension and the structure of language itself.

Theoretical Convergence: Self-Supervised Learning and the Cloze Task Paradigm

The resurgence of interest in cloze-like procedures through their computational implementation in masked language modeling revealed fundamental theoretical connections to self-supervised learning paradigms that had catalyzed the deep learning revolution[50]. Self-supervised learning, as distinguished from supervised learning (which requires labeled training pairs) and unsupervised learning (which discovers structure without explicit targets), obtains supervisory signals from the data itself, leveraging the underlying structure present in raw, unlabeled information[50]. The cloze task, whether administered to human readers or to computational systems, exemplified this principle perfectly: rather than requiring external annotations indicating correct word choices, the cloze procedure leveraged the inherent linguistic structure of text itself[50].

Taylor's original cloze procedure, administered to humans, was fundamentally a form of self-supervised assessment: rather than having test designers pre-specify what readers should know (as in traditional vocabulary tests or grammar tests), the procedure allowed readers' own predictions to provide the supervisory signal[1]. When a reader produced a cloze response, they demonstrated what words they expected to follow given contexts; their success rate at recovering the original author's choices quantified how closely their probabilistic language model aligned with the author's choices[1]. In this sense, Taylor had developed a self-supervised assessment paradigm decades before computer scientists formalized self-supervised learning as a machine learning approach[50].

The parallel development of self-supervised learning in machine learning and the cloze procedure in educational assessment reflects deep isomorphisms in how language is structured and how intelligent agents—human or artificial—learn to comprehend language patterns[22][50]. Both approaches recognize that language is inherently sequential and contextual: understanding each word depends on what preceded it[50]. Both approaches exploit the observation that future linguistic material is substantially predictable from past material, given the probabilistic regularities embedded in language use[22][50]. Both approaches avoid the expense and potential arbitrariness of manual labeling by automatically generating training targets from the data itself[50]. When Devlin and colleagues (2018) developed BERT using masked language modeling, they were, in effect, computationally implementing the cloze procedure that Taylor had introduced for human assessment sixty-five years earlier[1][18][22].

This theoretical convergence suggests that cloze procedures should not be viewed as merely historical artifacts in the assessment field, valuable primarily for their practical utility in measuring reading comprehension. Rather, the cloze procedure represents an implementation of a fundamental principle applicable across cognitive science and artificial intelligence: that predicting linguistic continuations from contextual constraints engages core language comprehension mechanisms[22][50]. The remarkable success of masked language modeling in pretraining transformer models that achieved state-of-the-art performance across diverse language understanding tasks provided empirical validation of what Taylor had theorized: that predicting words from context captures something essential about language comprehension[1][18][20][20]. Contemporary work combining cognitive neuroscience (via eye-tracking and EEG studies of cloze performance), educational assessment (via validation of automatically generated cloze tests), and computational linguistics (via masked language modeling) demonstrates that the cloze procedure remains theoretically vital even as its specific implementations evolve.

Persistent Theoretical Challenges and Ongoing Debates

Despite seventy years of research and the apparent vindication of cloze procedures' theoretical foundations in computational language modeling, fundamental questions about what cloze tests measure remain incompletely resolved[11][11]. The exact-word versus acceptable-answer scoring debate never generated a definitive resolution, though evidence of high correlations between scoring methods suggested they captured largely similar constructs[30]. Yet the existence of such high correlations itself raises questions: if accepting synonyms produces nearly identical results to requiring exact-word matches, are cloze tests primarily assessing lexical choice precision or broader semantic-syntactic appropriateness? The answer likely involves recognizing that reading comprehension itself involves multiple levels of precision—sometimes readers need to understand the specific author's intended meaning (favoring exact-word scoring), while other times they need to extract essential semantic content (favoring acceptable-answer scoring)[11][24].

The relationship between cloze performance and broader reading comprehension remains complex. While cloze tests often correlate with other reading comprehension measures, the correlation is not perfect, and cloze tests may measure something more narrowly focused on local syntactic-semantic constraints than on global discourse comprehension, inference generation, or critical evaluation of meaning[11][24][11].
