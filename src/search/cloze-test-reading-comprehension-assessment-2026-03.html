The Cloze Test in Reading Comprehension Assessment: From Taylor's Innovation to Modern Machine Learning Applications

The cloze test represents one of the most enduring and versatile assessment instruments in educational research and language evaluation, spanning over seven decades from its introduction by Wilson L. Taylor in 1953 to its contemporary applications in artificial intelligence and personalized digital learning systems1,2. This assessment method, which systematically removes words from text passages and requires test-takers to supply missing elements based on contextual understanding, has evolved from a simple readability measurement tool into a sophisticated psycholinguistic instrument that illuminates fundamental processes underlying human language comprehension2,20. The procedure's trajectory reveals not merely changing assessment fashions but rather fundamental insights into how humans process written language, predict upcoming information, and construct meaning from incomplete patterns. From pioneering work examining the redundancy of English language by Claude Shannon through contemporary machine learning applications in models like BERT, the cloze test has remained central to understanding both human cognition and artificial intelligence. This comprehensive examination traces the theoretical foundations, methodological debates, empirical evidence, and modern instantiations of cloze methodology, demonstrating how an innovative 1950s communication research tool has become foundational to twenty-first-century natural language processing and adaptive educational assessment.

Historical Origins and Wilson L. Taylor's Foundational Work

Wilson L. Taylor's groundbreaking 1953 paper fundamentally transformed literacy assessment research by introducing the cloze procedure as a new psychological tool for measuring the effectiveness of communication23. Taylor's innovation emerged from communication theory and psychological research on information processing, hypothesizing that a reader's ability to fill in systematically deleted words from a text would reveal their comprehension and linguistic competence20. Before Taylor's work, reading specialists and educators relied primarily on readability formulas such as the Flesch Reading Ease Formula and the Dale-Chall Readability Score, which calculated text difficulty based on word length and sentence length statistics23. However, Taylor recognized a critical limitation in these mechanical formulas: they assessed text complexity based on linguistic features of the text itself rather than measuring actual reader comprehension23,11. His insight was revolutionary—by asking readers to reconstruct deleted words from context, he could measure not just the readability of text according to structural properties, but the genuine difficulty that a particular audience experienced in understanding the material11,22.

The term "cloze" itself derives from the Gestalt psychology concept of "closure," which describes the human tendency to complete familiar but incomplete patterns1,2,20. Taylor specifically referenced this psychological principle, proposing that just as Gestalt psychology demonstrates humans see a broken circle as a whole one by mentally closing the gap, language users similarly employ cognitive mechanisms to complete fragmented linguistic patterns23. This theoretical grounding connected cloze assessment to fundamental principles of human perceptual organization, elevating the procedure beyond a mere test administration technique to a window into core cognitive processes20. Taylor's original 1953 studies compared cloze test results with readability formulas on multiple text passages, demonstrating correlations between cloze procedure outcomes and established readability measures23. His work established that cloze procedure provided reliable information about text difficulty while offering several practical advantages over existing formulas—it required no complex calculations, could be applied to any text regardless of content area, and seemed to directly measure what actually interested educators: whether students could comprehend the material.

Theoretical Foundations: Gestalt Psychology, Psycholinguistics, and Language Processing

The psycholinguistic foundations underlying the cloze procedure extend beyond simple Gestalt psychology principles to encompass complex theories about how humans process written language and construct meaning from context19. In psycholinguistic models of reading comprehension, skilled readers engage in a sophisticated four-step process: sampling words and phrases selectively from text, predicting other words and phrases that might follow based on accumulated context, testing predictions against actual text, and confirming or correcting predictions as they encounter subsequent information19. The cloze procedure essentially crystallizes this natural reading process by forcing readers to make the predictions and confirmations that normally occur unconsciously during fluent reading into explicit, measurable responses19,20. Rather than allowing readers to process text passively, the blanks in a cloze passage demand active reconstruction of meaning using three fundamental cue systems that all proficient readers employ: graphic cues derived from letters and words, syntactic cues reflecting grammatical arrangements, and semantic cues drawn from word meanings and reader knowledge19.

The relationship between cloze testing and information theory constitutes another crucial theoretical dimension, particularly through the work of Claude Shannon, who examined the redundancy and predictability inherent in English language structure10,35. Shannon's landmark 1951 paper "Prediction and Entropy of Printed English" demonstrated that English language contains substantial statistical redundancy, estimating that approximately 50 to 75 percent of the information in written English is redundant when accounting for various levels of contextual constraint10,35. This redundancy stems from the high frequency of certain letters, strong tendency of particular letter combinations to follow one another, rules of grammar, parts of speech patterns, and vocabulary conventions that make English more predictable than a random symbol system10,35. Shannon employed an ingenious experimental method asking subjects to guess letters in phrases one by one, finding that 69 percent of letters in a phrase were correctly predicted, suggesting approximately 69 percent redundancy in English10,35. The implication for cloze testing is profound: because language contains extensive redundancy and constraint from context, skilled language users can often reconstruct deleted words with reasonable accuracy based on surrounding linguistic patterns20. This information-theoretic perspective explains why cloze tests work as reading comprehension measures—the very redundancy that Shannon quantified enables readers with adequate linguistic knowledge to predict missing words.

The psycholinguistic conceptualization of cloze procedure's validity rests additionally on what Oller termed "pragmatic expectancy grammar"—the proposition that language users employ systematic expectations about language constraints when completing cloze items, just as they do during normal language comprehension in any context25,25. According to this theory, reduced redundancy resulting from systematic word deletion forces test-takers to rely upon their knowledge of underlying linguistic rules and to maintain coherence of the passage to fill in blanks25,25. Constraints imposed by the internalized rule system allow test-takers to make predictions about passage content, and their success in generating contextually appropriate words theoretically reflects their mastery of these underlying linguistic regularities25,25. This theorization suggested that cloze scores might serve as indicators of a general language proficiency factor applicable to various testing purposes25,25. However, subsequent research questioned whether cloze tests genuinely measure this global proficiency or instead measure more specific linguistic components, a debate that continues to shape methodological discussions about test validity and construct measurement.

Methodological Debates: Deletion Procedures, Scoring Methods, and Passage Selection

The technical implementation of cloze procedures has generated sustained scholarly debate regarding which design choices produce valid and reliable assessments4,5,8,21,21,21. One foundational distinction differentiates random deletion methods from rational deletion approaches1,5. In random or fixed-ratio deletion, words are removed systematically according to mechanical rules—for example, every fifth, sixth, or seventh word is deleted regardless of word type or linguistic function1,5,23. Taylor originally employed mechanical deletion methods and specifically distinguished this random approach from pre-selected, targeted word deletion1,23. The systematic deletion method offers theoretical advantages: it reduces test constructor bias, ensures representative sampling of text elements, and produces tests that supposedly measure comprehensive language processing rather than specific linguistic features5. However, random deletion has faced criticism from researchers who argue that deleting arbitrary words means some deletions require only local, sentence-level context while others demand understanding of broader discourse connections5,8,24.

Rational deletion, by contrast, deliberately targets specific word types or positions for deletion, allowing test constructors to focus assessment on particular linguistic constructs5,8,21. Researchers including Bachman (1982, 1985) pioneered rational deletion approaches, classifying missing words according to the type of information presumably required to replace them—for example, distinguishing between clause-level, sentence-level, intersentential, text-level, and beyond-text information requirements5. This targeted approach theoretically permits assessment of specific comprehension components: does the reader understand local grammatical structure, or can they maintain discourse-level coherence across broader passages?5 Item analysis comparing difficulty of different word categories revealed that some types of words proved substantially more difficult to replace than others, providing empirical grounding for rational deletion approaches5. However, disagreement persists about whether rational deletion approaches constitute genuine variants of cloze methodology or instead represent different assessment procedures1,5. Some researchers, including Alderson, argue that gap-filling procedures should be separated from cloze procedure, while others, like Bachman, view rational deletion as simply another type of cloze procedure1,5.

The deletion rate—the proportion of words removed from text—represents another critical methodological variable with substantial impacts on test difficulty and validity3,5,21. Standard practice typically deletes every fifth through seventh word, creating passages with approximately 15-20 percent of text content missing2,11,11. However, research examining different deletion rates demonstrates that varying this parameter significantly affects test properties3,21. Higher deletion rates (removing more words, such as every fourth word) produce more difficult tests, while lower deletion rates (such as every tenth word) produce easier tests2,11. The deletion rate also interacts with passage difficulty—when combined with already challenging text, higher deletion rates can produce ceiling effects where even strong readers fail comprehension3. Beyond mere difficulty effects, deletion rate appears to influence the cognitive processes that the test measures5. Some evidence suggests that different deletion rates may preferentially tap different comprehension abilities, with higher deletion rates potentially requiring more extensive context use5. This sensitivity of cloze tests to deletion rate raises questions about whether the procedure genuinely measures stable, generalizable reading comprehension or instead measures a construct that is heavily method-dependent5.

Scoring methodology constitutes a third major area of methodological variation that substantially affects the interpretation of cloze results4,30,36. The two primary scoring approaches are exact-word scoring, where only the original deleted word receives credit as correct, and acceptable-answer scoring, where semantically and grammatically appropriate alternatives are accepted as correct responses4. These methods reflect different theoretical assumptions about what constitutes reading comprehension: exact-word scoring presumes the reader must accurately predict or recall the specific word choices made by the original author, while acceptable-answer scoring assumes comprehension is demonstrated by producing any contextually appropriate word, regardless of whether it matches the original4. A comprehensive study comparing these methods found extraordinarily high correlations between exact and acceptable scoring (.92 for whole test scores, .83-.87 for individual 50-item cloze tests), suggesting that both methods yield highly similar information4. This evidence indicates that exact-word scoring, while theoretically more restrictive, produces essentially equivalent results to acceptable-answer scoring for most practical purposes, and the choice between scoring methods may have minimal impact on overall test validity4. However, the acceptable-answer method requires substantially more subjective judgment about whether alternative responses genuinely constitute appropriate completions, introducing scoring reliability concerns4,11.

Application in First Language Literacy Assessment and Research

The adoption of cloze procedures within first language (L1) reading assessment and research has followed a trajectory from skepticism to broader acceptance, though with persistent questions about specific applications and interpretation22,39,46. Early applications of cloze methodology to L1 reading comprehension assessment demonstrated moderate to strong correlations with standardized reading tests and specific comprehension measures5,39. Early studies reported correlations between cloze scores and reading achievement measures ranging from 0.54 to 0.95, with most falling in the moderate to strong range of 0.80-0.95 for well-designed tests with native speakers5,39. These promising correlations led some researchers to recommend cloze as an efficient, practical alternative to more time-consuming comprehension tests39. However, reanalysis of influential concurrent validity studies revealed that the strength of correlations between cloze and reading comprehension tests varied substantially depending on what reading comprehension entailed39. Specifically, cloze tests predicted performance best when comprehension assessment focused on within-sentence information, but demonstrated weaker predictive validity when tests required readers to integrate information across sentence boundaries and understand broader discourse organization39. This pattern suggested that cloze procedure, while measuring certain aspects of reading comprehension, might be relatively insensitive to integrating information across larger textual units—a construct validity limitation that affected confidence in using cloze as a comprehensive measure of reading comprehension39.

The pedagogical application of cloze procedures in L1 classrooms has emphasized the procedure's value for developing essential reading strategies rather than merely assessing existing comprehension19,46. When used for instruction rather than assessment, the cloze procedure can help readers develop scanning and search reading skills often neglected in traditional reading instruction, assist in predicting likely upcoming content, and provide systematic practice in guessing meaning from context, which represents an important real-world reading strategy46. Teachers employing cloze for instructional purposes create what are termed "aural," "aural-visual," and "conventional visual" cloze presentations, with the specific mode depending on whether audio components and text visibility accompany the activity46. Discussions following cloze completion prove crucial to pedagogical effectiveness—students explain reasons for their word choices, the teacher provides contextual analysis, and systematic instruction occurs on recognizing context cues at multiple locations within text46. When carefully sequenced with meticulously selected texts and appropriate teacher guidance, the cloze procedure can effectively develop reading strategies and comprehension abilities, though undifferentiated use of cloze hoping for generalized reading improvement without this carefully designed implementation represents what researchers term a "shotgun approach" unlikely to produce meaningful learning gains46.

Applications in Second Language Literacy Assessment and ESL/EFL Testing

The adoption of cloze procedures in second language (L2) literacy assessment achieved even more widespread enthusiasm than in L1 contexts, with cloze becoming a standard component of many prominent standardized English proficiency tests3,3,25,25. The appeal of cloze for L2 assessment reflected its efficiency in administration, its apparent measurement of integrative language ability combining multiple linguistic components, and research evidence suggesting cloze could serve as a general indicator of overall ESL/EFL proficiency3,25,25. Studies demonstrated that cloze tests correlated not only with reading comprehension tests but also with listening comprehension, dictation, grammar and structure tests, and even oral proficiency interviews, suggesting cloze might measure a fundamental language processing ability underlying multiple specific skills5,25. This multi-faceted validity evidence, though correlational rather than definitive, convinced many testing programs that cloze represented an efficient way to assess overall language proficiency in timed testing contexts25,25.

However, more sophisticated construct validity research examining what cloze tests actually measure in L2 contexts revealed that the procedure may not genuinely assess the global language proficiency that theory suggested3,25,25. A structural equation modeling investigation of the cloze section in the Examination for the Certificate of Proficiency in English determined that cloze items measured two separate but related components of grammatical knowledge—grammatical form and grammatical meaning—at sentential and suprasentential levels, rather than assessing overall language proficiency25,25. The analysis found that the majority of cloze items measured lexical meaning, with a smaller proportion measuring morphosyntactic form, and specific word types differentially assessed specific constructs25,25. Modals and logical connectors appeared to measure both grammatical form and meaning, content words measured primarily lexical meaning, and function words measured grammatical form25,25. These findings suggested that what makes cloze tests difficult for L2 learners may be as much about lexical knowledge and specific word associations as about broader reading comprehension or grammatical understanding25,25. Recent research examining cloze test performance among L2 learners at different proficiency levels found that cloze tests were "most informative for high-level learners and least informative for intermediate-level learners," suggesting the procedure may be less appropriate for assessing intermediate proficiency stages where performance might be constrained more by test format demands than by actual comprehension ability3,3.

The specific linguistic and cognitive demands of cloze testing have become better understood through research on how different learner populations approach the task2,31. Research using eye-tracking technology revealed that lower-ability test takers concentrate more on information contained in words immediately surrounding gaps, while higher-ability test takers employ wider context windows, enabling better comprehension of discourse-level relationships2. This pattern suggests that the cognitive processes engaged by cloze may vary substantially across proficiency levels, with less-skilled readers performing a different cognitive task than more-skilled readers on identical cloze items2. Furthermore, research on reading comprehension tests generally found that cloze items produce variance beyond and above general reading comprehension factors, suggesting a cloze-specific method factor that may relate to closure speed, working memory demands, or specific linguistic processing patterns rather than pure comprehension ability31. One bifactor analysis model found that including a cloze-specific dimension substantially improved model fit compared to unidimensional models, indicating that cloze items share something unique beyond core reading comprehension31. For older test-takers, cognitive variables like working memory showed differential effects on cloze performance compared to younger individuals, raising concerns that cloze test performance may reflect age-related cognitive changes rather than pure reading comprehension or literacy ability50. These construct validity complications suggest that while cloze tests provide useful information about reading ability and language proficiency, they measure specific constructs influenced by method-specific factors rather than pure global comprehension ability.

Standardized Testing Implementation and Contemporary Assessment Practices

The incorporation of cloze procedures into major standardized language proficiency tests reflects both the procedure's practical advantages and the theoretical rationales that have motivated its adoption despite methodological controversies25,25,25. The Test of English as a Foreign Language (TOEFL) includes a cloze section designed to assess vocabulary in context and comprehension of written English passages25. The Examination for the Certificate of Proficiency in English (ECPE) includes a substantial cloze component meant to assess integrated language knowledge25,25. The Michigan Assessment of Graduate English has similarly incorporated cloze methodology, and numerous other ESL/EFL proficiency examinations include cloze sections as efficient ways to assess language ability in large-scale testing contexts25. Multiple-choice cloze variants have become particularly prevalent in standardized testing because they address several limitations of open-ended cloze items: multiple-choice formats eliminate subjective scoring decisions about acceptable alternatives, standardize the complexity of distractors across items, and provide more uniform administration conditions2,11,17. Comparative research on multiple-choice cloze tests versus C-tests (a variant providing fewer letter clues) found that multiple-choice cloze produced better measurement of reading comprehension, suggesting the multiple-choice format provides beneficial structure for assessment36.

The practical efficiency that cloze methodology offers in standardized testing contexts—rapid administration, straightforward scoring (especially with multiple-choice formats), and generally acceptable reliability—has sustained its adoption despite ongoing theoretical questions about construct validity11. Efficiency considerations have become even more salient in modern digital testing environments where automated scoring and adaptive item selection are possible15,15,32. Several online platforms now enable rapid generation of cloze tests using web-based tools, with some incorporating artificial intelligence to generate test items and distractors automatically15,15. These platforms typically collect data on participant responses in real-time, enabling educators to identify comprehension difficulties and track progress across assessments15,15. The emergence of personalized cloze test generation frameworks utilizing large language models represents a contemporary innovation addressing longstanding criticism about test appropriateness across diverse learner populations32,32. These adaptive systems generate cloze items tailored to individual proficiency levels, dynamically adjusting difficulty and question characteristics to match learner abilities32,32. Such personalized approaches represent evolution of standardized cloze testing toward assessment instruments that adapt to individual learners rather than subjecting all test-takers to identical items regardless of prior performance32,32.

The Cloze Procedure and Information Theory: Connecting Psychology and Mathematics

The connection between cloze testing and information theory reveals a profound relationship between psychological assessment methodology and mathematical models of language structure, with implications for understanding both human cognition and artificial intelligence10,35,26,29. Shannon's work on English redundancy, conducted contemporaneously with Taylor's introduction of cloze procedure but through entirely different methodological approaches, provides a mathematical foundation for understanding why cloze tests work as comprehension measures10,35. Shannon calculated the entropy of English language—the average amount of information produced per letter or word—and estimated redundancy at approximately 50-75 percent, meaning that only 25-50 percent of information in English text is genuinely novel information not predictable from context10,35. This mathematical redundancy directly explains why readers can reconstruct deleted words: the missing words are typically highly predictable from surrounding context, and skilled readers with adequate linguistic knowledge can employ contextual constraints to reconstruct what was removed20,26,29.

Recent studies employing large language models and computational approaches to predict word cloze completions have empirically validated this information-theoretic foundation while revealing subtle differences between human and computational predictability26,29,43. Comparative analysis of human cloze responses against modern language models including GPT-2, RoBERTa, and LLaMA found that while language models generally correlate with human predictability estimates, they systematically under-estimate human probabilities for common completions and over-rank rare completions, revealing that the semantic space of model predictions diverges from human language use26,29,43. Large-scale evaluation of cloze probability values, predictability ratings derived from human judgments, and computational estimates from language models demonstrated that human predictability ratings proved the best predictor of neural responses (N400, P600, LAN components) and reading time patterns when spillover effects were accounted for29. Computational estimates were particularly effective at explaining variance in eye-tracking data without requiring spillover adjustment, suggesting language models capture different aspects of predictability than human cloze responses29. The finding that different measurement approaches—human cloze probability, human predictability ratings, and computational estimates—each most effectively predicted different processing indices indicates that "the choice of the best measurement of word predictability in context critically depends on the processing index being considered"29.

These contemporary computational investigations reveal that while Shannon's mathematical framework accurately describes language redundancy at an aggregate level, human language processing involves nuanced probabilistic computations differing from both Shannon's early estimates and contemporary neural language models26,29. The integration of cloze testing with eye-tracking research has demonstrated that word predictability directly affects how readers allocate visual attention and cognitive resources during reading comprehension26,29,29. Words with higher cloze predictability (words more frequently predicted by human judges to complete sentence fragments) are skipped more often, examined with shorter fixation durations, and show reduced regression patterns, indicating that readers devote less attentional resources to predictable words26,29,29. This research provides psychological validation for information-theoretic predictions: the mathematical redundancy Shannon quantified translates into psychological processing advantages for predictable words26,29. The implications extend to understanding how human readers optimize information processing—by allocating minimal cognitive resources to predictable words, readers conserve processing capacity for unexpected or informative words, representing an efficient allocation strategy that makes evolutionary sense for comprehension and retention26,29.

Masked Language Modeling and BERT: Computational Implementation of Cloze Methodology

The connection between classical cloze procedure and contemporary artificial intelligence represents perhaps the most striking vindication of Taylor's original insight about the predictability inherent in language patterns12,12,37,40. BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers), one of the most influential deep learning models for natural language processing, implements cloze procedure as its core pre-training objective through what researchers term "masked language modeling"12,12,37,40. In BERT's training procedure, 15 percent of input tokens are randomly selected for the masking operation; 80 percent of those selected tokens (comprising 12 percent of total tokens) are replaced with a special [MASK] token representing a blank space; 10 percent (1.5 percent of total) are replaced with random tokens to mitigate pre-training to fine-tuning mismatch; and 10 percent remain unchanged12,12,40. The model then learns to predict the original token at each masked position using bidirectional context from both preceding and subsequent tokens, generating a probability distribution over the entire vocabulary for each masked position12,12,37,40.

This computational implementation of cloze procedure represents a scaled-up, mathematically sophisticated version of the task humans perform when completing traditional cloze passages12,12,37. Like human readers engaging with paper-based cloze tests, BERT must utilize contextual information to predict likely words at blank positions, learning representations that capture semantic and syntactic patterns enabling accurate prediction12,12,40. The bidirectional attention mechanism employed by BERT represents a significant advantage over traditional left-to-right language models—instead of predicting each word based only on prior context (as in sequential reading), BERT can simultaneously attend to prior and subsequent context, analogous to how humans solving paper-based cloze passages can re-read surrounding sentences12,12,37. Researchers have explicitly noted that "the word-level objective for BERT comes from psychology" and specifically references Taylor's 1953 cloze test as the theoretical foundation for masked language modeling40,12. This direct lineage connects 1950s reading comprehension assessment methodology to state-of-the-art machine learning approaches, demonstrating how psychological insights about human language processing translate into effective artificial learning objectives12,40.

The effectiveness of masked language modeling as a pre-training objective has been remarkably successful, with BERT-based models achieving state-of-the-art performance across numerous natural language processing tasks including question answering, textual entailment, sentiment analysis, and semantic similarity assessment12,12,37,40,49. Subsequent refinements including RoBERTa, XLNet, and other transformer-based models have built upon BERT's masked language modeling objective while addressing various limitations12,12. The ELECTRA model represents a particularly interesting variant that reframes cloze prediction as an energy-based modeling problem, deriving it directly from cloze task theory while achieving performance comparable to or exceeding BERT on various downstream tasks37. The fact that cloze-derived pre-training objectives remain at the core of state-of-the-art language models nearly 70 years after Taylor's original proposal suggests that cloze captures something fundamental about language structure and learning—a principle as effective for training artificial neural networks as for assessing human reading comprehension12,12,37,40.

The comparison between human and machine cloze task performance reveals intriguing similarities and differences in how neural networks and humans approach word prediction from context43. Large-scale evaluation comparing language model predictions to human cloze completions found that while language models perform reasonably well on highly constraining contexts where humans show strong consensus about the appropriate completion, model performance diverges from human predictions as contexts become more ambiguous and multiple plausible completions exist43. In highly predictable sentences producing only one or two unique human completions, neural language models recovered the top human response approximately 28 percent of the time; for rarer responses appearing in the top 10 human responses, models selected them at that rank or higher only 9 percent of the time43. This pattern suggests that language models may assign probability distributions across possible completions that differ substantially from human probability distributions, potentially reflecting different learning mechanisms or training data characteristics43. The computational investigation of cloze probability, predictability ratings, and language model estimates demonstrates that while classical psychology-based cloze task and contemporary machine learning approaches share the same fundamental task structure, they may capture complementary rather than identical aspects of language predictability29,43.

Digital Reading Assessment Tools and Personalized Learning Platforms

The evolution of digital technologies has fundamentally transformed how cloze procedures are implemented, administered, scored, and interpreted in contemporary educational contexts15,15,32,32,38. Digital platforms enable capabilities impossible with paper-based cloze testing: immediate automated scoring with feedback, dynamic item selection adapting to learner performance, multimedia presentations combining text with audio and visual elements, interactive formats including drag-and-drop and dropdown selections, and sophisticated data analytics tracking comprehension patterns over time15,15,32,38. Several dedicated cloze testing platforms now enable educators and researchers to rapidly create, deploy, and analyze cloze tests, substantially reducing the time and expertise required compared to traditional test development15,15. Online platforms like Clozetesting.com, Clozemaster, and various learning management system integrations provide user-friendly interfaces where educators describe exercises needed, templates generate initial items, instructors refine or customize items, and students complete assessments while data collection occurs automatically15,15,38.

The contemporary integration of artificial intelligence and large language models into cloze test generation represents a significant methodological advancement addressing longstanding pedagogical limitations32,32. Personalized cloze test generation frameworks utilize large language models to generate cloze questions tailored to individual learner proficiency levels, automatically creating both question stems and appropriate distractors from a single input word32,32. These systems assess individual learner proficiency through initial placement testing or prior performance history, then adjust difficulty by manipulating which words are targeted for deletion, varying the difficulty of distractors, and selecting source texts of appropriate complexity32,32. Compared to traditional standardized cloze testing presenting identical items to all learners regardless of prior achievement, personalized approaches reduce cognitive overload for struggling readers while providing appropriate challenge for advanced learners, theoretically supporting greater learning gains through improved task appropriateness32,32. The efficiency gains from automated test generation and personalized item selection represent substantial practical advantages, reducing teacher preparation time while improving assessment alignment with individual learning needs32,32.

Multimedia cloze implementations incorporating audio, visual, and video elements represent another contemporary advancement extending cloze methodology beyond traditional text-only formats15,15. Audio cloze variants present sentence fragments orally and require listeners to supply missing words, assessing listening comprehension while employing the same predictability-from-context principle as text-based cloze9,46. Visual cloze using diagrams, images, or graphic organizers applies cloze principles to non-linguistic materials, requiring learners to identify missing labels, chart elements, or map features based on visual context2. These multimodal extensions broaden the applicability of cloze methodology to diverse learning contexts and learner populations with varying linguistic or sensory abilities15,15,38. Online platforms increasingly integrate cloze testing with broader learning management systems and adaptive learning algorithms that track individual learner progress across multiple assessment modalities, providing educators with real-time data informing instructional decisions15,15,32,38.

Construct Validity, Method Bias, and Current Criticisms

Despite cloze procedure's widespread adoption and general acceptance as a useful assessment tool, persistent questions about construct validity and method-specific factors affecting scores have accumulated over decades of research31,39,50. A fundamental concern involves distinguishing between what cloze tests actually measure versus what educators and test developers hope they measure—genuine reading comprehension and language proficiency versus test-specific competencies influenced by format demands31,39,50. The extensive literature on this question reveals that cloze items do not form a unidimensional construct but rather contain substantial method-specific variance beyond general reading ability31,39. Bifactor analysis separating general reading factors from cloze-specific dimensions found that cloze items produce variance significantly beyond what general reading comprehension explains, suggesting the procedure measures something additional to pure comprehension31. The cloze-specific factor potentially reflects individual differences in "closure speed," "closure flexibility," or specific cognitive abilities like working memory that are not central to reading comprehension itself31.

Working memory demands represent another substantial but often underappreciated contributor to cloze test performance, particularly affecting specific populations50. Research examining cloze test performance among older adults found that working memory capacity significantly predicted performance on cloze items for older participants but not younger participants, suggesting age-related differential item functioning where items appear harder for older individuals even when they possess equivalent reading comprehension ability50. When problematic items showing differential item functioning were removed from test scores, age-group differences in apparent health literacy (assessed through the Test of Functional Health Literacy in Adults, which employs cloze procedure) disappeared entirely50. This finding implies that at least part of reported age differences in health literacy from cloze-based assessments may reflect age-related working memory decline rather than genuine health literacy deficits50. The differential impact of working memory across age groups raises broader questions about test fairness and appropriate interpretation of cloze scores for diverse populations with varying cognitive abilities and processing speeds50.

Criticisms specific to using cloze procedure for health literacy assessment and other specialized domains highlight additional concerns about the procedure's appropriateness across all contexts50. Researchers have argued against routine use of cloze in healthcare settings, noting that the procedure's cognitive demands may not align well with actual health communication comprehension, where patients typically have additional time, can ask questions, and benefit from visual aids and explanations—conditions entirely unlike timed cloze test completion50. The artificially constrained context of cloze procedure may create comprehension demands exceeding those of actual health communication comprehension50. Similarly, criticisms have emerged regarding using cloze with populations of low literacy, arguing that the demands of reconstructing words exceeds what low-literacy individuals face during actual reading, potentially producing artificially low assessments of their comprehension abilities50. These domain-specific concerns about cloze appropriateness reflect broader questions about whether the procedure truly represents authentic comprehension demands or instead creates test-specific challenges affecting score interpretation31,39,50.

Contemporary Developments and Future Directions in Cloze Research

Recent research directions in cloze methodology and its applications reflect both refinements to traditional implementations and novel applications emerging from technological change and theoretical advances16,26,32,29,43. Advanced psycholinguistic research employing eye-tracking technology has provided unprecedented detail about how different reader populations process cloze passages, revealing that reader ability levels, proficiency in target language, and familiarity with content domains all influence how extensively readers use context to solve cloze blanks2,26,29. This research has practical implications for test design—understanding that higher-ability readers employ broader context windows suggests that rational deletion targeting important information elements might more effectively differentiate reader ability than mechanical deletion5,8. Computational research comparing human cloze predictions to large language model predictions has revealed fascinating divergences in how neural networks and humans distribute probability across possible completions, with implications for understanding both cognitive processing and the biases inherent in large language models26,29,43. This research further supports recommendations that cloze norms derived from human populations may provide better estimates of psychological predictability than computational models in contexts where understanding human cognition represents the primary goal26,29,29.

The integration of cloze methodology into adaptive learning systems and personalized education platforms represents perhaps the most significant frontier in contemporary cloze applications32,32. As educational technology increasingly employs machine learning to optimize instruction for individual learners, cloze tests integrated into adaptive systems can provide continual assessment informing dynamic adjustment of subsequent instruction, difficulty level, and content focus32,32. The combination of personalized cloze test generation with adaptive algorithms creates potential for assessment systems that are simultaneously more precise in measuring individual abilities and more appropriately calibrated to individual proficiency levels32,32. Furthermore, the convergence of cloze testing with masked language modeling in natural language processing raises intriguing possibilities for deepening understanding of commonalities and differences between human and machine language learning16,26,43. As larger language models become more sophisticated, comparative research examining how human and computational cloze performance diverge and converge promises insights into the cognitive processes underlying language understanding26,29,43.

The future trajectory of cloze research will likely involve continuing refinement of construct validity evidence, investigations of cross-cultural and multilingual applications, and integration of cloze methodology into broader assessment ecosystems addressing diverse educational goals32,29,32. Growing attention to fairness in educational assessment will likely spur additional research on differential item functioning across demographic groups, appropriate populations for cloze application, and modifications addressing specific populations' particular needs31,50. The ongoing convergence of psychological assessment methodology and computational linguistics offers potential for mutual enrichment—psychological research on cloze testing may inform more cognitively realistic approaches to language model training, while language models may help researchers better understand human language predictability through detailed computational analysis of contextual constraints26,29,43. Given that cloze procedure has remained theoretically relevant and practically useful for over seven decades despite substantial technological and theoretical changes, the methodology seems likely to persist as an important assessment tool while potentially evolving in form and application1,20,23,11,40.

Conclusion: Integrating Theory, Measurement, and Application

The trajectory of cloze procedure from Wilson L. Taylor's 1953 innovation to contemporary implementation in artificial intelligence and personalized learning systems illustrates how a sound theoretical insight about language structure and human cognition can maintain relevance and effectiveness across generations of methodological refinement and technological change1,20,23,11,40. Taylor's foundational observation—that readers' ability to reconstruct deleted words from context provides valid evidence about comprehension—connected reading assessment to psychological principles of closure from Gestalt theory and ultimately proved compatible with information-theoretic models of language structure and contemporary machine learning approaches to language modeling1,10,20,35,37,40. The convergence of these diverse theoretical and methodological streams—psychological assessment, information theory, psycholinguistics, and artificial intelligence—demonstrates that cloze procedure captures something fundamental about how language operates: the extensive redundancy and constraint characterizing natural language enables both humans and machine learning systems to predict missing elements with reasonable accuracy, and this predictability reflects underlying linguistic structure10,20,26,29,35,37,40.

The methodological debates surrounding cloze procedure—concerning deletion rate, deletion method, scoring approaches, and passage selection—represent not flaws in the fundamental methodology but rather recognition that implementation choices substantially affect what gets measured and how results should be interpreted4,5,8,21,31,21,39. The emerging consensus suggests that cloze tests do not measure a single, unidimensional construct but rather multiple components including contextual comprehension, lexical knowledge, grammatical understanding, working memory, cognitive closure abilities, and potentially test-taking strategies specific to cloze format3,3,25,31,25. This multidimensionality does not render cloze tests invalid but rather requires more nuanced interpretation of scores and careful attention to context when deciding whether cloze represents the most appropriate assessment method for specific purposes31,39,50. Research demonstrating that different measurement approaches—human cloze probability, human predictability ratings, and computational estimates—differentially predict various cognitive and neural outcomes suggests that multiple complementary measures of contextual predictability provide richer information than any single approach alone29.

The practical applications of cloze procedure across diverse contexts—from classroom reading instruction to large-scale standardized proficiency testing to artificial intelligence training—attest to its remarkable versatility and robust effectiveness despite methodological complexities15,20,15,11,32,40. The emergence of digital platforms, artificial intelligence-driven test generation, and adaptive learning systems built around cloze assessment demonstrates that the methodology remains dynamic and continues evolving in response to technological capabilities and educational needs15,15,32,32. Yet the persistence of fundamental questions about construct validity, fairness across populations, and appropriate interpretation of scores indicates that future research must continue examining when cloze procedures prove most appropriate, for which populations they provide valid measurement, and how results should inform educational decisions31,39,50. The remarkable durability of Taylor's original insight—that human predictability of language patterns provides a window into comprehension—suggests cloze procedure will continue occupying an important place in reading assessment and language evaluation, even as specific implementations evolve and our theoretical understanding of reading comprehension becomes increasingly sophisticated. From psychology to information theory to artificial intelligence, the cloze procedure has proven a remarkably fertile methodology for generating insights about language and cognition, and its future in educational assessment and computational linguistics appears secure.
