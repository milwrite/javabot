The Rubin Museum of Himalayan Art: A Comprehensive Analysis of Its Evolution into a Global Institution and Contemporary Impact

The Rubin Museum of Himalayan Art represents one of the most significant institutional transformations in contemporary museum practice, having deliberately closed its physical location in New York City in October 2024 to become a globally distributed cultural institution without traditional walls.1 Founded in 2004 by philanthropists Donald and Shelley Rubin, the museum has evolved from a pioneering repository of Tibetan and Himalayan art into a decentralized platform that serves artists, scholars, and the general public across multiple continents through traveling exhibitions, digital platforms, institutional partnerships, and direct grant support.1,2,2 The museum's transition represents a bold reimagining of what museums can accomplish in the twenty-first century, moving beyond the conventional model of a singular physical space to instead deploy its nearly 4,000-object collection, institutional expertise, and curatorial vision through collaborations with universities, cultural organizations, and communities from Salt Lake City to Dharamsala.2,2 This comprehensive analysis examines the Rubin's origins, its strategic transformation to a global model, its extensive network of traveling exhibitions and international partnerships, its innovative educational programs and digital resources, its collection-sharing initiatives, its support for contemporary artists and scholars, and its current projects as it operates in its second full year as a museum without walls.

Historical Origins and Institutional Foundation

The Rubin Museum of Himalayan Art emerged from a remarkable personal collecting journey that began in the mid-1970s when Donald and Shelley Rubin encountered a thangka painting—a traditional Tibetan painting on cloth depicting White Tara, the Buddhist deity of longevity—displayed in an art gallery window.1 This initial purchase sparked what would become a three-decade-long passion for accumulating one of the most significant collections of Himalayan art outside the region itself, with the Rubins gradually recognizing their role as stewards of the material culture of people in diaspora and feeling compelled to preserve and share these works with a broader public. In 1998, the collectors made a decisive investment in their vision by purchasing a 70,000-square-foot building at 150 West 17th Street in the Chelsea neighborhood of Manhattan that had formerly housed Barneys New York, a designer fashion department store that had filed for bankruptcy, investing approximately $22 million for the property.1 The acquisition of this substantial Chelsea real estate marked a turning point, demonstrating the Rubins' commitment to creating a permanent institutional home for their collection and their determination to make Himalayan art accessible to the American public at a time when such art remained relatively unfamiliar to mainstream audiences.1

The building's renovation from 2000 to 2004 was orchestrated through a collaboration of preservation architects Beyer Blinder Belle, museum designers Atelier Imrey Culbert, and graphic designer Milton Glaser, who collectively created a space that honored the building's architectural history while embedding Himalayan aesthetic principles into its design.1 The architects made the strategic decision to preserve the original six-story spiral staircase that had been a defining feature of the Barneys building, transforming it into the architectural centerpiece of 25,000 square feet of exhibition galleries that would become the public face of the institution.1 When the Rubin Museum of Himalayan Art opened its doors on October 2, 2004, it presented itself as the first museum in the United States dedicated exclusively to Himalayan art, an ambitious declaration that underscored both the cultural significance of the region and the perceived gap in American cultural institutions' engagement with Tibetan and broader Himalayan artistic traditions.1 The inaugural exhibition program featured scholarly presentations such as "Methods of Transcendence," "Portraits of Transmission," and "The Demonic Divine in Himalayan Art," establishing from the outset that the museum would balance popular accessibility with rigorous academic engagement.1 Beyond exhibition galleries, the museum incorporated multiple programmatic spaces including a theater for multimedia events and performances, a café called Café Serai, a gift shop, and eventually an education center with studios and classrooms, creating what functioned as a comprehensive cultural institution dedicated to Himalayan perspectives and artistic traditions.1

The museum's founding collection comprised more than 1,000 objects from the Rubins' personal collection, with subsequent acquisitions and donations expanding this to nearly 4,000 objects by the time of the museum's closure, spanning more than 1,500 years of artistic production from the Tibetan Plateau, Northern India, Nepal, Bhutan, Mongolia, and culturally related areas of present-day China.1 The collection's significance lay not only in its size but in the historical breadth and artistic quality of its holdings, featuring works ranging from ancient Buddhist sculptures and ritual objects to contemporary Himalayan art created by living artists engaging with traditional forms and iconographies.1 Throughout its first twenty years of operation, the Rubin presented over 145 exhibitions, including scholarly historical examinations, cross-cultural comparative studies, contemporary art projects, photography-focused presentations, and innovative interactive installations, establishing itself as a major force in North American scholarship and public engagement with Himalayan art. The museum's impact extended beyond its walls through publications that became essential resources for art historians and scholars, through docent-led public programs that attracted visitors from diverse backgrounds, and through pioneering educational initiatives that introduced students to Himalayan artistic traditions and the Buddhist and Hindu philosophical frameworks that informed them.1

The Structural Crisis and Strategic Response Leading to the Global Model

Despite the Rubin's critical acclaim and cultural significance, the institution faced mounting financial pressures beginning in the late 2010s that would ultimately catalyze its transformation into a globally distributed model.1 In 2019, responding to budgetary constraints that reflected broader economic challenges facing cultural institutions, the museum implemented difficult cost-cutting measures including firing one-quarter of its staff, reducing operating hours, and decreasing the number of special exhibitions offered annually.1 These measures proved insufficient to address the underlying structural challenges facing the institution as it operated a large physical building in an expensive Manhattan location with high maintenance, insurance, and operational costs while serving primarily a geographically localized audience. The arrival of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 compounded these financial difficulties, as the institution faced temporary closures, reduced visitor capacity, and the economic uncertainties that impacted museums globally.1 Rather than accepting a slow decline or seeking only to maintain the status quo, the museum's leadership—including Executive Director Jorrit Britschgi, who had joined in 2017 with extensive experience in international museum practice and Asian art scholarship—undertook an in-depth analysis of the cultural sector, the museum's position within it, and the evolving needs of international audiences in the context of accelerating technological change.

During the years preceding its closure announcement, the Rubin had begun experimenting with alternative operational models that would prove crucial to its transformation strategy. The museum launched major digital resources including the Project Himalayan Art platform, which offered 108 object-focused essays, introductions to key themes, and an interactive map of Himalayan cultural regions.8,10 The museum also developed the AWAKEN podcast, exploring themes at the intersection of Buddhism, contemplative practice, science, and contemporary life, which by 2022 had achieved an audience of 6,000+ listeners in over 150 countries, demonstrating significant appetite for the museum's content beyond geographic limitations. The museum created the Mandala Lab, an immersive interactive installation inspired by Buddhist principles and neuroscience research, which was originally built as a permanent fixture in the Chelsea building but proved easily adaptable to traveling formats, opening in Bilbao in 2022 where it attracted 18,000 visitors, subsequently traveling to London in 2023 where it welcomed 18,500 visitors, and then to Milan in 2024 where it received over 40,000 visitors. These experimental projects provided proof of concept that the museum's mission could be effectively advanced through digital platforms, traveling installations, and strategic partnerships with institutions beyond New York City.

In January 2024, the museum announced its bold decision to close its Manhattan building on October 6, 2024, and to transition into operating as what it termed a "museum without walls"—a deliberately chosen phrase signaling not closure or diminishment but rather a fundamental reimagining of what institutional form could best serve the museum's mission.1 Executive Director Jorrit Britschgi articulated the decision in philosophical terms, noting that "The art in our galleries teaches us that change is constant and inevitable. We take inspiration from this, as we boldly let go of an old model and redefine what it means to be a museum in the 21st century." The decision reflected careful strategic analysis regarding how the Rubin could deploy its collection, knowledge, networks, and financial resources with maximum impact for global audiences while ensuring the institution's long-term sustainability and ability to fulfill its mission of advancing appreciation and understanding of Himalayan art. Rather than attempting to sustain an expensive physical plant while facing persistent operational deficits, the institution would concentrate resources on expanding access to its collection and content through multiple simultaneous channels, positioning itself as a hub and platform for artists, scholars, and practitioners from the Himalayan region and diaspora.

Traveling Exhibitions and the Gateway to Himalayan Art Initiative

Central to the Rubin's post-closure global strategy is the Gateway to Himalayan Art traveling exhibition, which represents one of the most ambitious institutional initiatives in the museum's history, designed specifically to bring curated presentations of Himalayan art to university museums and cultural institutions across North America and internationally.8 Gateway to Himalayan Art originated as a cornerstone exhibition at the original Chelsea location, introducing viewers to the main forms, concepts, meanings, and living traditions of Himalayan art through carefully selected objects from the Rubin's permanent collection and supplemented by significant loans from other institutions.8 The traveling iteration of the exhibition, which debuted at Lehigh University Art Galleries in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania in January 2023 and subsequently traveled to the McMullen Museum of Art at Boston College in fall 2023, is specifically designed for colleges, universities, and art museums, with educational materials supporting faculty teaching in art history, Buddhist studies, religion, and related disciplines.8 The exhibition explores exemplary objects organized into thematic sections including Figures and Symbols, Materials and Techniques, and Purpose and Function, with extensive didactic graphics and in-gallery digital materials that help visitors engage with the fundamental concepts underlying Himalayan art.8

The exhibition's structure reflects the museum's educational philosophy that Himalayan art functions as both historically significant cultural production and as a resource for contemporary understanding of spiritual practice, ethics, and the human condition.8,10 As of the museum's 2026 programming announcements, Gateway to Himalayan Art is scheduled to open at the Jordan Schnitzer Museum of Art at the University of Oregon in Eugene and at the USC Pacific Asia Museum in Pasadena, California, with plans for continued national distribution through 2026 and potentially beyond.2,2,2 Complementing the traveling exhibition is a comprehensive 504-page publication titled Himalayan Art in 108 Objects, which features contributions from approximately seventy scholars from different fields and disciplines and was published in June 2023 by the Rubin Museum of Art and Scala Arts Publishers, presenting significant objects from the Neolithic era to the present day.8 The scale of this initiative—encompassing a traveling exhibition, scholarly publication, and expanded digital platform—represents the Rubin's commitment to fundamentally reshaping how Himalayan art is taught and understood in American higher education, addressing what the museum identified as a significant gap in undergraduate curricula regarding Himalayan, Tibetan, and Inner Asian cultures.8,10

The traveling exhibition model offers particular advantages that align with the museum's new operational philosophy, as it provides direct access to Himalayan art for audiences in multiple cities while generating revenue through rental fees that support the institution's broader operations, and creates opportunities for collaborative programming with partner institutions that might not otherwise have resources to mount comprehensive exhibitions on Himalayan topics. The collection-sharing program that supports these traveling exhibitions operates through both short-term special exhibition loans and long-term object loans spanning three to five years, with the latter designed to enable more people in more places to learn about Himalayan art and cultures while providing meaningful, extended access that allows partnerships to develop between the Rubin and receiving institutions. Museums including the Worcester Museum of Art in Worcester, Massachusetts and others have entered into multi-year partnerships with the Rubin to display works from the permanent collection, with these objects serving as anchors for the museum's digital educational resources and as focal points for curatorial programming developed in collaboration with Rubin staff.6

The Tibetan Buddhist Shrine Room and Long-Term Institutional Partnerships

Among the most significant manifestations of the Rubin's global strategy is the relocation of its beloved Tibetan Buddhist Shrine Room to the Brooklyn Museum, which represents not a diminishment of the exhibition but rather its transformation into a long-term institutional partnership demonstrating the museum's commitment to maintaining presence in New York City cultural life while expanding geographic reach. The Tibetan Buddhist Shrine Room, which operated as one of the most popular permanent installations at the original Chelsea location from its opening in 2013 through the building's closure in 2024, was experienced by more than one million visitors during its original run, making it arguably the most widely encountered exhibition of Himalayan art in North American cultural institutions. The installation presented more than 100 artworks and ritual objects from the Rubin's collection arranged as they would be displayed in an elaborate private household shrine—a sacred domestic space where Tibetan Buddhist practitioners make offerings, pray, contemplate, and perform rituals.

The Shrine Room's design incorporated elements of traditional Tibetan architecture and employed the color schemes of traditional Tibetan homes, creating an immersive environment that offered visitors the opportunity to experience Tibetan religious art in its proper cultural context rather than as decontextualized museum objects displayed against white walls. The installation followed the Geluk Tibetan Buddhist tradition and included scroll paintings known as thangkas, sculptures, ritual implements, and musical instruments dating from the twelfth to twenty-first centuries, arranged on traditional Tibetan furniture according to their hierarchy in Tibetan Buddhist practice. The experience was designed to engage multiple senses, with chanted prayers by monks and nuns, flickering butter lamps, and the subtle smell of incense creating a multisensory environment that reflected how Buddhist rituals engage practitioners' complete phenomenological being.

Beginning in June 2025, the Shrine Room opened in a custom-designed space within the Brooklyn Museum's Arts of Asia galleries as part of a six-year partnership between the two institutions, representing an unprecedented collaboration in which a major contemporary art museum opened dedicated gallery space for a neighboring institution's traveling installation. This partnership extends beyond the physical exhibition to include collection sharing and curatorial exchange, with Rubin and Brooklyn Museum staff collaborating on programming, scholarly research, and future exhibitions that examine connections between Himalayan art and other Asian art traditions represented in the Brooklyn Museum's permanent collection. The partnership exemplifies the Rubin's strategic approach to maintaining cultural presence in its original home city while simultaneously demonstrating that an institution without a dedicated physical plant can still create meaningful long-term commitments to specific locations and communities. The Shrine Room partnership is supported by significant funding from private donors including Anne and Albert Chao, the Museum's Board of Trustees, and individual donors, as well as public funding from the New York City Department of Cultural Affairs in partnership with the City Council, demonstrating the continued public commitment to maintaining access to Himalayan art in New York.

Educational Innovation and the Mandala Lab Initiative

The Mandala Lab represents perhaps the most innovative of the Rubin's educational initiatives, functioning as an interactive installation that translates Buddhist psychological and ethical teachings into a contemporary experiential learning environment designed to cultivate emotional awareness, empathy, and contemplative practice among diverse audiences. The original Mandala Lab opened as a permanent installation at the Chelsea location in September 2021, featuring five thought-provoking, playful experiences designed to guide visitors along an inner journey focused on self-awareness and awareness of others, inviting exploration of challenging emotions including pride, attachment, envy, anger, and ignorance, while imagining how visitors might have the power to transform these emotions into wisdom. The installation employed multisensory elements including videos accompanied by scents, a sculpture that invites collective breathing, curated percussion instruments dipped in water, and immersive visual environments, creating what participants described in evaluation comments as experiences that left them feeling "more thoughtful, introspective, and self-aware" or "in a better state of mind."

The Mandala Lab's theoretical foundation draws on established research in neuroscience, contemplative studies, and social-emotional learning, grounding its experiential activities in evidence-based practices for developing emotional regulation, empathy, and resilience. The installation was developed in collaboration with Emory University's Social, Emotional, and Ethical Learning (SEE Learning) program, which was itself developed through collaboration between Emory scholars and His Holiness the Dalai Lama, bringing together Buddhist philosophical traditions and contemporary neuroscience in an integrated pedagogical framework. This combination of Buddhist wisdom traditions and scientific research positions the Mandala Lab at the intersection of cultural heritage, contemporary psychology, and educational innovation, making it equally valuable as an introduction to Himalayan spiritual traditions and as a tool for personal development and mental health support.

Following the Mandala Lab's success as a permanent installation, the Rubin invested in creating a traveling version designed to bring this experience to global audiences without the need for a dedicated physical museum building. The traveling Mandala Lab debuted in Bilbao, Spain, in June 2022 as part of The Wellbeing Project's artistic programming for the 2022 Wellbeing Summit, attracting 18,000 visitors during its seventeen-day run and receiving lead support from The Robert H.N. Ho Family Foundation Global. The installation subsequently traveled to the London Design Festival in 2023, where it welcomed 18,500 visitors during its nine-week run and featured a free evening program that included a live gong-and-water improvisation by Dame Evelyn Glennie and the first-ever "Breathe-In," a synchronized participatory breathing meditation. The Mandala Lab then traveled to Milan, Italy, where it opened at BAM—Biblioteca degli Alberi Milano—in March 2024, welcoming over 40,000 visitors during its three-week run and demonstrating the installation's remarkable capacity to engage large diverse audiences in European cultural contexts.

Beyond the traveling physical installation, the Rubin has developed an extensive program bringing Mandala Lab experiences into New York City schools through the Mandala Lab in Your Classroom initiative, a free program available to third through twelfth-grade teachers in New York City schools that aims to cultivate emotional awareness and compassion through Himalayan art, mindfulness techniques, and interdisciplinary learning. The classroom program is led by experienced Rubin teaching artists who guide lessons that interweave art-making activities, guided discussion, engagement with objects from the Rubin's touch collection of 140 objects that students can observe, handle, and sketch, and mindfulness techniques to help students develop critical life skills. Lessons are approximately fifty minutes in length and are designed with flexibility to accommodate varied school period schedules, allowing teachers to select individual lessons or multiple consecutive lessons for their classes. The classroom program is grounded in Social, Emotional, and Ethical Learning principles developed through the Emory-Dalai Lama collaboration and represents the museum's commitment to serving students and teachers in its original home city even after closing its physical location.2

The Rubin has made the lesson plans from the Mandala Lab in Your Classroom program available for free download online, enabling teachers anywhere in the world to activate the teachings of the Mandala Lab in their own classrooms regardless of access to the physical installation. Additionally, the museum has developed plans to expand the traveling Mandala Lab to additional European cities beyond Milan, with installations planned for multiple major European cities through 2026, and the museum is actively pursuing corporate and foundation partnerships to support continued expansion of this initiative.2 The multiyear Mandala Lab campaign has received leadership support from The Robert H.N. Ho Family Foundation Global, with additional major support from foundations and individual donors including the Milton and Sally Avery Arts Foundation, Barbara Bowman, the Dharma Joy Foundation, Agnes Gund, and Shelley and Donald Rubin themselves. This broad philanthropic support demonstrates confidence among cultural funders in the Mandala Lab's innovative approach to making Buddhist teachings and Himalayan art accessible to contemporary diverse audiences across multiple continents.

Digital Platforms and Project Himalayan Art

The Rubin's commitment to digital accessibility is exemplified through Project Himalayan Art, a comprehensive three-part initiative comprising an integrated digital platform, a scholarly publication, and a traveling exhibition, all designed to support the inclusion of Himalayan cultures into undergraduate teaching on Asia.8,10 The digital platform features 108 object-focused essays written by scholars and specialists, introductions to key themes in Himalayan art and culture, content drawn from the traveling exhibition, and an interactive multimedia map that highlights Himalayan cultural regions represented in the Rubin Museum's collection, including Nepal, Bhutan, and culturally related areas in present-day India, China, and Mongolia.8,10 The platform provides extensive didactic materials helping users engage with fundamental concepts in Himalayan art including representations of deities, the materials and techniques employed in creating traditional objects, and the purposes these objects serve in religious, social, and domestic contexts.8,10

Complementing the digital platform and traveling exhibition is the comprehensive publication Himalayan Art in 108 Objects, which features contributions from approximately seventy scholars from diverse academic fields and disciplines, presenting significant objects from the Rubin's collection spanning from the Neolithic era to contemporary artistic production.8,10 The publication was released in June 2023 and has become an essential resource for art historians, scholars, students, and enthusiasts of Himalayan art, providing richly illustrated presentations of major works alongside scholarly essays contextualizing objects within broader historical, religious, and aesthetic frameworks.8 The book serves as both a standalone resource and as a companion to the digital platform and traveling exhibition, with readers able to explore the same artworks across multiple media formats, deepening engagement and supporting different learning styles and educational contexts.8,10

As of 2026, the Rubin continues to expand the Project Himalayan Art digital platform with new resources for faculty and students, including additional essays, videos, images, and audio materials.2,2,2 The resources developed for Project Himalayan Art have become foundational to new academic partnerships, including the development of Columbia University's first-ever Tibetan art-focused seminar course, which debuted in spring 2026 and was developed by the Rubin's curatorial team, bringing university-level instruction on Tibetan aesthetics and Buddhist art directly into partnership with the Rubin's pedagogical expertise.2,2,2 The platform also features hundreds of definitions and audio pronunciations for key terms essential to understanding Himalayan art and cultures, making the content accessible to learners at different levels of prior knowledge and to international audiences for whom English or other languages of instruction may not be native.8,10

The Rubin has also entered into partnership with Bloomberg Connects, a dynamic app that cultural institutions customize to their content and mission, offered for free to users and supported by Bloomberg Philanthropies.7 Following the museum's closure of its physical building, Bloomberg Connects remains a key partner in the Rubin's global museum model, offering audio tours for traveling exhibitions and content in over forty languages, with links to this content integrated into the museum's website, extending the reach of the Rubin's exhibitions far beyond those who can visit in person.7 The partnership demonstrates how contemporary digital infrastructure and philanthropic support can enable cultural institutions to maintain meaningful engagement with international audiences while operating without a traditional physical plant.7

Artist Support, Research Grants, and Community Engagement

A central commitment of the Rubin's global museum model is direct support for contemporary artists and scholars working in the field of Himalayan art, implemented through the Rubin Museum's annual grant program, which provides unrestricted funding to artists, creatives, and scholars in the field with the explicit goal of expanding awareness and understanding of Himalayan art globally. The grant program operates through multiple tracks including the Rubin Art Prize, an annual unrestricted award of $30,000 to recognize innovative individuals and collectives who have made significant marks in creative and critically relevant dialogues between Himalayan art and contemporary life. The inaugural recipient of the Rubin Art Prize in 2024 was artist Tenzin Gyurmey Dorjee, a Tibetan refugee-born artist based in Himachal Pradesh, India, whose practice explores themes of displacement, belonging, and cultural heritage through multiple media including painting, sculpture, and mixed media. Dorjee's work examines paradoxes present in seemingly ordinary things, addressing how global changes in culture, politics, climate, and science impact local surroundings, with his artistic vision grounded in his experience as a second-generation Tibetan refugee.

In 2025, the Rubin Art Prize was awarded to Khadok—Tibetan Artists' Collective, a Dharamsala-based collective founded in 2023 by Tashi Nima, Tenzin Melak, and Lea Taake to provide a dedicated space for Tibetan contemporary visual artists to present their work, exchange ideas, and strengthen their practice with particular focus on emerging artists. Khadok emerged from a deeply emotional circumstance when JC (Jigme Choedak), a twenty-six-year-old Tibetan contemporary artist, passed away in 2023, leading the three founders to create a memorial exhibition honoring his life and work within forty-nine days of his passing—a critical timespan in Buddhist tradition—within the temple complex of the Dalai Lama. That initial exhibition sparked recognition that there was significant demand and interest in showcasing contemporary Tibetan artistic work within the community, leading the founders to establish Khadok as an ongoing platform, meaning "colors" in Tibetan, intended to celebrate the diversity, vibrancy, and richness of Tibetan artistic expression. Since its founding, Khadok has grown into a vibrant platform for artistic exchange, community engagement, and youth empowerment, operating exhibitions, mural projects, workshops, talks, and youth engagement initiatives in schools and community spaces across Dharamsala and beyond.

Beyond the annual art prize, the Rubin's research grant program provides funding ranging from $2,500 to $25,000 for art and research initiatives that promote the cultural legacy and living traditions of Himalayan regions. The 2025 grant cycle selected fifteen projects totaling $200,000 in funding from among 132 letters of inquiry received from applicants in over thirty countries, demonstrating remarkable global reach and indicating the critical need for this funding resource among scholars, artists, and nonprofit organizations working in the field. Representative 2025 grant recipients included projects addressing conservation training for painters in Mongolia, documentation of historic art in temples in central Bhutan, research into the provenance of cultural objects removed during colonial periods, development of Tibetan visual arts curriculum for educators, and creation of multimedia installations exploring Buddhist concepts of interdependence in northern India. The range and geographic distribution of funded projects demonstrates the Rubin's commitment to supporting diverse forms of knowledge production and artistic expression across the greater Himalayan region and diaspora.

International Partnerships and Institutional Collaborations

The Rubin's transformation into a global museum has been accompanied by the development of strategic partnerships with cultural institutions, educational organizations, and community groups across multiple continents.2,2,9 One significant partnership involves the Wangduechhoeling Palace Museum and Cultural Center in Bhutan, where the Rubin Museum of Himalayan Art and the Bhutan Foundation, a nonprofit organization based in Washington, D.C., have launched a collaboration to promote cultural preservation and education in Bhutan, working closely with the Wangduechhoeling Palace, the birthplace of Bhutan's monarchy.9 This partnership reflects the museum's commitment to supporting cultural institutions in the Himalayan region itself rather than merely viewing the region as a source of art for Western consumption.9

Another major partnership involves the Itumbaha Museum in Kathmandu, Nepal, the first public museum gallery within a vihara (Newar Buddhist monastery) in Nepal, which resulted from the Rubin's discovery that a fourteenth-century wooden apsara sculpture in its collection had originally been located at Itumbaha and had been unlawfully removed in 1999. Following the Rubin's voluntary return of this object to the monastery in January 2022, the museum partnered with the Keshchandra Mahavihara Conservation Society and curator Swosti Rajbhandari Kayastha to help realize the Conservation Society's decade-long desire to create a museum at Itumbaha. The Rubin provided principal funding and expertise on museum practices to help the Conservation Society document, preserve, and interpret Itumbaha's collection of over 500 objects, with the museum ultimately inaugurated on July 29, 2023, featuring three display galleries curated by Kayastha and her students. This partnership demonstrates the Rubin's commitment to ethical collecting practices and its willingness to return objects to their communities of origin while simultaneously supporting those communities' own cultural preservation efforts.

At the international level, the Rubin played a significant role in supporting Nepal's inaugural participation as a nation at the Venice Biennale in 2022, providing lead global support for the Nepal Pavilion titled "Tales of Muted Spirits—Dispersed Threads—Twisted Shangri-La," curated by artists Sheelasha Rajbhandari and Hit Man Gurung and featuring the work of contemporary artist Tsherin Sherpa. The pavilion, which opened at Sant'Anna Project Space One located strategically between the two main venues of La Biennale di Venezia, presented artworks that challenged stereotypical conceptualizations of Nepal and the broader Himalayan region, rejecting the duality of viewing these places as simultaneously impoverished backwaters and romantic Shangri-Las. The Rubin's support for this international contemporary art project, involving coordination with Nepal's Ministry of Culture, the Nepal Academy of Fine Arts, and the Siddhartha Arts Foundation, extended the museum's influence into the realm of contemporary artistic representation on the global stage, connecting Himalayan art to urgent contemporary conversations about decolonization, representation, and artistic sovereignty.

Recent and Ongoing 2026 Global Initiatives

As the Rubin enters its second full year of operation as a global museum, the institution has announced an ambitious series of exhibitions, programs, and strategic initiatives demonstrating the viability and momentum of its decentralized model.2,2,2 The traveling exhibition Gateway to Himalayan Art is expanding its national reach with scheduled openings at the Jordan Schnitzer Museum of Art at the University of Oregon in Eugene and the USC Pacific Asia Museum in Pasadena, California, continuing the exhibition's mission to bring comprehensive introductions to Himalayan art to university communities and bringing the perspectives of multiple constituencies to bear on how Himalayan art is understood and contextualized in educational settings.2,2,2 In New York City, the public art sculpture "About a Living Culture" by Nepalese artist IMAGINE (Sneha Shrestha), created in collaboration with the New York City Department of Transportation's Art Program, remains on view through September 2026 in Diversity Plaza in Jackson Heights, Queens, a bustling cultural and transit hub.
