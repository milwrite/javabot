Institutional Support, Informal Learning, and Student Persistence in Higher Education: A Synthesis of Retention Theory, Cultural Capital, and High-Impact Practices

Student persistence in higher education remains a persistent challenge facing contemporary universities, with nearly one-quarter of entering students failing to persist to their second year and significant disparities existing across demographic groups31. Over the past three decades, three major theoretical frameworks have emerged to explain the mechanisms underlying student retention and success: Vincent Tinto's model of student integration, Tara Yosso's conceptualization of community cultural wealth, and George Kuh's identification of high-impact practices. While these frameworks have profoundly shaped institutional practice and research agendas in higher education, their integration remains incomplete, particularly regarding the role of informal learning environments and institutional support systems as mechanisms that mediate between student characteristics and outcomes. This literature review synthesizes the theoretical foundations and empirical evidence from these three major approaches, exploring how institutions can leverage both formal and informal support structures—what might be termed an "institutional fallback" system—to promote persistence and success among diverse student populations. The review identifies areas of convergence among these frameworks while also highlighting tensions and gaps that future research must address.

The Foundation of Modern Student Retention Theory: Tinto's Integration Framework and Its Evolution

Vincent Tinto's model of student integration has fundamentally shaped how educators and researchers understand the mechanisms underlying student persistence and departure from institutions of higher education1,6,24. First articulated in the 1970s and subsequently refined through longitudinal studies and theoretical elaborations, Tinto's framework argues that student decisions to persist in college are shaped not merely by their individual attributes, backgrounds, and goals, but rather emerge from their cumulative experiences within the social and academic communities that comprise the university1. The central theoretical insight of Tinto's work holds that student integration along two primary dimensions—academic integration and social integration—constitutes the fundamental mechanism through which institutions either promote or inhibit student persistence6,19. Academic integration occurs when students become intellectually engaged with the scholarly life of the institution, developing meaningful connections to the intellectual community and demonstrating commitment to the learning enterprise. Conversely, social integration encompasses the development of relationships and connections outside the classroom, wherein students come to perceive themselves as valued members of a community that extends beyond purely academic domains19.

Critically, Tinto's theoretical contribution extends beyond merely identifying these dimensions of integration to argue that they interact dynamically with student commitment and institutional characteristics over time6. His longitudinal model proposes that students progress through stages of college, from separation and transition to incorporation, during which their level of integration shapes their ongoing commitment to the institution and their subsequent decisions regarding persistence1. When students experience positive interactions within the academic and social communities of the institution—when they feel recognized as valued members and experience a sense of belonging—their institutional commitment deepens, thereby increasing the likelihood that they will persist through inevitable challenges1. Conversely, negative experiences or feelings of incongruence and isolation inhibit the integration process and diminish the psychological bonds that bind students to the institution19. The implications of this theory proved transformative for institutional practice, as universities began developing programs explicitly designed to enhance student integration, from first-year seminars to learning communities to faculty mentoring initiatives1.

Yet while Tinto's framework proved remarkably influential, subsequent scholarship has identified important limitations and contextual factors that the original formulation did not adequately address. Researchers examining community college students, who represent a growing proportion of the higher education population, discovered that Tinto's framework required modification to account for the distinctive characteristics of students who often attend part-time, balance employment and family responsibilities, and lack time for participation in traditional co-curricular activities19. Investigation of community college persistence revealed that despite the theoretical expectation that social integration would be minimal, most first-year community college students did indeed develop attachments to their institutions, and these attachments predicted second-year persistence19. Moreover, this research demonstrated that for community college students, academic and social integration did not develop distinctly but rather occurred in concert, with the same classroom-based activities generating both academic and social relatedness19. This finding challenged assumptions about the necessary separation of academic and social spheres and suggested that the pathways to integration might vary substantially depending on institutional context, student characteristics, and the structure of educational experiences.

Furthermore, recent theoretical refinements emphasize that the meaning students derive from their engagements with others constitutes the critical mechanism through which integration influences persistence1. This reconceptualization shifts analytical attention from the quantity of engagement—the frequency with which students participate in institutional activities—to the qualitative dimensions of those engagements and, crucially, the interpretive work that students undertake in making meaning from their experiences1. Students who perceive themselves as belonging to the academic and social communities of the university, who come to understand that they matter and that their presence is valued, develop a sense of connection that generates sustained motivation to persist even when institutional or personal challenges arise1. This emphasis on the phenomenological experience of belonging, on students' subjective sense of mattering and inclusion, marks an important evolution in retention theory, one that recognizes the psychological and emotional dimensions of persistence alongside structural and behavioral factors.

Recognizing and Mobilizing Community Cultural Wealth: Yosso's Critical Race Theory Approach

Emerging from critical race theory perspectives on education and responding to perceived limitations in existing retention frameworks, Tara Yosso's conceptualization of community cultural wealth offers a fundamentally different lens through which to understand student assets and the mechanisms through which students navigate higher education environments2,7. Rather than adopting what Yosso terms a "deficit" framework—one that emphasizes the academic, social, and cultural disadvantages that marginalized students allegedly bring to college—her community cultural wealth model focuses analytical attention on the array of cultural knowledge, skills, abilities, and contacts possessed by socially marginalized groups that frequently remain unrecognized and underutilized within institutional contexts2,7. This theoretical shift represents a profound challenge to traditional interpretations of cultural capital theory, which had long emphasized the forms of capital—typically middle-class linguistic, aesthetic, and behavioral dispositions—that schools and universities implicitly valued and rewarded7. By contrast, Yosso's framework argues that communities of color and other marginalized populations nurture multiple forms of capital that represent genuine assets for academic and personal success, yet these assets remain largely invisible to institutional actors operating within dominant cultural frameworks2.

Yosso identifies at least six distinct forms of capital within her community cultural wealth model, each representing different types of knowledge, resources, and capacities that students may draw upon in their pursuit of higher education2,7,11. Aspirational capital encompasses the hopes and dreams that students and their families maintain, demonstrating that students from historically marginalized communities often demonstrate high educational aspirations despite persistent structural inequities that might discourage such aspirations11. Linguistic capital refers not merely to proficiency in standard English but rather to the diverse language and communication skills students bring from their home and community contexts, including abilities in storytelling, code-switching, and oral tradition—capabilities that represent sophisticated communicative competencies with potential applications to academic work11. Familial capital encompasses the social and personal human resources drawn from extended family and community networks, representing the wisdom, values, and knowledge that students can leverage into positive educational experiences11. Social capital refers to peers and other social contacts who can provide support and information, while navigational capital encompasses the skills and abilities through which students navigate social institutions, including educational spaces and potentially hostile or unsupportive environments2,11. Finally, resistant capital emerges from the historical legacy of resistance to subordination exhibited by communities of color, providing students with frameworks for understanding and challenging inequitable structures7,11.

The significance of Yosso's framework extends beyond theoretical clarification to suggest fundamentally different approaches to institutional support and student success initiatives. If students arrive at college bearing multiple forms of cultural capital that institutions have historically failed to recognize or mobilize, then retention initiatives should focus not on compensating for perceived deficits but rather on creating institutional environments that acknowledge, validate, and actively incorporate the multiple strengths that marginalized students bring2. Research employing Yosso's framework reveals that first-generation, underrepresented students in community colleges, when enrolled in comprehensive college transition programs, do indeed mobilize and draw upon all six forms of community cultural wealth in ways that support their academic persistence, resilience, and sense of belonging20. Particularly striking, aspirational capital emerged as the most strongly expressed form of cultural wealth among these students, serving as a primary motivational force that sustained their persistence despite systemic barriers20. Familial and social capital further supported student success by providing emotional encouragement and critical networks through which students could access information and resources, while navigational and resistant capital empowered students to identify and access institutional resources and challenge inequitable structures20. These findings suggest that institutions which deliberately recognize and work with the cultural assets that marginalized students bring substantially enhance outcomes for these populations.

The integration of Yosso's framework with Tinto's retention theory reveals important complementarities while also highlighting tensions in how different scholars conceptualize student success. Both frameworks emphasize the centrality of belonging and integration to student persistence, yet they approach the question of how belonging develops through somewhat different lenses. Tinto's framework emphasizes the structural and behavioral dimensions of integration—the extent to which students develop connections to individuals and communities within the institution—while Yosso's approach foregrounds the cultural resources and capabilities that students bring to the integration process1,2. Together, these perspectives suggest that student persistence emerges from the interaction between institutional structures and practices and the cultural resources, aspirations, and capabilities that students possess. When institutions create spaces and practices that validate and mobilize the community cultural wealth that marginalized students bring, these students appear more likely to develop the sense of belonging and integration that Tinto identifies as central to persistence20.

High-Impact Practices and Student Engagement: Kuh's Contribution to Understanding Effective Learning Experiences

George Kuh's extensive body of research on student engagement and high-impact practices represents a complementary and in many ways orthogonal contribution to retention literature compared to Tinto's integration framework3,12. Rather than focusing primarily on the social and institutional mechanisms through which students develop commitment and belonging, Kuh's work emphasizes the learning activities and pedagogical approaches that generate deep intellectual engagement and substantial learning gains3. Drawing on data from the National Survey of Student Engagement (NSSE), which has surveyed hundreds of thousands of students across thousands of institutions, Kuh and colleagues identified a set of undergraduate experiences that demonstrated particularly strong associations with learning outcomes and retention3,8. These high-impact practices—including first-year seminars and experiences, learning communities, service-learning courses, research with faculty, internships and field experiences, study abroad, and culminating senior experiences—share several common characteristics3,8. Specifically, high-impact practices demand considerable time and effort on the part of students, facilitate learning outside the classroom, require meaningful interactions with faculty and diverse peers, encourage collaboration with students possessing different backgrounds and perspectives, and provide frequent and substantive feedback on student performance8,8.

The significance of high-impact practices lies not merely in their association with student learning but rather in evidence suggesting that participation in these practices generates particularly pronounced benefits for students who enter college with lower levels of academic preparation3,25. This finding proves especially important given persistent inequities in college completion, wherein students from low-income backgrounds, first-generation students, and students of color demonstrate lower completion rates than their more privileged peers3. Research employing NSSE data reveals that when students across demographic groups participate in high-impact practices, learning outcome differences attributable to background factors substantially diminish, suggesting that these practices may serve as "equalizers" that promote success across diverse student populations25. Students who engage more frequently in deep learning behaviors—which represent the intellectual processes that high-impact practices are designed to foster—report greater educational gains, higher grades, and greater satisfaction with college16. Moreover, the effects of deep approaches to learning appear relatively consistent across disciplinary contexts, suggesting that the underlying mechanisms through which engagement promotes learning operate across the curriculum16.

Kuh's research further demonstrates that most high-impact practices still reach only a fraction of today's college students, with substantial disparities in access existing across demographic groups3,30. Historically underrepresented minority students participate in high-impact practices at lower rates than their white and Asian peers, creating educational disparities in engagement that translate into disparities in learning outcomes and persistence30. This recognition of unequal access to high-impact practices prompted Kuh to recommend that institutions ensure all students participate in at least two high-impact practices during their college careers3. However, considerable evidence suggests that moving toward universal access to high-impact practices requires intentional institutional effort and resource allocation. Barriers to participation include financial constraints (particularly for expensive experiences such as study abroad), logistical challenges such as scheduling conflicts and lack of information, and structural factors such as academic preparation and work obligations that constrain some students' availability30. For institutions to truly leverage high-impact practices as a mechanism for promoting equitable student success, they must deliberately identify and attempt to mitigate these barriers, particularly for students facing historical underrepresentation in higher education.

The relationship between Kuh's high-impact practices framework and Tinto's integration theory suggests important insights about the mechanisms through which institutional practices promote persistence. High-impact practices appear to function partly through the integration mechanisms that Tinto identifies—by bringing students into meaningful contact with peers, faculty, and the broader academic community, these practices create opportunities for the development of academic and social connections that strengthen institutional commitment3,25. Yet the high-impact practices framework adds important texture to integration theory by highlighting how the quality and substance of those engagements matter profoundly. Not all student-faculty interactions, not all collaborations with peers, generate equivalent impacts; rather, it is the intellectually rigorous, substantively meaningful, and reflective character of high-impact experiences that produces the strong learning and retention effects that research demonstrates3. This insight suggests that institutional efforts to promote retention through integration must attend not only to the structure and availability of opportunities for engagement but also to the pedagogical quality and intellectual substance of those opportunities.

Institutional Support Systems, Informal Learning, and the Concept of Institutional Fallback

Building upon and extending the foundational theories articulated by Tinto, Yosso, and Kuh, recent research has increasingly emphasized the role of informal learning environments and institutional support systems as critical mechanisms through which students navigate higher education and develop the capabilities necessary for success4,9,13. While formal educational experiences—the structured curriculum, the classroom instruction, the official co-curricular programs—comprise the visible, intentionally designed institutional infrastructure, students engage in substantial learning and development within informal contexts that institutions may not explicitly recognize or support4,9,13. These informal learning environments encompass peer interactions outside the classroom, online communities where students exchange information and advice, campus spaces where students gather informally, and countless moments of interaction through which students develop understanding and skills4. Increasingly, scholars recognize that what might be termed an "institutional fallback" system—the informal support structures and peer networks that students construct and through which they navigate institutional environments—constitutes a critical complement to formal support systems and may prove particularly important for students who lack the cultural capital or prior experience to navigate bureaucratic institutional structures independently.

Peer-to-peer learning represents a particularly significant component of this informal institutional fallback system, with recent evidence suggesting that informal peer-based learning constitutes over fifty percent of total learning in many organizations and educational settings9. The mechanisms through which peer learning generates developmental benefits are multifaceted. Peers may serve as sources of practical information about academic and institutional processes, helping others navigate course selection, major requirements, and support services. They may also provide emotional support and encouragement, helping struggling students persist through challenges9. Furthermore, peer learning communities can model effective academic strategies, normalize help-seeking behaviors, and create social contexts in which students feel comfortable asking questions and working collaboratively9. Research on peer mentoring programs, which formalize and structure what might otherwise be informal peer relationships, reveals that peer mentoring constitutes an effective support mechanism yielding favorable impacts across multiple outcomes, including social integration, academic achievement, and retention22,39. The effectiveness of peer mentoring appears particularly pronounced for students who have experienced educational disadvantage, suggesting that peer support may be especially valuable for first-generation and underrepresented students who may lack access to other sources of support and guidance22.

First-generation college students, who comprise over forty percent of entering students at many institutions, may be especially reliant upon informal institutional fallback systems given their reduced access to family knowledge about college processes and expectations10. While institutions increasingly recognize the importance of targeted support for first-generation students through formal programs such as mentoring initiatives, financial aid counseling, and college transition programs, the informal peer networks that first-generation students develop appear equally critical to their success10,26. Research on the support networks of first-generation students reveals that students construct personalized networks comprising friends, family members, institutional personnel, and other contacts, and that the structure and quality of these networks significantly predict academic outcomes including persistence, GPA, and credit attainment26. Notably, for first-generation students, informal peer support often constitutes the most accessible and frequently used form of support, suggesting that institutions which successfully integrate peer support into their formal support infrastructure may expand access to critical resources among populations historically underserved by traditional institutional support26. Some institutions have attempted to formalize and structure peer support through programs such as peer tutoring networks, learning communities, and peer coaching, though questions remain about how to maintain the accessibility and authenticity that make informal peer relationships valuable while simultaneously ensuring that peer-based support reaches all students who need it5,9.

The rise of digital platforms and online communities has substantially expanded the infrastructure through which informal peer-based learning and support operate, creating new opportunities and challenges for institutional support systems4,13. Students increasingly exchange information, seek advice, and develop study communities through online platforms ranging from institutional learning management systems to social media networks to dedicated peer learning applications4. These digital spaces offer particular advantages for students who may struggle to participate in synchronous, physically localized learning communities—part-time students, students with disabilities, commuting students, and students with substantial work and family obligations may find digital peer communities more accessible than traditional campus-based peer groups4. Yet the emergence of these informal digital support systems also creates challenges for institutions attempting to assess, support, and improve student success initiatives. When much of students' learning and support-seeking occurs outside the official institutional infrastructure, institution leaders may lack visibility into the factors shaping student experiences and outcomes. Furthermore, the quality and accuracy of peer information exchanged through informal networks can vary substantially, potentially providing misinformation that harms rather than helps4.

The Integration of Formal Support Services, Informal Learning, and Targeted Interventions for Specific Student Populations

While the discussion above has emphasized informal institutional fallback systems and peer-based learning, the most effective institutional approaches appear to combine carefully designed formal support services with intentional recognition and support for informal peer networks5,10,14. For first-generation students, institutions have developed comprehensive programs that address multiple dimensions of student need through integrated formal and informal support structures. Exemplary programs such as UC San Diego's Chancellor's Associates Scholars Program and Rockford University's Regents First program provide financial support, academic coaching, peer mentoring, and college life skills development, recognizing that student success depends upon addressing interconnected academic, financial, social, and developmental needs10. These comprehensive programs typically adopt what might be termed a "twin-track" approach to support, combining mainstreamed support structures accessible to all students with targeted interventions addressing the specific needs of particular populations14. Such an approach acknowledges that while all students benefit from accessible, universal support systems—clear advising, peer tutoring, campus engagement opportunities—students facing particular barriers or possessing particular characteristics may require additional specialized support to overcome specific obstacles to success14.

The emergence of institutional recognition that both formal and informal support systems contribute to student success has prompted research into the relationship between campus-based support services and the informal networks that students construct26. This research reveals that student use of formal institutional support services appears partially mediated by the characteristics and composition of students' informal support networks26. Students who possess informal networks including individuals with college knowledge and institutional expertise are more likely to learn about and utilize campus-based support services, whereas students lacking such connections may remain unaware of available resources26. This finding suggests that institutions should attend explicitly to helping students develop the social capital necessary to navigate institutional bureaucracies and access formal support, recognizing that cultural wealth and social capital represent not innate characteristics but rather capabilities that can be developed through deliberate institutional practice. When institutions provide students with information about support services, facilitate connections between students and service providers, and create opportunities for students to learn about available resources through their informal networks, access to formal support increases substantially26.

For students with disabilities, institutional support systems present particular challenges and opportunities for the integration of formal and informal support14. Research on how students with disabilities experience higher education institutions reveals that while most institutions maintain formal disability support offices providing legally mandated accommodations such as accessible materials and modified assessments, the visibility and accessibility of these services vary substantially14. Students who proactively disclose their disabilities and engage with disability services offices access substantial support including assistive technology, academic accommodations, and supplemental services. However, students who do not disclose their disabilities, or who remain unaware of available services, receive little support, creating substantial disparities in access to support and, consequently, in academic outcomes and persistence14. These findings emphasize the importance of universal design principles that embed accessibility and accommodation considerations into mainstream institutional infrastructure rather than relegating support to specialized services accessed only by students who actively self-identify14. Furthermore, the dual importance of mainstream and specialized support suggests that institutions should work deliberately to reduce stigma surrounding disability disclosure and support-seeking, as fear of discrimination or marginalization may deter some students from accessing available support14.

Belonging, Engagement, and the Mechanisms Through Which Support Promotes Persistence

Recent research has increasingly emphasized the role of belonging—students' subjective sense that they are valued members of their educational communities—as a critical mechanism through which institutional support and engagement promote persistence and success17,27. This emphasis on belonging appears to bridge between Tinto's theoretical framework emphasizing integration and more recent psychological research on belongingness and its relationship to motivation, achievement, and persistence. A large-scale longitudinal study based on survey data from more than 21,000 undergraduates at two-year and four-year institutions reveals that even small gains in students' sense of belonging correlate with substantial increases in the likelihood of four-year degree completion17. Specifically, students whose sense of belonging increased by one step on a five-point scale between their first and second year were 3.4 percentage points more likely to graduate within four years, with effects persisting and remaining substantial when examined over six-year timeframes17. Notably, the magnitude of the effect of belonging on persistence rivals the effect of thousands of dollars in additional financial aid, suggesting that institutional efforts to promote belonging may represent a cost-effective approach to improving retention outcomes17. Furthermore, the observation that intentional support structures—carefully designed first-year seminars, removing logistical barriers to course registration, peer mentoring—can foster belonging with relatively modest resource requirements suggests that institutions of all resource levels can invest in belonging-promoting interventions17.

The mechanisms through which belonging influences persistence appear multifaceted and operate through both motivational and behavioral channels27. Students who perceive themselves as valued members of their academic communities experience heightened motivation to persist, reduced likelihood of responding to challenges by withdrawing, and increased willingness to help-seeking when needed1. Furthermore, students with strong senses of belonging appear more likely to engage in the very activities—forming study groups, seeking academic support, participating in campus organizations—that research identifies as associated with learning and retention1. In this way, belonging functions both as a direct predictor of persistence and as a mechanism promoting the engagement and integration processes that Tinto identifies as central to retention. Students' sense of belonging appears constructed through three primary dimensions identified by recent qualitative research: sense of familiarity encompassing familiar elements in institutional places and culture; sense of recognition wherein students perceive themselves recognized by peers and community members; and sense of relevance wherein students find their studies meaningful and interesting27. These three dimensions suggest that institutional efforts to promote belonging should attend to making campus environments welcoming and navigable, creating opportunities for recognition and validation from peers and faculty, and helping students understand the relevance and significance of their educational experiences.

The relationship between institutional support, engagement, and belonging appears particularly important for understanding persistence among students from historically marginalized populations who may arrive at college with reduced institutional trust or greater psychological distance from academic communities17. While the effects of belonging on graduation rates appear broadly consistent across demographic groups, some important differential effects have been documented. The link between belonging and four-year graduation rates was stronger for students whose parents attended college than for first-generation students, a pattern the research attributes to first-generation students being more likely to face structural and psychological challenges that may weaken the benefits of belonging17. Similarly, belonging showed a weaker connection to six-year graduation rates for Asian students compared to non-Asian students, partially attributable to Asian students possessing alternative support structures promoting academic persistence17. These differential effects highlight that while belonging represents a powerful and universal influence on student persistence, its effects operate within broader contexts of educational advantage and disadvantage, suggesting that institutions must work to create belonging while simultaneously addressing the structural barriers and disadvantages that marginalized students encounter17.

Identifying Gaps and Tensions in Current Understanding of Student Persistence and Success

While the three major theoretical frameworks discussed above—Tinto's integration theory, Yosso's community cultural wealth, and Kuh's high-impact practices—have substantially advanced scholarly understanding of student persistence and institutional practice, important tensions and gaps remain in the literature that warrant attention from future research and practice. First, while these frameworks emphasize complementary aspects of student success, they have rarely been integrated into comprehensive theoretical models that specify how student characteristics, institutional practices, peer support networks, belonging, and engagement interact to influence persistence and learning1,2,3. Most institutional practice adopts elements from each framework in somewhat disconnected ways—implementing high-impact practices, supporting student engagement, and providing support services without necessarily theorizing how these pieces fit together or optimizing their integration3,5. Research integrating these frameworks into comprehensive models of how institutional context, student characteristics and capital, peer networks, and institutional practices combine to influence outcomes would substantially advance both theoretical understanding and institutional practice.

Second, while recent scholarship has increasingly recognized the importance of informal learning and peer-based support systems, limited research has systematically examined how institutions can formalize and support informal learning and peer networks without destroying the accessibility and authenticity that make informal systems valuable9,13,26. Many institutions have attempted to professionalize peer support through training programs and structured protocols, which may improve consistency and quality but potentially reduces the accessibility and authenticity of peer support. Conversely, entirely informal peer support systems reach inconsistent populations and may reinforce existing social inequalities rather than mitigating them. Understanding how institutions can design "structured informality"—systems that maintain the accessibility and peer-based character of informal learning while simultaneously ensuring equitable access and quality—remains an important frontier for research and practice9,18.

Third, while research demonstrates that high-impact practices generate substantial learning and retention effects, and that these effects prove particularly pronounced for students with lower entering academic preparation, disparities in access to high-impact practices remain pronounced and appear to be widening30. Overcoming barriers to universal participation in high-impact practices requires resources and coordinated effort, and questions remain about the most effective institutional strategies for expanding access. Research examining how different institutional approaches to expanding high-impact practice participation affect outcomes, cost-effectiveness, and equity would substantially inform institutional practice30,30.

Fourth, while the importance of institutional support services is widely acknowledged, research examining the actual impacts of different institutional support structures on student outcomes remains limited, particularly for specific student populations and specific types of support23,26. Many institutions offer a wide array of support services—tutoring, mentoring, advising, coaching—yet limited research has systematically compared the relative effectiveness of these different approaches or identified which types of support prove most beneficial for which student populations under which conditions23. Such research would enable institutions to allocate limited resources more strategically and to improve support effectiveness23.

Finally, while recent research has highlighted the importance of belonging and the psychological dimensions of persistence, research examining how different institutional structures, cultures, and practices shape students' subjective experiences of belonging remains relatively limited17,27. Understanding not merely that belonging matters but how institutions can deliberately create conditions and cultures that promote belonging represents an important direction for future research, particularly given that many institutional initiatives addressing belonging remain relatively new and not yet systematically evaluated.

Conclusion: Toward Integrated Understanding of Student Success Through Formal and Informal Support Systems

The three major theoretical frameworks examined in this review—Tinto's student integration theory, Yosso's community cultural wealth, and Kuh's high-impact practices—have substantially advanced understanding of student persistence and institutional practice in higher education, yet neither alone nor in their current disconnected combination do they fully capture the complexity of how institutions promote student success. The evidence synthesized in this review suggests that student persistence emerges from complex interactions among student characteristics and resources (including the multiple forms of community cultural wealth that Yosso identifies), institutional structures and practices (including the high-impact practices that Kuh identifies), and the informal networks and support systems—what this review has termed institutional fallback systems—through which students navigate college and construct meaning from their experiences. Institutions that successfully promote equitable student success appear to implement comprehensive approaches combining intentionally designed formal support services, widely accessible high-impact practices, recognition and support for students' cultural assets and capabilities, and intentional efforts to promote belonging and integration. Furthermore, the most effective institutions appear to recognize and value the informal peer networks and learning communities that students construct, seeking to support and extend these informal systems rather than simply replacing them with professionalized alternatives.

The evidence further suggests that particular attention to the experiences and needs of first-generation students, students from historically marginalized racial and ethnic groups, students with disabilities, and other populations experiencing educational disadvantage remains critically important. While the frameworks and practices discussed in this review offer potential pathways to more equitable student success, these populations continue to experience substantial disparities in persistence and completion relative to more privileged peers17,31. Closing these gaps will require that institutions move beyond one-size-fits-all approaches to support, instead deliberately designing both formal support systems and informal community-building opportunities that recognize and value the particular strengths and address the particular challenges that diverse student populations bring to college.

Future research should prioritize several key directions. First, research integrating Tinto, Yosso, and Kuh's frameworks into comprehensive theoretical models specifying how student characteristics, institutional practices, and peer networks interact to influence outcomes would advance understanding substantially. Second, research examining the effectiveness of different approaches to supporting and expanding informal learning and peer-based support systems would inform institutional practice. Third, large-scale empirical studies examining the relative effectiveness of different institutional support structures on student outcomes, disaggregated by student characteristics and support types, would enable more strategic allocation of institutional resources. Fourth, qualitative research examining students' subjective experiences of belonging, integration, and support would complement quantitative investigations and provide insights into the mechanisms through which institutional practices influence psychological and developmental outcomes. Finally, longitudinal research examining how students' use of informal support networks, formal services, and engagement in high-impact practices changes over time and how changes in these behaviors predict long-term outcomes would advance understanding of causal mechanisms. As higher education institutions face persistent pressures to improve completion and equity, leveraging the full potential of the theoretical frameworks and practical approaches discussed in this review—while simultaneously addressing the gaps and limitations that remain—offers important opportunities to better support all students in achieving their educational aspirations.
