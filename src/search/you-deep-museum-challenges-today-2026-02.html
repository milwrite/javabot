Museums at a Critical Juncture: Navigating Funding Crises and Artificial Intelligence Transformation in 2026

Museums across the globe face an unprecedented confluence of challenges that threaten their operational viability and mission effectiveness. A comprehensive analysis of recent surveys and industry research reveals that American and international museums are simultaneously confronting severe funding constraints while attempting to harness artificial intelligence technologies to enhance visitor experiences and operational efficiency. The American Alliance of Museums' 2025 National Snapshot of United States Museums indicates that over half of American museums (55 percent) have not yet returned to pre-pandemic attendance levels, with attendance and financial performance "trending in the wrong direction for the first time since the pandemic."1,2 Compounding these attendance challenges, one-third of museums have suffered the cancellation of government grants or contracts, with only 8 percent reporting that lost federal funding has been fully replaced by private sources.1 Simultaneously, the museum sector is grappling with the integration of artificial intelligence, with over 70 percent of museums planning to incorporate AI and augmented reality technologies into visitor experiences by 2026.37 This report synthesizes research from blog posts, academic articles, and industry surveys to provide detailed analysis of how museums navigate funding pressures while simultaneously adopting transformative technologies, with particular attention to the ethical considerations, implementation barriers, and strategic opportunities these dual challenges present.

The Multifaceted Funding Crisis Confronting Modern Museums

The Structural Collapse of Public Funding

The decline of public funding for museums represents one of the most significant structural transformations in cultural institutions over the past two decades. A global study published by the International Research Alliance on Public Funding for Museums (IRAPFM) provides compelling evidence that declining public funding for museums is not merely an American phenomenon but rather a worldwide trend with substantial implications for the entire museum sector.15,27 The research documents that government funding as a proportion of museum income has experienced a dramatic decline, dropping from 40 percent in 1998 to under 24 percent by 2010, with further erosion continuing through 2026.5,7 This shift represents more than a simple reduction in available dollars; it fundamentally restructures the financial ecosystem upon which museums depend, forcing institutions to develop new revenue models and seek alternative funding sources to maintain operations.

In the United States specifically, the impact has been particularly acute following recent federal policy changes. The 2025 American Alliance of Museums survey documents that the second Trump administration's cuts to federal arts funding have created widespread difficulty for the country's museums.1 The cancellation of grants from the Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS), the National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH), and the National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) has left hundreds of institutions with sudden financial gaps.1,2 One particularly troubling statistic reveals that 7 percent of museums had already spent money they were promised by government grants or contracts and are now unable to get reimbursed.1 This situation creates not merely a loss of anticipated funding but actual financial liability for institutions that operated in good faith based on committed federal support.

The ripple effects of federal funding cuts extend far beyond the museums themselves, affecting broader economic ecosystems. The report notes that the economic impact of lost federal support extends far beyond museum walls, affecting architects, construction firms, engineers, and design professionals who partner with cultural institutions.2 This demonstrates how museum funding challenges create cascading consequences throughout local and regional economies, underscoring the interconnected nature of cultural institutions within broader community infrastructure.

The Challenge of Declining Attendance and Earned Revenue

Parallel to the decline in government funding, museums are confronting a troubling reversal in post-pandemic recovery patterns. The most recent attendance data reveals that only 45 percent of responding museums are currently matching 2019 attendance numbers, down from 51 percent in the previous year's survey.1 This represents not merely slower recovery but actual deterioration from earlier progress. Even more concerning, only half of museums (52 percent) report stronger financial performance in 2024 than before the pandemic, a decline from 57 percent in the previous year.2 These figures indicate that the psychological and behavioral changes induced by the pandemic have not fully reversed, with visitors establishing new patterns of cultural engagement that do not necessarily prioritize museum visits.

The decline in attendance has direct implications for earned revenue, which constitutes approximately 32 percent of average museum income.7 With attendance remaining below pre-pandemic levels, many institutions lack the visitor volume to generate sufficient revenue through ticket sales, membership fees, and facility rentals. This creates a particularly precarious situation for museums that relied on earned income as a stabilizing force during periods of declining government funding, as this alternative revenue stream itself has become unreliable.

Analysis of visitor behavior reveals that the problem extends beyond overall attendance numbers to encompass changes in visitation frequency. Research from the American Alliance of Museums' Annual Survey of Museum-Goers indicates that repeat visitation from the most frequent museum-goers has not returned to pre-pandemic levels.43 While the proportion of adults visiting any museum in a given year has recovered to approximately pre-pandemic levels (33 percent in 2025, consistent with the 25-31 percent range observed before the pandemic), the frequency of visits among those who do visit has declined meaningfully.43 This suggests that museums are attracting visitors but losing the loyal repeat patrons who historically sustained operations through frequent visitation and membership.

The Philanthropic Funding Landscape and Its Volatility

Charitable giving, which comprises approximately 30 percent of average museum income, has become subject to unprecedented political and ideological pressures.10,26 The research documents that a chilling effect is actively undermining corporate and foundation support for certain categories of programming. Museums that have historically relied on corporate sponsorships report that corporations are declining to support programs they would have naturally funded in the past, citing concerns about political and ideological controversy.4 This represents not merely a reduction in available philanthropic funds but a fundamental reshaping of which institutional priorities receive support based on evolving corporate risk assessments and political considerations.

Foundation funding itself faces direct threats. According to analysis from the American Alliance of Museums, 346 foundations collectively holding $900 billion in assets and accounting for over half of all grant dollars awarded are falling under parameters that create legal vulnerability and chilling effects regarding support for diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives.10,30 The explicit threat of civil litigation against foundations supporting what the administration characterizes as "illegal DEI" creates profound uncertainty that extends beyond institutions directly targeted by policy to affect the entire philanthropic ecosystem that has traditionally supported museums.

However, not all philanthropic responses follow patterns of retreat. The research documents that some individuals are stepping in to fill gaps left by cancelled government grants, though these examples remain episodic rather than systematic.10,30 When the Japanese American National Museum lost critical funding from the NEH for a continuing education program for teachers, an anonymous donor contributed $85,000, leading to a successful $170,000 appeal that saved the program.10 Similarly, pastors of Black churches have been rallying their congregations to support the National Museum of African American History and Culture in response to attacks on the institution.10 These examples demonstrate that public understanding of museums' societal value remains strong, even as institutional support mechanisms deteriorate.

Revenue Diversification and Its Limitations

In response to declining government funding and the unreliability of traditional philanthropic sources, museums have aggressively pursued revenue diversification strategies. The American Alliance of Museums survey documents that over 55 percent of museums have raised prices on general admission, special events, or in other areas to compensate for funding losses.1 Additionally, 55 percent of respondents have begun seeking corporate sponsorships, while others are developing new models of support.1 These efforts, while necessary, create their own vulnerabilities and ethical complications.

The increased reliance on corporate sponsorship introduces substantial risks related to public perception and institutional integrity. The research extensively documents examples of corporations withdrawing sponsorship support due to public backlash regarding their business practices or values alignment with museums' missions.1,5 Museums have witnessed what researchers term the "Nan Goldin effect," named for the successful campaign led by Nan Goldin and Sackler P.A.I.N. against institutions taking money derived from the sale of deadly opioids.1 Similarly, climate activists like Just Stop Oil have targeted museums for accepting funds from fossil fuel companies, physically disrupting exhibitions and generating negative publicity that extends far beyond the immediate incident.1 These scenarios demonstrate that corporate sponsorship, while financially necessary, carries reputational risks that can actually undermine long-term financial and community relationships.

The research also documents that some museums have been forced to shutter entirely due to unsustainable financial pressures. San Francisco's California Historical Society, the Center for Italian Modern Art in New York, and the Bellevue Arts Museum in Washington have all closed.1 These closures represent not merely individual institutional failures but losses of community cultural assets and irreplaceable collections management expertise.

Rural and Small Museum Vulnerabilities

Rural and small museums face particularly acute funding challenges that differ systematically from those confronting larger urban institutions. These institutions typically rely on a patchwork of funding sources including limited grant opportunities, small donor bases drawn from local communities with limited financial capacity, and minimal earned income from lower visitor numbers.32 Unlike larger urban museums with substantial endowments, board connections to wealthy donors, and access to foundation funding expertise, rural museums often lack the administrative capacity to navigate complex grant applications or develop sophisticated fundraising campaigns.32

The research identifies funding as "arguably the most significant and persistent challenge" facing rural museums, with limited grant opportunities, small donor bases, and lack of earned income creating a cycle of financial precarity.32 Rural museums typically manage their collections through prioritization approaches that concentrate professional, archival-quality conservation care on "core" collections while allowing less critical items to receive only basic maintenance to prevent further deterioration.32 This approach represents a painful compromise between institutional mission and financial reality, where institutions must essentially triage their collections based on resources rather than curatorial assessment of significance.

The Integration of Artificial Intelligence: Opportunities and Ethical Challenges

Current State of AI Adoption in Museums

The museum sector is actively exploring artificial intelligence applications across multiple operational domains, though adoption remains uneven and primarily experimental. Recent research indicates that 72 percent of museums are actively discussing possible AI uses, while 33 percent are using AI daily, and 40 percent are still exploring potential applications without yet seeing concrete benefits.40 Among prominent European museums, 42 percent have adopted AI in some operations, indicating that adoption is occurring but remains incomplete.40 This distribution suggests that AI integration in museums is transitioning from early exploration to broader implementation, though many institutions remain in assessment phases.

The most common museum AI applications currently being deployed involve relatively bounded, operational uses rather than fundamental institutional transformation. The research documents that museums are using AI for automated transcriptions, multilingual audio guides, personalized content recommendations, cataloging artwork, detecting patterns in visitor movement, and monitoring the condition of fragile pieces.40 A recent study introducing a model combining reinforcement learning, computer vision, and affective computing to optimize exhibition layouts demonstrated that optimized layouts improved spatial flow by 18.1 percent, reduced congestion, and increased exhibit visit rates by approximately 50 percent.40 These applications demonstrate concrete, measurable benefits that justify institutional investment.

The High Museum of Art in Atlanta, operating from a philosophical position articulated by the director, frames the core challenge of AI integration not as technological adoption but rather as redefining museums' purpose in a world where AI advances human capability.3 This perspective suggests that the fundamental museum question is not "how do we implement AI?" but rather "what should museums become as AI reshapes the nature of human work and leisure?" This philosophical reframing acknowledges that AI adoption in museums must ultimately serve institutional mission rather than technology adoption for its own sake.

AI Applications Enhancing Accessibility and Visitor Experience

One of the most compelling applications of AI in museums involves enhancing accessibility for visitors with disabilities and creating more inclusive experiences. Research documents that AI-powered accessibility tools such as text-to-speech systems for visually impaired visitors and haptic technologies allowing visitors to "feel" digital representations of artifacts are expanding inclusive access.6 Audio descriptions for visually impaired visitors generated through AI, real-time sign language translation for Deaf or hard-of-hearing audiences, and 3D-printed forms of artwork allowing tactile engagement represent concrete ways that AI addresses longstanding accessibility barriers.6 The research notes that "AI has begun reducing and resolving long-time access barriers for visitors with disabilities" through its many creative applications.6

Multilingual support represents another substantial accessibility application. AI-powered translation enables real-time translation of exhibition descriptions, educational materials, and guided tours in visitors' preferred languages, making cultural experiences accessible to international and multilingual visitors without requiring institutions to employ multilingual staff.6 The National Museum of Singapore has successfully integrated augmented reality experiences into exhibits, allowing visitors to see historical scenes recreated in real-time as they walk through galleries, demonstrating how AI-enabled immersive technologies can enhance visitor engagement while accommodating diverse learning styles.37

Personalization of visitor experiences through AI represents another promising application domain. Invisible AI systems can analyze visitor behavior in real-time, offering tailored recommendations for exhibits, workshops, or café stops based on individual preferences, while dynamically adapting to offer alternative routes if galleries become crowded or suggesting breaks when visitors appear fatigued.20,37 This creates what researchers term "Invisible Experiences"—frictionless interactions with technology where systems anticipate and respond to user needs intuitively without obtrusive screens or cumbersome gadgets.20,37

AI Supporting Curatorial and Conservation Functions

Beyond visitor-facing applications, AI is demonstrating significant potential for supporting essential museum functions related to collections management, conservation, and scholarly research. Natural language processing and data science enable museums to identify derogatory language and bias in digital collections, allowing institutions to retroactively contextualize collections databases and promote more equitable access.41 The research documents that the Harvard University Herbaria have used data sciences to identify problematic terminology in collections, demonstrating how AI can support institutions in addressing historical biases embedded in collection descriptions.41 This application represents a concrete way that AI can help museums fulfill their stewardship obligations while supporting more inclusive access to cultural heritage.

AI-powered systems can assist museum conservators by analyzing artifacts and their environmental conditions to generate condition reports, identify risk factors, and suggest preventive conservation strategies optimizing collections care while reducing emergency preservation costs.6 By monitoring environmental conditions of collections objects to detect threats and adjusting climate controls for optimal temperature, humidity, and light levels, AI systems can prevent deterioration of irreplaceable cultural materials.6 This preventive conservation approach represents a particularly valuable application for resource-constrained museums that lack capacity for constant human monitoring of collection environments.

Machine learning algorithms can identify patterns in museum collections that humans might overlook, enabling curators to discover new connections between objects and deepen scholarly understanding. The research documents that MoMA's Digital Media team collaborated with Google Arts & Culture Lab to introduce a machine learning algorithm that combs through over 30,000 exhibition photos to identify matches with more than 65,000 works in the museum's online collection, creating a vast network of new links between exhibition history and online collections.19 This type of application demonstrates how AI can augment rather than replace human expertise, providing curators and researchers with tools for discovering previously invisible patterns within institutional collections.

Ethical Frameworks and Responsible AI Implementation

Despite the clear potential benefits of AI integration, the research identifies substantial ethical concerns that must be addressed through responsible implementation frameworks. A survey at the American Alliance of Museums' Future of Museums Summit revealed that 60 percent of poll respondents cited ethical concerns as their biggest concern about implementing AI at their museums.13 These concerns encompass data privacy, algorithmic bias, transparency regarding AI decision-making, potential job displacement, and the risk that AI systems might undermine institutional values or harm vulnerable populations.

The research extensively documents the profound importance of data privacy in AI implementation. Museums typically collect personal data on patrons including members and donors, and some track visitor movements using apps while keeping anonymized data for decision-making.16 The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the European Union has prompted some American museums to consider visitor data privacy more systematically, though most museums have yet to address privacy issues holistically.16 The British Museum's recent encounter with visitor data privacy concerns related to its AI analytics tools demonstrates that even well-resourced institutions can face public backlash regarding data collection and use.20,37

Algorithmic bias represents another critical ethical concern that demands systematic attention. The research documents that machines can replicate the same poor judgments about people of color as humans because algorithms are built without scrutinizing engineers' inherent biases.16 This manifestation of bias is particularly concerning for museums planning to use machine vision to analyze images, as the same faults that create problems with facial recognition photography can create problems when analyzing museum objects with people depicted.16 The research emphasizes that "we cannot code our way out of racism" and that addressing bias requires museums to "talk to the people affected and understand their concerns."16 This principle underscores that responsible AI implementation requires human engagement with affected communities rather than purely technical solutions.

The research identifies transparency about AI use as a fundamental requirement for maintaining public trust. Institutions must clearly communicate how AI tools were built, what data is used to train them, and how they make decisions.13 Labeling AI-generated content to ensure it is not confused with human-generated content, documenting how AI content was produced, and explaining human involvement in reviewing AI outputs are essential practices for responsible AI deployment.13 This transparency serves not merely as a compliance obligation but as a mechanism for building stakeholder trust and ensuring that visitors can engage with AI-enhanced experiences without privacy concerns.

Several museums have developed formal ethics frameworks to guide AI implementation. The research documents that approximately 87 percent of museum practitioners surveyed believed AI applications could enhance accessibility and appeal to diverse audiences, indicating broad recognition of AI's potential when implemented ethically.40 However, achieving this potential requires that institutions develop what researchers term "ethical AI statements"—public-facing documents that clearly outline institutional approaches to AI implementation, principles guiding AI applications, and safeguards used to reduce potential risks such as bias or privacy concerns.13 These statements serve both as internal guidance when deciding which solutions to implement and as public commitments building trust with stakeholders including donors, staff, visitors, and the broader community.13

Implementation Barriers and Resource Constraints

Despite the clear benefits AI can provide, substantial barriers prevent many museums from realizing these opportunities. The most frequently cited challenge involves financial constraints, with 57 percent of museum professionals reporting that limited financial resources represent a key obstacle to adopting new technologies.8 Initial costs for AI implementation, combined with ongoing maintenance and infrastructure upgrade requirements, prove particularly burdensome for smaller institutions lacking established technology budgets.8 This reality creates a troubling bifurcation in the museum sector where well-resourced institutions can invest in AI-enhanced visitor experiences and operational efficiency while smaller institutions fall further behind.

Lack of technical expertise represents another critical barrier preventing AI adoption. Many institutions report they simply do not have in-house capabilities to implement or maintain advanced digital tools, a challenge especially evident in smaller institutions lacking dedicated IT departments or staff with specialized training in digital tools.8 The research documents that small and medium-size institutions lack staffing to tackle digital planning and development, with professional development opportunities and national conferences remaining difficult for professionals from smaller institutions to access, further exacerbating digital capacity gaps within the sector.25

Institutional inertia and resistance to technological change constitute additional implementation barriers. The research documents that about 33 percent of museum professionals felt that introducing advanced technologies might detract from the authenticity of cultural experiences or undermine the physical connection to artifacts that many visitors still value.8 This represents not mere technophobia but legitimate concern regarding whether technology integration might compromise core museum functions related to direct engagement with original cultural objects. Some museum professionals express worry that AI could "distract" from core experiences, overcomplicate simple pleasures, or create barriers between visitors and art itself.40

The Intersection of Funding Constraints and Technology Adoption: Compounded Challenges

The Paradox of Needing Investment to Reduce Costs

Museums confront a fundamental paradox regarding AI and other digital technologies: institutions need significant capital investment to adopt technologies that would potentially reduce long-term operational costs. For cash-strapped institutions losing federal funding and experiencing declining attendance, the upfront investment required for AI systems represents a prohibitive barrier even though the systems would ultimately enhance operational efficiency and visitor engagement. A museum director facing the prospect of cutting programming and reducing opening hours due to funding losses has limited capacity to justify substantial technology expenditures, even when these investments would theoretically improve institutional resilience.

This paradox manifests particularly acutely regarding staffing decisions. The research notes that AI can automate tasks previously performed by museum staff, raising concerns about job displacement.14 However, current AI technology functions best as a complement to human expertise, requiring employee oversight to verify outputs, meaning AI serves more as a "helper" than a "replacer" within museum ecosystems.14 This reality means that many institutions cannot achieve the cost savings through AI automation that would theoretically justify the technology investment. Instead, museums adopting AI frequently find themselves needing to maintain existing staff while adding new technological capabilities, expanding rather than reducing operational costs.

Digital Strategy Development as Resource Drain

The research documents that development of coherent digital strategies represents a significant resource commitment that many institutions struggle to sustain. A study of museum digital readiness found that only 25 percent of museums have a broadly shared digital strategy or incorporate one into their overall strategic plan, with 31 percent admitting they had no digital strategy while another 29 percent said theirs was in development.25 This pattern reflects not lack of recognition regarding digital importance but rather competing resource demands. Many museums use digital strategy documents as tools to drive organizational change and secure internal funding resources rather than as components of comprehensive institutional strategic planning.25 This instrumental use of digital strategy suggests that many institutions approach digital transformation tactically rather than strategically.

Project management represents another critical gap undermining digital transformation efforts. The research indicates that nearly one-third of all museums report little to no formalized project management practices or project manager roles, with another 40 percent noting that while their organization has project management, it is unevenly applied across projects.25 This lack of systematic project management creates conditions where digital initiatives proceed without clear goals, measurable outcomes, or coordination across departments, reducing the likelihood of successful implementation and return on investment.

The Funding-Technology Dependency Loop

Museums experiencing federal funding cuts face a paradoxical situation wherein they need technology investments to maintain competitiveness and enhance operational efficiency while lacking the financial resources to make these investments. Institutions that successfully adopt AI and digital tools gain competitive advantages in attracting visitors, improving operational efficiency, and securing philanthropic support from donors impressed by institutional innovation. Meanwhile, institutions lacking resources to invest in technology find themselves in deteriorating competitive positions, making it increasingly difficult to reverse financial declines.

The research suggests potential approaches for breaking this dependency loop. Museums globally are experimenting with shared services models wherein multiple institutions collaborate to access professional expertise and reduce operational costs.33 By consolidating duplicated operational efforts, museums can become more efficient and proficient, with the concept echoing shared-services models that many for-profit industries use to control costs.33 Cross-institutional partnerships bring increased efficiency and expanded capability without risking loss of institutional integrity, particularly when partnerships focus on back-office or front-of-house departments that do not deal with proprietary assets.33 This collaborative approach could potentially enable smaller institutions to access AI and digital capabilities that individual institutions could not justify independently.

Strategic Responses and Emerging Solutions

Business Model Innovation and Revenue Diversification

Beyond cost-cutting and operational efficiency, some museums are pursuing more fundamental business model transformations that position them for long-term sustainability. The research documents cases of museums that have successfully transformed business models in response to financial crisis by fundamentally reorienting institutional priorities toward community engagement and co-creation. The Santa Cruz Museum of Art & History exemplifies this approach, having experienced a loss of community relevance and a drastic halving of revenue income during the 2008 financial crisis.50 The museum transformed by implementing a community-driven business model where co-creation and community-led use of museum assets become central, resulting in visitor numbers tripling and income improving hugely.50

The research documents diverse revenue diversification strategies museums are experimenting with, including membership programs contributing over $350 million annually to museum revenues in the United States alone.45 However, membership success requires more than offering traditional perks; museums must develop compelling value propositions and personalized engagement strategies. The Metropolitan Museum of Art offers tiered membership levels allowing members to choose packages fitting their needs and interests, while SFMOMA excels in building member loyalty through exclusive curator-led tours, personalized event invitations, and opportunities to contribute to museum initiatives.45

Some museums are experimenting with innovative revenue streams beyond traditional earned income. The research documents that museums are exploring cryptocurrency donations, with the Great Lakes Science Center becoming the first major museum to accept bitcoin in 2018.53 The Toledo Museum of Art has incubated a business called the Center of Visual Expertise expanding visual literacy training into industrial operations.53 These entrepreneurial approaches demonstrate how museums are applying start-up philosophy and business model innovation to address financial pressures.

Institutional Collaboration and Resource Sharing

The research extensively documents that institutional collaboration represents a promising approach for addressing both funding and technology challenges. The Art Fund's National Art Pass program demonstrates how museums can collaborate to enhance member value and attract new audiences, offering discounts at 250 museums across the UK for subscribers with proceeds supporting the Art Fund's grant programs.36 Museums are also working collaboratively on conservation and storage, suggesting that sharing collections through loans and even joint ownership represents a logical next step in collaboration.36

The research documents that consortiums of museums are successfully reducing costs without reducing programming or staffing by identifying areas where they can work together. For example, a consortium of Midwest institutions is currently examining areas where they can work together to cut costs, recognizing that institutions often organize art travel for patrons independently but could share itineraries or run trips together.36 These collaboration models suggest that museums need not address funding and technology challenges purely through individual institutional action but can leverage collective resources and expertise.

The Role of AI in Enabling Strategic Sustainability

Paradoxically, AI and digital technologies may provide solutions to challenges they themselves amplify, particularly regarding resource constraints. Affordable AI tools increasingly enable small museums to access capabilities that would previously have required expensive consultants or large development teams. The research documents that "AI is incredibly affordable, accessible, and gives small museums new super powers as compared to needing to hire expensive consultants, firms, etc. to achieve similar results."40 The proliferation of accessible tools such as free ChatGPT plans and paid subscriptions to Claude by Anthropic creates a sense of optimism that more museums will find appropriate tools regardless of size or resources.40

Museums are discovering that AI can enhance operations and visitor experiences without requiring massive technology infrastructure investments. Examples include using AI to automate social media content creation, generate curatorial descriptions, streamline administrative processes, optimize visitor flow, and analyze donor datasets to enhance fundraising strategies.6,14,40 These applications can provide significant operational benefits without requiring the level of technical expertise or infrastructure investment that more sophisticated implementations would demand.

Visitor Experience and Attendance Recovery

Post-Pandemic Behavioral Changes

Understanding why museums continue to struggle with attendance despite recovery of overall visitation incidence provides critical context for strategic decisions regarding technology and programming investment. The research documents that while 33 percent of U.S. adults reported visiting a museum in 2025 (consistent with pre-pandemic norms of 25-31 percent), the critical difference lies in visitation frequency.43 Repeat visitation from the most avid museum visitors has not recovered to pre-pandemic levels, suggesting that while museums are attracting visitors, they are losing the loyal repeat patrons who historically sustained operations.43 Parents and guardians of minor children are the least likely demographic to have returned to pre-pandemic visitation rates, indicating that pandemic-induced behavioral changes have particularly affected family audiences.43

This behavioral change likely reflects new leisure patterns and competing entertainment options that emerged during pandemic lockdowns. The research notes that concerns about inflation, financial instability, and weakened travel and tourism affect museum attendance decisions.4 With cost-of-living pressures increasing and families reassessing discretionary spending, museum visits increasingly compete with other entertainment options rather than representing automatic cultural practices.

Visitor Expectations and Experience Design

Post-pandemic visitor expectations have shifted substantially regarding what museum experiences should provide. The research documents that visitors increasingly expect museums to provide active, instructive, and moving experiences rather than merely displaying artifacts in silence.57 Visitors prioritize safety and comfort, with clean facilities, limited visitor numbers, and contactless services enhancing confidence in museum visits.57 Additionally, visitors increasingly expect special exhibitions and rotating displays, with regularly refreshing exhibits making repeat visitation more likely.57

The research indicates that younger audiences particularly value technology-driven experiences. A significant majority of younger respondents aged 18-34 indicated that interactive, technology-driven experiences such as VR or AR would encourage greater engagement with cultural heritage.8 Museums that understand these shifting expectations can use technology strategically to attract younger audiences while maintaining engagement with traditional visitors who value more static, contemplative experiences.

The Role of Inclusive Programming

Despite political pressures targeting diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives, the research documents strong public support for inclusive museum programming. An analysis of visitor attitudes reveals that the majority of U.S. adults and the majority of museum-goers support and want museums to share inclusive content about women, people of color, religious minorities, and LGBTQ+ people.47 This support has actually increased rather than decreased since 2021, with attitudes among representative U.S. adult samples shifting significantly toward inclusion.47 While the larger segment of anti-inclusive conservatives may drive political rhetoric and initiatives, they do not represent the majority of Americans, and they do not even represent the majority of conservatives.47

This public opinion data provides important context for museums navigating political pressures regarding inclusive programming. The research suggests that "the biggest risk we face is losing our credibility with the public by not telling the truth," and for most people, telling the truth requires inclusive representation of all humanity's experiences and perspectives.47 Museums that maintain commitment to inclusive programming despite political headwinds may actually strengthen their connections with the majority of the public while preserving institutional integrity.

Global Perspectives on Museum Sustainability

International Funding Patterns and Disparities

The IRAPFM research provides global perspective on funding decline, documenting that declining public funding for museums is not merely an American phenomenon but a worldwide trend with significant implications for the entire museum sector.15,27 The research documents that in 37 percent of responding countries, 71 to 100 percent of museums receive the majority of their funding from private sources, indicating a fundamental global shift in museum financial models.21 National museums often receive more stable funding while smaller institutions struggle to compete, creating significant disparities both between countries and between national and smaller-scale museums.15,27

The research documents that European museums face similar or greater funding pressures than American institutions. Berlin has reduced cultural funding by €130 million ($151 million).5 Yet European museums are responding with endowment-building initiatives that may provide models for American institutions. The Louvre became the first French museum to create an endowment fund in Europe in 2009, to the tune of €175 million ($205 million).21 More recently, Tate announced its Future Fund to help solidify its long-term budget, with more than £43 million ($58 million) already raised and a goal to reach £150 million ($202 million) by 2030.21

Private Museum Crises and Market Vulnerabilities

The research documents that private museums, once prestigious symbols of wealth and cultural ambition, face particular crises amid economic slowdown and real estate instability. China's private museum sector exemplifies these vulnerabilities, with multiple high-profile closures documented including the TAG Art Museum in Qingdao, which shut down less than four years after opening.5 Beijing's UCCA, long considered a sector leader, struggles with delayed salaries and canceled programming amid donor fatigue and insufficient tax incentives.5 These international examples demonstrate that funding challenges extend beyond public institutions and affect the entire global museum ecosystem.

The Future Landscape: 2026 and Beyond

Emerging Threats to Institutional Stability

The research documents several emerging threats that compound funding and technology challenges facing museums in 2026 and beyond. Museum directors identify shifts in philanthropy as the factor most likely to cause disruption in 2026, with 63 percent citing this concern, up from 48 percent in the previous year.1 Over half of museums (53 percent) are concerned about inflation, while 52 percent cite potential risks of financial instability.1 These forward-looking concerns suggest that museum leaders anticipate continued deterioration in the operating environment.

The research also documents emerging threats to nonprofit status itself, a fundamental foundation upon which American museums operate. The analysis identifies potential policy shifts that could restrict the ability to qualify for nonprofit status or erode the financial benefits of being a nonprofit.29,52 While these threats remain prospective rather than fully realized, they underscore the precarious legal and political environment in which museums operate.
