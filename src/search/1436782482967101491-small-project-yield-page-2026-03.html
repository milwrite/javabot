The Politics of Writing: Chinese Characters, Diaspora, and the Making of Modern Chinese Identity

This comprehensive report examines the profound connections between systems of Chinese characters—simplified and traditional—and the political, cultural, and historical experiences of Chinese diaspora communities spanning Southeast Asia, South Asia, and the trans-Pacific world. The standardization of simplified characters by the People's Republic of China in the 1950s represented far more than a technical reform aimed at increasing literacy; it embodied a fundamentally reimagined vision of Chinese modernity and national identity rooted in revolutionary ideologies1,2. Meanwhile, the continued use of traditional characters by diaspora communities from Southeast Asia to the Caribbean has become a crucial marker of cultural identity and political resistance, reflecting divergent understandings of what it means to be Chinese in an era of global migration, decolonization, and competing national projects. By tracing how different communities have adopted, preserved, resisted, or transformed systems of writing Chinese characters, this report reveals how linguistic practices constitute lived engagement with historical processes of modernization, colonialism, transnational movement, and identity formation. The character system itself becomes a text through which we can read the political stakes of cultural nationalism, the experiences of diaspora, the complications of heritage language maintenance across generations, and the ongoing negotiations of belonging among ethnically Chinese populations worldwide.

The Historical Evolution and Political Standardization of Chinese Characters

The transformation of Chinese character writing systems in the twentieth century cannot be separated from broader processes of national modernization and state formation. Before the deliberate simplification campaigns of the mid-twentieth century, the evolution of Chinese characters had followed a lengthy historical trajectory marked by gradual stylistic and structural changes spanning millennia. According to Chinese palaeographer Qiu Xigui, "the broadest trend in the evolution of Chinese characters over their history has been simplification, both in graphical shape and in graphical form," indicating that the impulse toward simpler character forms represented a continuation of longstanding historical patterns rather than an unprecedented innovation1. However, the mass standardization of simplified characters during the twentieth century by the People's Republic of China constituted a deliberate, systematic, and politically motivated transformation with implications far beyond mere convenience or legibility.

The conceptual groundwork for character simplification emerged during the early twentieth century as Chinese intellectuals grappled with the question of how modernization could be achieved while confronting what many saw as the impediments posed by the traditional writing system. During the late Qing and early Republican periods, the educator and linguist Lufei Kui formally proposed the use of simplified characters in education as early as 1909, representing the first explicit call for mass simplification1. The subsequent decades witnessed growing intellectual consensus that the country's writing system constituted "a serious impediment to its modernization," particularly during the turbulent period encompassing the 1911 Xinhai Revolution and the 1919 May Fourth Movement1. Leading intellectual figures including Yuen Ren Chao and Hu Shi advanced the radical position that the language should be written with an alphabet rather than characters, viewing romanization as more logical and efficient1. This alphabetization campaign coexisted alongside simplification efforts throughout the Republican era, with many Chinese intelligentsia convinced that character simplification would significantly "increase literacy rates throughout the country"1.

These reformist impulses achieved official institutional expression during the Republican period when the Kuomintang government published the first official list of simplified forms in 1935, consisting of 324 characters collated by Peking University professor Qian Xuantong1. However, fierce opposition within the KMT resulted in the rescission of this list in 1936, indicating that even during the Republican period, character simplification remained a contested and politicized issue1. The establishment of the People's Republic of China in 1949 transformed these earlier reformist dreams into state policy backed by governmental authority and revolutionary legitimacy. Extensive work throughout the 1950s culminated in the promulgation of the 1956 Chinese Character Simplification Scheme, which established 515 simplified characters and 54 simplified components whose simplifications would appear in most compound characters1. This scheme represented not merely a technical linguistic reform but fundamentally embodied the revolutionary state's vision of breaking with the feudal past and constructing a modern, socialist nation capable of rapidly achieving mass literacy and national cohesion2.

The deliberate promotion of simplified characters served multiple interconnected objectives within the revolutionary project of nation-building. At the most immediate level, the simplification of characters through the reduction of strokes and the elimination of complex radical combinations made the written language more accessible to ordinary people, particularly those from peasant and working-class backgrounds who had been systematically excluded from literacy under the imperial and Republican regimes. The literacy achievements following simplification were genuinely remarkable; when the communists took over mainland China, the literacy rate stood below 20 percent, but this figure had risen to 95 percent by subsequent decades, with experts attributing this "considerable achievement" significantly to the simplification of written characters actively promoted by the communist government20. The simplification initiative thus represented a crucial component of the CCP's broader modernization agenda and its claims to have liberated the Chinese people from the constraints of feudal culture.

Beyond the quantitative expansion of literacy, however, the standardization of simplified characters served deeper political purposes relating to national unification and the construction of a unified Chinese identity under communist leadership. The Script Reform Committee engaged in careful deliberation on the characters included in the 1956 scheme, collecting "public input regarding the recognizability of variants, and often approving forms in small batches"1. This participatory rhetoric masked a fundamentally top-down project of cultural standardization wherein the state sought to impose a unified system of written expression across territories inhabited by speakers of mutually unintelligible languages and dialects1. The promotion of simplified characters thus accompanied the parallel promotion of Putonghua (Standard Mandarin) as the national language, creating a comprehensive apparatus through which linguistic diversity could be systematized, regulated, and subordinated to the project of constructing a unified Chinese nation-state2. In this context, the character system itself became a technology of governance through which the revolutionary state could extend its authority into the domain of cultural expression and everyday communication.

The Symbolic and Cultural Significance of Traditional Characters in Diaspora Communities

While the People's Republic pursued the standardization of simplified characters as a tool of national modernization and social transformation, diaspora communities scattered across the globe maintained and continued to develop traditional character systems, transforming them into powerful symbolic resources through which to assert distinct ethnic and cultural identities. Traditional Chinese characters emerged as more than merely an alternative script; they became inscribed with profound political and cultural meanings relating to questions of belonging, authenticity, cultural preservation, and resistance to what many diaspora members perceived as enforced assimilation7. The geographic distribution of character system usage reveals stark divergences reflecting divergent political trajectories and competing visions of Chinese national identity. While simplified characters became the standard forms used in mainland China, Malaysia, and Singapore, traditional characters remained officially used in Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan1,1. This bifurcation of the Chinese-speaking world into simplified-character and traditional-character regions embodied competing political projects and fundamentally different relationships to Chinese cultural heritage.

In Hong Kong and Macau, the retention of traditional characters during the colonial period created an enduring linguistic and cultural distinction between these territories and mainland China7. Following the promulgation of the 1956 Character Simplification Scheme on the mainland, Hong Kong and Macau continued to preserve traditional characters, thereby maintaining a visible textual marker of their separate political status and cultural identity. As the Wikipedia article on traditional Chinese characters notes, traditional characters "were retained during the colonial period, while the mainland adopted simplified characters," creating a linguistic divide that would persist even after Hong Kong's 1997 handover to China7. The continued use of traditional characters in Hong Kong became increasingly freighted with political significance, particularly as anxiety emerged regarding the erosion of Hong Kong's distinct identity within the "one country, two systems" framework designed to preserve its autonomy11. Young Hongkongers in particular came to view the preservation of traditional characters as intrinsically bound up with the defense of their distinctive way of life, democratic values, and cultural autonomy against what many perceived as encroaching mainland influence16.

Taiwan's relationship to traditional characters paralleled but differed from Hong Kong's experience in important ways reflecting the unique trajectory of Taiwan's political history. The Taiwanese government, administered by the Kuomintang (KMT) following the retreat from mainland China in 1949, explicitly established traditional characters as the standard form and actively discouraged the use of simplified characters, which became associated with communism and mainland domination17,49. This transformation of simplified characters into symbols of "Communist" ideology extended to the point where simplified characters were branded in Taiwan as "bandit characters" (匪字), revealing the deeply politicized nature of character choice within the context of cross-strait competition49. The Ministry of Education in Taiwan regulated the official form of traditional characters through the "Standard Form of National Characters," thereby institutionalizing the connection between traditional character usage and Taiwanese national identity7. Even as Taiwan democratized and younger generations increasingly identified as distinctly Taiwanese rather than Chinese, the preference for traditional characters persisted as a taken-for-granted marker of local cultural identity, precisely because character choice had become so thoroughly bound up with the historical experience of Japanese colonialism, postwar KMT rule, and ongoing competition with mainland China17.

Interestingly, while the mainland government officially standardized simplified characters, it maintained a pragmatic awareness that traditional characters retained economic and cultural value, particularly when addressing audiences in Hong Kong, Macau, Taiwan, and overseas Chinese communities. The People's Republic followed a subtle dual-script strategy wherein "mainland companies selling products in Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan use traditional characters in order to communicate with consumers; the inverse is equally true as well"7. This commercial pragmatism reflected a deeper recognition that traditional characters carried affective and commercial resonance within diaspora communities and in non-mainland Chinese-majority territories. Moreover, "in digital media, many cultural phenomena imported from Hong Kong and Taiwan into mainland China, such as music videos, karaoke videos, subtitled movies, and subtitled dramas, use traditional Chinese characters," indicating that traditional characters continued to circulate within mainland popular culture despite official governmental promotion of simplified forms7. This persistent visibility of traditional characters within mainland contexts despite formal standardization suggests that the relationship between character systems and political identity remained more complex and contested than official policies might suggest.

The debate over traditional versus simplified characters became increasingly contentious in the early twenty-first century, with critics of simplification arguing that the transformation of characters had diminished their aesthetic and cultural value. Pro-traditional commentators maintained that "the changes through the history are almost exclusively alterations in writing styles, especially vernacular writing, and not in the fundamental structure of the characters," and alleged that simplified characters "were arbitrarily schematized and then imposed by the PRC on its people with the intention of subverting and eradicating selected elements of traditional Chinese culture"49. These critiques articulated a vision of traditional characters as repositories of historical depth, etymological integrity, and cultural authenticity that simplification supposedly destroyed. Others contended that "traditional characters offer a stronger and richer connection with the history of the Chinese language and traditions," arguing that "the writings of Confucius, Lao Tzu and many others make full use of the wide range and expression of traditional Chinese characters"20. From this perspective, the retention and use of traditional characters became a form of cultural preservation through which diaspora communities and Taiwan could maintain continuity with millennia of Chinese cultural achievement.

Language, Identity, and the Construction of Diaspora Consciousness in Southeast Asia

The profound historical transformations affecting Chinese character systems during the twentieth century unfolded against the background of massive Chinese migration to Southeast Asia, creating complex intersections between writing system standardization, diaspora identity formation, and the negotiation of belonging in postcolonial societies. Chinese migration to Southeast Asia spanned centuries but dramatically intensified during the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, driven by economic opportunity, political instability in southern China, and the expansion of colonial trading networks. Between 1853 and 1879, approximately 14,000 Chinese indentured servants were imported to the British Caribbean as part of a larger system of low-wage labor, while roughly 18,000 Chinese were brought as indentured workers to the British West Indies, primarily to British Guiana, Trinidad, and Jamaica23. Within Southeast Asia proper, Chinese communities established themselves as merchants, laborers, and middlemen throughout the nineteenth century, gradually building networks of institutions, associations, and cultural practices that would sustain Chinese identity across generations15,19.

The early Chinese diaspora communities in Southeast Asia organized themselves primarily around particularistic identities based on dialect, family ties, kinship, and place of origin rather than around a comprehensive vision of unified Chinese nationality. The kongsi, also known as surname associations, represented relatively exclusive organizations that "granted membership on the basis of ostensible familial or kinship relations to individuals with variations of a single surname, who spoke the same dialect, and had migrated from a distinct geographical locale in the Fujian or Guangdong provinces"19. These kinship-based organizations reinforced familial relationships and connected clansmen across the colonies and back home, but maintained parochial identities rooted in specific regional origins. By contrast, huay kuan represented broader organizations structured on ethnolinguistic identities, designed to represent the economic, social, and political interests of entire dialect groups in relation to other associations, colonial authorities, and their counterparts back home19. The predominance of Hokkien and Teochew dialect-based organizations in Malaya and the East Indies reflected the demographic composition of Chinese migrants, while significant minorities speaking Hakka, Cantonese, and Hainanese also maintained their own guilds19. These dialect-based organizations thus institutionalized linguistic pluralism within the Chinese diaspora, with different topolects serving as primary markers of identity and community affiliation.

The character writing system played a crucial though often invisible role within these dialect-based diaspora communities. Communities organized around Cantonese, Hokkien, Hakka, and other languages would have used written forms corresponding to their spoken languages, whether through classical Chinese, regional writing traditions, or emerging vernacular forms. The written expression of Chinese identity within early diaspora communities thus reflected linguistic diversity rather than standardization, mirroring the spoken multilingualism of Chinese migration flows. The historical record remains fragmentary regarding the precise character practices of early diaspora communities, but the existence of dialect-specific schools, newspapers, and institutional structures suggests that written Chinese within diaspora contexts remained tied to specific spoken languages rather than to a unified character system9,21. This early twentieth-century environment of linguistic and orthographic pluralism would be dramatically transformed by the subsequent emergence of Chinese nationalism and the standardization initiatives undertaken by both the Republic of China and the People's Republic.

The pivotal transformation of diaspora consciousness occurred during the early twentieth century when revolutionary activists and Chinese nationalist intellectuals sought to mobilize overseas Chinese communities toward nationalist and modernization goals. Sun Yat-Sen, the founding father of the Republic of China, himself "played a vital role in the spread of Chinese nationalism among the Chinese diaspora from his base of operations in British Malaya where much of the Revolution was planned"19. Through organizations such as the Xing Zhong Hui (Revive China Society) and the Tung Meng Hui (Chinese Revolutionary League), Chinese activists "fostered a distinctly Chinese nationalist consciousness that shifted communities' ties from dialect and place of origin towards a broader imagination of the Chinese nation"19. This nationalist mobilization transformed diaspora identity by promoting Mandarin as a unifying language that could transcend the parochialism of dialect-specific identities. The subsequent establishment of the Republic in 1912 represented a watershed moment whereby "the new Chinese state sought out the newly galvanized Chinese diaspora in Southeast Asia to use their capital, knowledge, and skills for the modernization of China," simultaneously claiming governance over overseas Chinese through the principle of jus sanguinis (right of blood)19.

This shift from dialect-based to nationalist consciousness accompanied significant educational transformation. Chinese consuls sent to Malayan and East Indies colonies served as representatives to "maintain this connection and serve as advocates and protectors of resident Chinese communities," while another crucial tool involved "sending teachers to develop vernacular education programs in Mandarin: remolding the dialect speaking Overseas Communities into ideal Chinese citizens"19. These educational initiatives represented deliberate efforts to displace local dialect usage in favor of a standardized, nationally unified form of Chinese. The establishment of Kuomintang (KMT) branches in Malaya further institutionalized the connection between diaspora communities and the Chinese nation-state, creating organizational structures through which to promote nationalist consciousness and tie overseas Chinese identity to the political project of Republican China19. Within this emerging framework, the choice of character system—whether to use simplified forms emerging on the mainland or to preserve traditional forms—became increasingly tied to political alignment with either the Republic or subsequent developments in Chinese politics.

The Bifurcation of Chinese Diaspora Character Practice Following 1949

The establishment of the People's Republic of China in 1949 and its subsequent promotion of simplified characters created fundamental divisions within diaspora communities regarding proper practice of Chinese identity through written forms. The postwar period witnessed increasingly divergent trajectories for Southeast Asian Chinese communities relative to mainland China. Those who "fled during 1912–1949 and settled down in Singapore and Malaysia automatically gained citizenship in 1957 and 1963 as these countries gained independence," transforming their legal and political status12. This shift toward citizenship in new nation-states meant that Chinese diaspora communities increasingly had to negotiate their relationship to Chinese cultural nationalism and the Chinese nation-state through the lens of their obligations as citizens of Malaysia, Singapore, and other postcolonial nations. The adoption of simplified versus traditional characters thus became intertwined with questions about whether diaspora communities should align themselves with the PRC's modernization project or maintain cultural continuity with pre-simplification traditions.

The Malayan Chinese communities experienced particular pressure to standardize on Mandarin and adopt corresponding character systems as the basis for educational and institutional life. Whereas "the Chinese American community was overwhelmingly Cantonese speaking" through the early twentieth century, with "the majority of Chinese schools in the U.S. taught Cantonese," this pattern shifted dramatically after World War II9. "Mandarin gradually superseded Cantonese as the predominant language of instruction in Chinese schools," reaching the point where "by the mid-1990s, about eight out of ten pupils in Chinese schools were taught in Mandarin; Cantonese remained the language of instruction for only one of every eight students"9. This dramatic linguistic shift represented far more than a merely technical change in language instruction; it reflected the displacement of earlier dialect-based identities in favor of a nationally unified form of Chinese consciousness. The accompanying shift toward simplified characters in some diaspora contexts and the reinforcement of traditional characters in others thus embodied these deeper transformations in diaspora identity formation.

Singapore's approach to Chinese language and character standardization differed in important respects from Malaysia and overseas Chinese communities. Following independence in 1965, "English along with other three mother tongues (Malay, Mandarin and Tamil) were proclaimed under Article 153A of the Singapore Constitution as the four official languages of the nation"28. Singapore adopted simplified characters following a 1969 governmental decision to officially adopt them, yet "traditional characters still are widely used in contexts such as in baby and corporation names, advertisements, decorations, official documents and in newspapers"7. This pragmatic coexistence of simplified and traditional characters within Singapore's multilingual and multiscriptal landscape reflected the city-state's position as a crucial node within regional trade networks where both simplified-character mainland commerce and traditional-character diasporic communities held significant economic importance. Singapore's bilingual education policy required students to master "English which is the core language of instruction in government schools" while also learning "their Mother Tongue (Mandarin Chinese, Malay or Tamil) as a second language"28. Within this framework, the character system employed became less a marker of political allegiance than a practical tool adapted to Singapore's unique multilingual ecology.

The cultural and political divergences between diaspora communities using traditional characters and mainland communities using simplified characters became increasingly pronounced throughout the latter twentieth century. Hong Kong and Taiwan developed sophisticated literary and media cultures based on traditional characters that circulated within regional networks of Chinese-language publishing, film, and television16,48. These cultural productions from Hong Kong and Taiwan in traditional characters exercised enormous influence throughout Southeast Asian Chinese communities, providing alternative models of Chinese modernity that diverged sharply from the mainland revolutionary vision. Meanwhile, the People's Republic maintained relative cultural and informational isolation from diaspora communities through much of the Maoist period, gradually intensifying engagement following economic reforms and the opening to the world beginning in the late 1970s. This extended period of relative separation allowed diaspora communities using traditional characters to develop distinct cultural practices and identity formations that centered traditional characters as integral to their cultural heritage rather than as backward or impedimentary to modernity.

Heritage Language Learning and the Politics of Character Systems in Diaspora Schooling

The emergence of formal heritage language schooling in diaspora contexts created important institutional spaces through which the politics of character systems became negotiated and contested across generations. The first Chinese heritage language school in the United States was established in San Francisco in 1886, when "Chinese immigrants were segregated in specific neighborhoods" and the curriculum was "highly traditional, teaching Chinese classics written in traditional Chinese characters"9. This early institutional development reflected immigrants' determination to preserve Chinese cultural knowledge and maintain connections to their homeland despite conditions of severe racial discrimination and exclusion. "Because Chinese immigrants were segregated in specific neighborhoods at that time, the curriculum was highly traditional, teaching Chinese classics written in traditional Chinese characters. Cantonese was the only dialect of instruction, since the majority of students were from Cantonese areas of China"9. These early schools thus institutionalized both the use of traditional characters and the preservation of dialect-specific (in this case Cantonese) instruction, representing the predominant patterns of Chinese settlement in nineteenth-century America.

The character of Chinese heritage language education underwent dramatic transformation following World War II and the subsequent waves of Chinese immigration. After 1949, and particularly from the 1970s onward, new cohorts of Chinese immigrants arrived in the United States and other Western nations, many of whom spoke Mandarin rather than Cantonese as their primary language. This demographic shift fundamentally altered the character of heritage language schooling. "Mandarin gradually superseded Cantonese as the predominant language of instruction in Chinese schools," with "by the mid-1990s, about eight out of ten pupils in Chinese schools were taught in Mandarin; Cantonese remained the language of instruction for only one of every eight students"9. The widespread adoption of Mandarin as the language of instruction accompanied an important choice regarding which character system to teach. American Chinese heritage schools, influenced partly by Taiwan's prominence as an alternative center of Chinese cultural production and partly by the persistence of early educational traditions, continued to privilege traditional characters despite the fact that "the United States ecological environment for Chinese immigrants fluctuates from time to time"21.

This decision to maintain traditional characters in diaspora heritage language schools represented a deliberate preservation of character systems that linked diaspora communities to regions (Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau) that had maintained traditional character practices. The continued teaching of traditional characters in diaspora schools constructed these characters as essential to legitimate Chinese cultural knowledge and identity, positioning the character system itself as a crucial link binding diaspora members to Chinese civilization and heritage. For heritage language learners in particular, mastery of traditional characters became intertwined with broader questions of ethnic and cultural identity, as research has demonstrated that "Chinese fluency is linked closely to how Chinese a person is"8. Studies of American-born Chinese children revealed that "a statistically significant relationship between Chinese heritage language proficiency and ethnic identity" existed, with "a large majority of young CHLLs (95%) held that as Chinese people, they should learn and be able to speak Chinese"8. Within this framework where language proficiency became understood as central to ethnic identification, the choice of which character system to learn—simplified or traditional—carried profound implications for how diaspora youth understood their own relationship to Chinese identity.

The organizational structure of Chinese heritage language schooling in the United States reflected and reinforced these choices regarding character systems and educational priorities. Two major national organizations emerged to serve diaspora schools: "NCACLS (National Council of Associations of Chinese Language Schools)" established primarily "by immigrants from Taiwan," and "CSAUS (Chinese School Association in the United States)" established "by immigrants from the People's Republic"9. These parallel organizations embodied the bifurcation of diaspora Chinese identity between supporters of the PRC and supporters of Taiwan, with the choice of character system serving as a concrete marker of these political and organizational divisions. Schools affiliated with NCACLS and Taiwan-origin immigrants taught traditional characters in alignment with Taiwan's educational standards, while CSAUS-affiliated schools theoretically might adopt simplified characters in alignment with PRC practices. However, the overall dominance of Taiwan-origin educators and traditional character instruction in heritage language schools suggests that the Taiwanese trajectory of character preservation exercised greater influence over diaspora educational practice than mainland simplified-character instruction.

The perpetuation of traditional characters in heritage language schools generated complex responses among diaspora youth. Some heritage language learners embraced the challenge of mastering traditional characters as a meaningful connection to cultural heritage, experiencing literacy in classical forms as an affirmation of ethnic identity and cultural belonging8. Others experienced the requirement to learn traditional characters as an obstacle to communication with family members in mainland China or as an impractical skill in an era of digital technologies and globalization. The search for online resources and instruction for Chinese heritage language learning became increasingly difficult when restricted to traditional characters alone, as the dominance of mainland internet usage and simplified-character digital content meant that "most of the Chinese speakers in today's world now use simplified Chinese," making it "very difficult to conceive of a change in this trend"20. These generational tensions over character choice within diaspora heritage language schools thus reflected broader uncertainties about the relationship between diaspora communities, the mainland, Taiwan, and the meanings of Chinese cultural identity in a rapidly globalizing world.

Contemporary Language Policy and the Reassertion of Mainland Authority

The early twenty-first century witnessed dramatic intensification of mainland Chinese state efforts to establish simplified characters and Mandarin as globally dominant forms of Chinese expression, accompanied by increasing pressure on diaspora communities and non-mainland regions to conform to mainland linguistic and orthographic standards. In March 2026, China enacted a sweeping "Law on Promoting Ethnic Unity and Progress" that mandated "Mandarin as the basic language of instruction in schools from kindergarten through the end of high school, and for all government and official business"18. This landmark legislation represented an escalation of decades-long pressure on minority languages and regional writing systems, extending mainland authority over linguistic practice in ways that directly threatened the viability of traditional characters and regional languages in minority regions45. The draft legislation specified that "in public settings where Mandarin and minority languages appear together, Mandarin must be given 'prominence in placement, order, and similar respects,'" institutionalizing hierarchies of linguistic prestige that marginalized all non-Mandarin forms18.

More broadly, China released in January 2026 "a national plan to systematically promote the country's linguistic and cultural heritage, setting clear targets for 2030 and 2035 to enhance national language capabilities"41. This comprehensive plan emphasized "expanding the use of standard spoken and written Chinese nationwide while preserving ethnic minority languages" in ways that critics recognized as assimilationist rhetoric disguising efforts to displace minority languages from educational and public domains41. The plan called for "integrating language culture across the entire national education system, from primary schools to universities," while simultaneously claiming to respect minority languages as long as they remained relegated to secondary status41. Notably, the plan also stipulated expansion of "language and cultural exchanges with Hong Kong, Macao and Taiwan," signaling mainland intent to extend linguistic and cultural authority over these territories despite their distinct institutional arrangements41. These developments suggest a contemporary reassertion of mainland authority that parallels the standardization projects of the 1950s, now implemented through more sophisticated mechanisms of linguistic policy and cultural governance.

The mainland government's approach toward traditional characters has shifted from outright suppression to more nuanced strategies of marginalization and instrumentalization. While "there is no legislation prohibiting the use of traditional Chinese characters" on the mainland, traditional characters have been relegated to "stylistic and commercial purposes, such as in shopfront displays and advertising," while "traditional Chinese characters remain ubiquitous on buildings that predate the promulgation of the current simplification scheme, such as former government buildings, religious buildings, educational institutions, and historical monuments"7. The Law of the People's Republic of China on the National Common Language and Characters "implies that simplified Chinese characters are the country's standard script, with traditional Chinese being used for purposes such as ceremonies, cultural purposes such as calligraphy, for decoration, in publications and books on ancient literature and poetry, and for research purposes"1. This framework permits continued ceremonial and aesthetic use of traditional characters while systematically privileges simplified forms for practical communication, suggesting a strategy whereby traditional characters become increasingly associated with the past and simplified characters with modernity and progress.

Diaspora communities have experienced these mainland pressures toward character standardization with varying degrees of resistance and accommodation. In Hong Kong particularly, the assault on traditional characters became intertwined with broader anxieties regarding the erosion of Hong Kong's autonomy under the National Security Law implemented in 202016. Young Hongkongers increasingly perceived the defense of traditional characters as intrinsically bound up with the defense of Hong Kong's democratic autonomy and distinctive identity against mainland encroachment. The survey data regarding Hong Kong identity revealed that "in its June 2022 poll, 39.1% of respondents identified as Hong Konger, 31.4% as Hong Konger in China, 17.6% as Chinese, 10.9% as Chinese in Hong Kong, and 42.4% as mixed identity," with "the decrease in the number of people who identify as Hong Kongers" potentially "related to the mass exodus of pro-democracy Hong Kongers"48. This mass departure of Hong Kong residents, many of whom migrated to English-speaking nations such as Australia, Canada, and the United States, created new diaspora communities composed of individuals who maintained fierce commitment to traditional characters as markers of Hong Kong identity rather than Chinese identity proper.

Taiwan has experienced parallel pressures toward mainland linguistic conformity, though the democratic political system and the government's explicit commitment to Taiwanese identity have reinforced continued use and promotion of traditional characters. The Taiwanese government remained firmly committed to traditional character instruction despite periodic proposals for introducing simplified characters into the educational system. In December 2004, the Ministry of Education "rejected a proposal from a Beijing Chinese People's Political Consultative Conference (CPPCC) political conference member that called for elementary schools to teach traditional Chinese characters in addition to the simplified ones"1. The rejection itself indicated Taiwan's determined commitment to traditional character education as non-negotiable national policy. Meanwhile, Taiwan implemented sophisticated policies supporting the use of indigenous languages and scripts alongside traditional Chinese characters, creating a multilingual, multiscriptal educational ecology fundamentally different from mainland approaches. The Council of Indigenous Peoples promoted "a Six-Year Plan for Taiwan's Indigenous Languages Revitalization," establishing learning mechanisms and teaching programs designed to ensure "intergenerational inheritance in families, villages, and communities"31. This commitment to preserving multiple language systems and writing scripts—both indigenous Taiwanese languages and traditional Chinese characters—articulated a vision of linguistic and cultural diversity as essential to national identity rather than as obstacles to modernization.

The Diasporic Politics of Identity Formation Across Generations

The experience of Chinese diaspora communities across Southeast Asia, South Asia, and the trans-Pacific world reveals how the choice between simplified and traditional characters has become bound up with fundamental questions of generational identity, belonging, and the meanings of being Chinese in an era of globalization and transnational migration. Research on heritage language learners within diaspora communities has documented the complex ways that engagement with Chinese character systems reflects and shapes ethnic identity formation. Studies of heritage language learners have found that "learning Chinese has provided affordances to heritage language learners to affirm and enhance the Chinese part of their multifaceted identity," with learners reporting that "the process of learning Chinese heritage language enabled her to 'understand what kind of person I am and that I know I am Chinese'"8. Yet this identity affirmation through heritage language learning has not followed uniform trajectories; younger generations of diaspora youth, born and socialized in Western educational systems, often experienced Chinese heritage language learning as a domain of family obligation and parental expectation rather than as freely chosen pursuit.

The divergence between first-generation immigrants and their diaspora-born offspring regarding character system preferences mirrors broader generational divides regarding Chinese identity and belonging. First-generation Chinese immigrants who arrived as adults and established heritage language schools and community institutions were often deeply committed to preserving traditional characters and regional linguistic practices as markers of cultural authenticity and connection to homelands. By contrast, second-generation diaspora youth increasingly questioned whether mastery of traditional characters represented a practical skill or an impossible barrier to meaningful engagement with the lived realities of China and Chinese culture. The digitalization of communication and the dominance of simplified-character internet content created practical pressures that favored simplified character learning, even among heritage language students committed to developing Chinese proficiency. Online resources for Chinese language learning, social media platforms used by diaspora communities, and the sheer volume of simplified-character content available through internet channels meant that students seeking functional Chinese literacy increasingly encountered simplified characters as the default script.

The emergence of sophisticated digital platforms for diaspora community communication has further complicated character system politics. Transnational Chinese mothers in diaspora contexts, particularly those maintaining active engagement with extended family networks in mainland China through digital platforms, have adopted complex translanguaging practices that incorporate both simplified and traditional characters depending on their specific communicative purposes and audiences. Research on "digital community, identity, and multilingual parenting" among transnational Chinese mothers found that "transnational mothers deploy linguistic and non-linguistic semiotic resources to navigate multicultural contexts, construct a sense of belonging, and represent translingual identities in the digital space". These mothers engaged in "translanguaging practices" that strategically employed simplified characters when communicating with mainland family networks, traditional characters when addressing diaspora community members, and English when navigating official interactions within their host societies. The character system chosen became a practical tool for audience accommodation rather than an assertion of fixed political allegiance, yet these pragmatic choices simultaneously indexed and perpetuated the transnational networks through which diaspora identity remained constituted.

The political stakes of character system choice have become particularly acute in recent diaspora formations resulting from Hong Kong's political upheaval and the decline of Hong Kong's democratic freedoms. A new generation of Hong Kong diaspora, dispersed across Canada, Australia, the United States, and the United Kingdom following mass emigration after 2019, has articulated emphatic commitment to traditional characters as central to Hong Kong identity and as a form of resistance to mainland cultural assimilation. These newer diaspora communities, unlike earlier waves of Chinese migrants from mainland or Taiwan origins, constitute themselves as specifically Hong Kong diaspora rather than as generic Chinese diaspora, and their commitment to traditional characters has become intertwined with political projects of maintaining Hong Kong autonomy and preserving democratic values. The circulation of traditional-character media, literature, and digital content among these communities has created transnational networks of Hong Kong identity that remain geographically dispersed but politically and culturally cohesive.

Similarly, diaspora communities emerging from Taiwan, particularly among younger Taiwanese who have increasingly embraced distinctive Taiwanese identity in distinction to Chinese identity, have become increasingly committed to the preservation of traditional characters as essential to Taiwanese cultural distinctiveness. Research on "identity and democratization in Taiwan and Hong Kong" documented the ways that young people in both territories have come to associate "distinctive social, economic and political identities" with commitments to preserving local languages and writing systems in their traditional forms16. The desire for democracy and distinctive ways of life differentiated both Hong Kong and Taiwan from mainland China and has become intertwined with resistance to simplified character imposition and Mandarin linguistic standardization. Traditional characters have thus acquired symbolic potency as tangible, visible markers through which diaspora communities can assert and maintain political and cultural positions that distinguish them from mainland China and its revolutionary modernization project.

Conclusion: Characters as Historical Documents of Diaspora, Modernity, and Contested Identities

The historical evolution of Chinese character systems in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries constitutes far more than a technical matter of orthographic convenience or literacy pedagogy. Rather, the bifurcation of Chinese writing into simplified and traditional systems, and the complex negotiations of character choice across diaspora communities, embodies fundamental political struggles over the meanings of modernity, the legitimate content of Chinese identity, and the proper relationship between individuals, communities, nations, and civilizations. The People's Republic of China's promotion of simplified characters in the 1950s represented a revolutionary modernization project that sought to remake Chinese civilization through radical transformation of its foundational communication systems. This project achieved genuine successes in expanding literacy and creating a unified orthographic system capable of serving an expanding nation-state apparatus. Yet it also entailed the displacement of regional and dialectal linguistic practices, the foreclosure of alternative modernities that might have built upon Chinese cultural traditions rather than rejecting them as feudal impediments to progress, and the subordination of cultural diversity to national standardization.

The diaspora communities scattered across Southeast Asia, South Asia, the Caribbean, and the trans-Pacific world have engaged with Chinese character systems in ways that reveal alternatives to the revolutionary modernization trajectory that dominated mainland China. By continuing to use, teach, and circulate traditional characters, diaspora communities created spaces in which Chinese cultural identity could develop through mechanisms other than state standardization and enforced conformity. The persistence of traditional character practice in Hong Kong, Macau, and Taiwan despite decades of mainland pressure toward simplification demonstrates the resilience of these alternative modernities and their continued capacity to attract loyalty and commitment from diaspora members. The recent assertion of even more aggressive mainland linguistic and character policies, extending governmental authority over character and language choice through legislation and policy directives, reveals the ongoing stakes of these struggles. The Chinese nation-state continues to perceive linguistic and orthographic diversity as threats to national unity and continues to deploy character standardization as a technology for extending state authority and constructing unified national consciousness.

Yet the dynamic evolution of diaspora character practices also reveals the capacity of communities to maintain multiple, fluid, and contextual relationships to writing systems rather than to reproduce the binary politics of simplified versus traditional as fixed ideological commitments. Transnational communities increasingly adopt strategic and practical approaches to character choice, employing simplified forms for communication with mainland networks, traditional forms for connection to diaspora communities and heritage practices, and English or other languages for navigation of official institutions and global networks. These pragmatic adaptations suggest that character systems may be gradually transformed from political symbols indexing rigid allegiances into tools for managing complex transnational communication across multiple audiences and contexts. At the same time, younger diaspora members dispersed from Hong Kong and Taiwan continue to articulate emphatic political commitments to traditional characters as essential to democratic values and cultural autonomy, suggesting that character systems retain their capacity to carry profound political and cultural meanings.

The research undertaken here reveals that character systems constitute essential texts through which to read the political and cultural histories of modern China, its revolutionary transformation, and the complex experiences of diaspora communities negotiating belonging and identity across national, linguistic, and cultural borders. To study the history of simplified versus traditional characters is to study the history of China's encounters with modernity, its efforts to construct unified national identity through cultural standardization, and the alternative paths taken by diaspora and non-mainland communities committed to preserving Chinese cultural forms and practices in their traditional iterations. The ongoing contestation over character systems in contemporary China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and diaspora communities demonstrates that these struggles remain vital and unresolved. As global migration accelerates and diaspora communities continue to grow and develop distinct identities, the politics of writing systems will likely continue to serve as crucial domains through which communities assert their distinctive understandings of modernity, cultural authenticity, and the meanings of being Chinese in an increasingly interconnected yet deeply divided world. The future of Chinese character systems thus remains contested terrain where questions of political authority, cultural identity, linguistic rights, and social justice remain urgently at stake.
