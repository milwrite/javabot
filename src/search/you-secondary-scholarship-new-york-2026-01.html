The Common School Movement in the United States and New York State: A Comprehensive Analysis of Secondary Scholarship

Executive Summary

The common school movement represents one of the most transformative educational initiatives in American history, fundamentally reshaping the landscape of public education from the 1820s through the nineteenth century. This comprehensive research report examines secondary scholarship on the common school movement nationally and in New York State specifically, synthesizing the work of prominent historians including Carl Kaestle, David Tyack, Lawrence Cremin, and contemporary scholars. The movement emerged in response to rapid urbanization, immigration, and industrial development, driven by reformers who envisioned free, nonsectarian, publicly funded schools as essential institutions for creating an educated citizenry capable of sustaining democratic governance and industrial productivity. New York State played a particularly significant role in this transformation, establishing one of the first statewide systems of common schools in 1812 and serving as a model for educational development throughout the nation. The common school movement generated intense scholarly debate regarding its true motivations, with revisionist historians arguing that schools served primarily as instruments of social control and cultural homogenization, while other scholars emphasize the genuine democratic aspirations of educational reformers. This report draws upon diverse secondary sources to illuminate the complex origins, implementation, controversies, and lasting legacies of this pivotal movement in American educational history.

The Origins and Early Development of the Common School Movement

The common school movement did not emerge suddenly but rather evolved gradually from earlier educational initiatives spanning colonial America through the early nineteenth century. According to the EBSCO Research Starters account of the Free Public School Movement, educational reform had its "roots in the late eighteenth century, when a number of U.S. states drafted constitutions containing clauses urging public aid to education."3 However, as this source emphasizes, "the idea that education was a government responsibility, rather than a responsibility of families, churches, and philanthropy, took hold only gradually."3 The transition from private, church-based, and philanthropic education to government-sponsored public education represented a fundamental ideological shift in how American society conceptualized the purpose and delivery of schooling. The historiography of this transition reveals important distinctions between the rhetorical arguments made by reformers and the actual conditions that prompted educational expansion.

During the colonial period and early republic, education in the American states remained predominantly a local and private concern. As one historical analysis notes, "the colonists valued education," and by 1635, "Boston Latin School in Denholm, MA became the first publicly funded school."47 However, this early public funding existed primarily within the context of community institutions serving limited populations. The Massachusetts Act of 1647 "established the right of the state to require communities to create and maintain elementary schools in all towns for every child and secondary schools for youth in larger towns," and critically, this law "established the tradition that these schools should be funded through local property tax as land was considered to be a valid measure of wealth."47 This foundational decision to link educational funding to property taxation would have profound and enduring consequences for equity in American education.

The historical development of New York State's educational system illustrates the gradual movement toward comprehensive public education. Documentation from the history of education in New York indicates that prior to systematic reform, "the only schools available to New York kids were private schools run by churches" and "the system created a gap between the haves and have-nots."13 In response to these inequities, "on April 9, 1795, Governor George Clinton signed a bill allocating $50,000 to assist charitable schools across the state."13 This initial funding effort proved insufficient, as "the money was not renewed in 1800," and "the fight for a more serious commitment to 'common schools' resumed."13 The struggle to establish sustained public funding for schools reveals the political obstacles that reformers faced in convincing taxpayers to support education for all children regardless of family wealth.

The ideological foundations for common school advocacy were articulated by forward-thinking leaders such as DeWitt Clinton, who served as New York governor and mayor. According to scholarship examining Clinton's vision, he articulated "two moral imperatives behind common schools": first, "education was a right, not an inherited privilege," and second, "a public education system must be available to every single student, regardless of family background."13 Clinton emphasized that in a democratic republic "no privileged orders, no factitious distinctions in society, no hereditary nobility, no established religion, no royal prerogatives, exist to interpose barriers between the people," and therefore "all men being considered as enjoying an equality of rights, the propriety and necessity of dispensing, without distinction, the blessings of education, followed of course."13 This rhetoric of equality and democratic necessity would become central to common school advocacy throughout the nineteenth century.

The Free School Society of New York, formally organized in 1805, represented an important intermediate step between purely private education and fully public systems. According to historical records, "the legislature passed 'the Act to incorporate the Society instituted in the city of New York, for the establishment of a free school for the education of poor children who do not belong to, or are not provided for by, any religious society.'"13 This organization employed the Lancasterian System, also known as the Monitorial System, which was "a system of education popularized by Joseph Lancaster" that "relied on students to educate other students—a small number of adult masters would oversee the education, while students who mastered a topic were enlisted to help their less advanced peers."13 The Lancasterian system proved attractive to reformers and policymakers because it was "cost-effective and efficient," allowing schools to serve growing numbers of students with limited resources.16

Horace Mann and the Massachusetts Model of Common School Reform

The common school movement's most crucial turning point occurred in Massachusetts with the appointment of Horace Mann as the first Secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education. According to secondary scholarship, "from 1837 until 1848, Horace Mann led the reform on education in Massachusetts as a lawyer, Massachusetts State senator, and the first secretary of the Massachusetts State Board of Education."10 The significance of this appointment cannot be overstated, as Mann transformed his position into a platform for promoting a vision of public education that would influence the entire nation. As one historical analysis notes, "Horace Mann (1796–1859), 'The Father of the Common School Movement,' was the foremost proponent of education reform in antebellum America," and "an ardent member of the Whig Party, Mann argued that the common school, a free, universal, non-sectarian, and public institution, was the best means of achieving the moral and socioeconomic uplift of all Americans."7

Mann's vision extended far beyond merely establishing schools; rather, he articulated a comprehensive philosophy of education rooted in moral development and civic preparation. The reform movement he championed "sought to create the virtuous republican citizenry needed to sustain American political institutions, the educated workforce required to expand the American economy, and the disciplined generation necessary to forestall the social disorders so common in American cities in the decades before the Civil War."7 This multifaceted justification for common schools—combining democratic, economic, and social control imperatives—would characterize common school discourse throughout the nineteenth century. Mann's personal background and connections enhanced his effectiveness as an educational reformer; as a scholar notes, "Brother-in-law of Nathaniel Hawthorne and close friend of Samuel Gridley Howe, Mann was well connected to the cultural and political elite of New England."7

The practical achievements of Mann's tenure as Secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education demonstrated the transformative potential of dedicated educational leadership. According to historical documentation, Mann "served as Secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education" from "1837 till 1848," during which time he "traveled around the U.S. and Europe to learn about the best educational ideas" and "used this knowledge to promote Common Schools reforms, including legislation to create the first state-wide graded elementary and secondary public school system with a well-organized curriculum, engaging instruction, and four teacher education colleges to professionally-train teachers."6 The innovations Mann championed included not only curriculum development and school organization but also the professionalization of teaching through teacher training institutions. His "public education innovations, publicized in his 12 Reports of the Secretary of the State Board of Education were imitated throughout the U.S."6

Mann's philosophy of moral education constituted a central element of his common school vision. In his influential writings, Mann articulated arguments for free schools grounded in moral necessity and democratic theory. As documented in historical scholarship, Mann contended that "under our republican government, the minimum of this education can never be less than sufficient to qualify each citizen for the civil and social duties he will be called to discharge," and that education should teach "the great laws of bodily health, as qualifies for the fulfillment of parental duties, as is indispensable for the civil functions of a witness or a juror, as is necessary for the voter in municipal and in national affairs of this great republic."6 Beyond democratic and civic justifications, Mann also advanced economic arguments, contending that "an educated people is always a more industrious and productive people" and that "intelligence is a primary ingredient in the wealth of nations."6

However, Mann's legacy remains complicated in several important respects. Historians note that "some historians consider his movement as an important step toward a more open and fluid society in which merit would trump birth," while "other historians view the common school as a rather blunt tool for social control, one that tended both to stifle intellectual curiosity and to suppress diversity."7 More particularly, "he certainly sought to universalize the values and beliefs of the mainstream Protestant middle class of the North," and "the Irish immigrants to Massachusetts were especially vociferous in their condemnation of his Protestant-centered morality and reacted by constructing their own system of parochial schools."7 This tension between Mann's universal rhetoric and the particularistic Protestant values embedded in common school curricula would generate sustained controversy throughout the common school era.

The Free Public School Movement and Expansion of Common Schools Nationally

The common school movement gained momentum throughout the 1820s and 1830s, driven by a combination of reform impulses, economic development, and democratic ideology. The EBSCO account of the Free Public School Movement documents that during "the late 1820's and the 1830's, demands were heard for the establishment of a system of free public schooling open to all," and "in some larger cities, workingmen's parties called upon their state legislatures to establish public schools."3 Significantly, working-class advocates articulated educational demands in explicitly political terms; for example, "the workingmen of Boston declared in 1830 that 'the establishment of a liberal system of education, attainable by all, should be among the first efforts of every lawgiver who desires the continuance of our national independence.'"3 This rhetoric linked educational expansion directly to the preservation of republican government, suggesting that widespread public education was necessary for the functioning of democratic institutions.

The movement achieved substantial policy success over the course of the nineteenth century. According to historical documentation, "by 1850, the movement for free public schooling had largely achieved its basic objectives."3 By mid-century, "every state had established some type of permanent school fund" and "every state except Arkansas had experimented with taxation as a means of school support."3 Critically, "the principle of public support for common schools was generally accepted throughout the union," and "by 1850, according to historian Lawrence Cremin, 'the people . . . largely controlled the schools which they had instituted with public funds.'"3 This assertion of popular control over schools reflected the democratic localism that characterized common school systems, at least in their early formulations.

However, the transition to publicly supported education generated considerable resistance from those who viewed education as fundamentally a private and familial concern. As one historical source documents, opponents of public schools persisted throughout the nineteenth century, and "through the rest of the nineteenth century and indeed into the twentieth century, the issue of free public schools continued to have opponents as well as supporters."3 These opponents included those who opposed common schools on principle, as well as more pragmatic objectors motivated by financial concerns. Historians note that critics included "many taxpayers who did not want to educate other people's children—who considered education strictly a private concern."3 This fundamental tension between those viewing education as a public good benefiting all citizens and those regarding it as a private family responsibility would persist throughout the common school era and beyond.

The establishment of permanent funding mechanisms proved essential to sustaining common school systems. Legislation in various states created "permanent school funds" that provided a more stable basis for educational financing than year-to-year appropriations. These structural changes reflected growing acceptance of the principle that education constituted a legitimate public responsibility. Significantly, "the principle of public control of education" accompanied the principle of public support, such that by 1850 "the people . . . largely controlled the schools which they had instituted with public funds."3 This emphasis on local public control distinguished American common schools from the more centralized systems being developed in Europe, and this decentralized character would become increasingly important as educational expansion proceeded.

New York State's Distinctive Contribution to Common School Development

New York State played a particularly significant role in establishing common school systems that would become models for other states. The state's educational system was organized through legislation in 1812, which "reestablished the system of school districts, supported by State and local funds (as well as by tuition) and administered by a State Superintendent of Common Schools."43 The timing of New York's legislation preceded Mann's Massachusetts reforms by approximately twenty-five years, indicating that New York rather than Massachusetts initiated the first comprehensive state-level common school system. According to historical documentation, the 1812 law followed an 1795 initiative that had sought to establish public education; "in 1812, a Legislative commission had pointed to the 'remote and thinly populated parts of the state' as the areas where 'education stands greatly in need of encouragement.'"43

Gideon Hawley, New York's first Superintendent of Common Schools, played a crucial role in establishing the machinery of state educational administration. According to historical sources, Hawley "from the Town of Charlton, Saratoga County, who is known as 'The Father of Common Schools' in New York," served as the first superintendent and "organized the system, distributed school aid from the Common School Fund, and prodded local officials to set up school districts and submit reports."19 Hawley's work in establishing district structures and accountability mechanisms created a foundational framework for managing a large-scale educational system. However, his tenure faced political obstacles; "Hawley was dismissed in 1821 for political reasons, and thereafter the Secretary of State served as the Superintendent of Common Schools,"14 illustrating how political considerations could interfere with educational administration even in this early period.

The administrative evolution of education in New York reveals the complex relationship between state oversight and local autonomy that characterized common school governance. After 1821, "the Secretary of State served as the Superintendent of Common Schools," and "in 1854 the Legislature created a Department of Public Instruction, headed by a Superintendent elected jointly by the Senate and Assembly for a three-year renewable term."33 This administrative structure reflected competing impulses toward centralization and localism. The New York Board of Regents, established in 1787, "adopted standards for incorporating private academies (1801) and colleges (1811), and required academies to offer acceptable programs in order to receive aid from the Literature Fund, established in 1801."33 The development of statewide educational standards proceeded gradually, and "during the later nineteenth century the Regents developed educational standards for academies and high schools statewide, through use of the Regents examinations and syllabi."33

Key Reformers and Philosophical Foundations of Common School Ideology

The common school movement was not a single unified phenomenon but rather encompassed diverse reformers and philosophical approaches united by commitment to expanded public education. Beyond Horace Mann, other significant figures contributed important ideas and practical achievements to the movement. Henry Barnard, "a fellow educational reformer in Rhode Island," worked alongside state leaders "to evaluate and reform the common schools of the state."10 Barnard "worked closely with Governor James Fenner" in the 1840s to accomplish these reforms, and "in 1845, Barnard established Rhode Island's first teaching institute."10 Barnard's emphasis on teacher training paralleled Mann's concerns about professionalizing teaching, and both reformers recognized that common school success depended fundamentally on producing an adequate supply of trained teachers.

The philosophical foundations of common school ideology drew upon various intellectual traditions and political arguments. Drawing on the work of scholar David Labaree, common schools served multiple and sometimes conflicting purposes that would generate ongoing tensions throughout the movement's history. According to educational analysis, common schools were intended to serve three primary purposes: "social mobility (schools should prepare students to compete for higher social positions)," "social efficiency (schools should focus on training workers)," and "democratic equality (schools should mold citizens)."38 These competing purposes reflected fundamentally different views of what education was for, and different stakeholders prioritized these purposes differently. While this framework was developed by a modern historian to analyze schools across American history, it illuminates the multiple objectives that nineteenth-century common school reformers articulated.

The ideological arguments for common schools drew heavily upon republican political theory and Enlightenment philosophy. Common school advocates contended that in a democratic republic where all adult males possessed political power through voting, all citizens required sufficient education to exercise this power responsibly. As one historical analysis of the common school's origins notes, "the universal argument in favor of free schools has been that the general intelligence which they are capable of diffusing is indispensable to the continuance of a republican government."6 This argument linked educational expansion directly to democratic governance, suggesting that democracy could not survive without an educated populace capable of making informed decisions about public affairs. This linkage between common schools and democratic stability would become a constant refrain in common school discourse throughout the nineteenth century.

Beyond democratic arguments, common school advocates also articulated economic justifications for public education. They contended that an educated workforce was more productive and capable of participating in industrial manufacturing. As Thomas Jefferson and other Founding Fathers had argued, "employers needed literate workers," and education was increasingly seen as contributing to economic development and national competitiveness.21 This economic argument for education gained force as industrial development accelerated during the nineteenth century, and by the time of the Civil War, arguments about education's contribution to economic productivity were common in common school discourse.

The moral and religious dimensions of common school ideology represented another important justification for educational expansion. Common school reformers believed that schools could inculcate moral values essential for social order and individual virtue. The McGuffey Readers, which "were widely used as textbooks in American schools from the mid-19th century to the early 20th century," exemplified this approach, as the content of these primers "was carefully curated to reflect the moral and ethical standards of 19th-century America" and "each lesson was designed to convey moral teachings."31 This emphasis on moral education reflected concerns about social disorder in rapidly urbanizing societies, and the belief that schools could serve as instruments of moral formation.

Curriculum, Morality, and Religious Education in Common Schools

The curriculum of common schools embodied the reformers' vision of what knowledge and values should be transmitted to the next generation. The dominant textbooks and pedagogical approaches reveal important information about what common schools were designed to accomplish. The McGuffey Readers demonstrate how common schools sought to instill particular values and knowledge through carefully selected texts. These primers "used word repetition in the text as a learning tool, developing reading skills by challenging students using the books" and "emphasized spelling, vocabulary, and formal public speaking."31 Beyond basic skills, the Readers conveyed cultural messages about American history, literature, and proper behavior. The series "consisted of stories, poems, essays, and speeches" and "the advanced Readers contained excerpts from the works of well-regarded English and American writers and politicians such as Lord Byron, John Milton, and Daniel Webster."31 This exposure to canonical literature served to transmit cultural knowledge and aesthetic values deemed essential for educated citizens.

The moral dimensions of the McGuffey Readers evolved significantly over the course of the nineteenth century, reflecting changing social conditions and cultural priorities. According to historical analysis, "McGuffey is remembered as a conservative theological teacher" who "interpreted the goals of public schooling in terms of moral and spiritual education, and attempted to give schools a curriculum that would instill Presbyterian Calvinist beliefs and manners in their students."31 However, "these goals were considered suitable for the relatively homogeneous America of the early- to mid-19th century, though they were less so for the increasingly pluralistic society that developed in the late 19th century and early 20th century."31 The response to this pluralism was noteworthy: "the content of the readers changed drastically between McGuffey's 1836–1837 edition and the 1879 edition," with revised versions "compiled to meet the needs of national unity and the dream of an American melting pot for the world's masses."31 In this revision, "the Calvinist values of salvation, righteousness, and piety were excluded from the later versions, though they had been prominent in the early Readers," and "the content of the books was secularized and replaced by middle-class civil religion, morality, and values."31

The religious character of common school curriculum generated sustained controversy, particularly in religiously diverse urban areas. According to scholarship examining this issue, "within the common schools in New York City – and elsewhere – daily scripture readings from the King James Version of the Bible were required," and "prayers, songs and general religious instruction at odds with Catholic belief were the norm."20 This Protestant religious orientation was not simply incidental to common school pedagogy but rather constituted a central element of the moral education that schools were designed to provide. The anti-Catholic content embedded in common school curricula reflected nativist sentiments and Protestant cultural dominance. Historians document that "anti-Catholic sentiments extended throughout the curriculum with references to deceitful Catholics, murderous inquisitions, vile popery, Church corruption, conniving Jesuits and the pope as the anti-Christ of Revelation commonplace."20

However, recent scholarship challenges the assumption that common schools were uniformly hostile to religious diversity. One important historical study examined how common schools actually handled religious differences, analyzing "over 2,000 legal appeals and 7,000 letters written by New York State residents complaining or inquiring about the policies of their local schools during the 1870s and 1880s."23 This research found that "in less than one percent of their letters did New York State residents complain about religious problems in their schools," and "they were far more likely to complain about trustees' buying maps and globes than about their children being persecuted by religious bigots."23 This finding suggests that while high-profile controversies over religion in schools occurred in major cities, the typical rural or small-town common school may have been less explicitly religiously contested. According to this analysis, "democratic localism allowed districts to blow off steam in a number of ways," such that "school districts tended to mirror settlement patterns and neighborhoods, so that religious and ethnic minorities could find themselves in the majority when it came to electing trustees, hiring teachers and setting school policy."23

The Feminization of Teaching and Gender Dynamics in Common Schools

The common school expansion generated unprecedented demand for teachers, which in turn created conditions for the feminization of the teaching profession. Scholarship on this transformation notes that "from colonial times to the present," the gender composition of teachers "changed," and "scholars disagree about why teaching transitioned from a male occupation in the colonial period to a female occupation in 1900."25 The transition was economically motivated and fundamentally gendered in its dynamics. According to historical analysis, "the drive for universal education increased the demand for teachers and the associated costs of instruction, giving an advantage to schools that hired female teachers," since "female teachers were paid about half as much as their male counterparts in standardized schools."25 This wage differential proved crucial; "some scholars attest that 'feminization occurred because school districts were unwilling or unable to pay the rising costs of retaining male teachers as school terms became longer and teaching became less attractive to men.'"25

The discourse surrounding women's role in teaching reveals important ideological dimensions of the common school movement. Educational leaders like Horace Mann promoted women's entry into teaching while simultaneously maintaining patriarchal assumptions about women's nature and proper sphere. According to scholarship examining this phenomenon, while "Mann insisted that women's innate gentleness and caring equipped them to be superior teachers in the new public schools," this rhetoric "coexisted with a sexist system that also questioned women's competence and made women easier to exploit as public schooling scaled nationwide."28 This contradiction between lauding women's supposed maternal qualities and denying them professional status and adequate compensation characterized the feminization process. Women educators "could be compensated at a third of the rate of male counterparts, had few alternative professional prospects, and were only permitted to teach prior to marriage."28

The professionalization of teaching through normal schools created opportunities for women while simultaneously subjecting them to new forms of control and constraint. Normal schools, established beginning in 1823 with the first public normal school "founded in Concord, Vermont, by Samuel Read Hall," and followed by "the first state-supported normal school" established "by the Commonwealth of Massachusetts on the northeast corner of the historic Lexington Battle Green" in 1839,48 were designed to improve teaching quality through formalized teacher preparation. However, the expansion of normal schools coincided with and facilitated the feminization of teaching. The increase in demand for teachers arising from common school expansion meant that normal schools could not provide sufficient numbers of trained teachers, necessitating the recruitment of women into the profession.

Despite initial barriers and discrimination, women teachers in the nineteenth century demonstrated agency in responding to their exploitation and seeking to improve their conditions. Importantly, "it was women who formed the first teachers' unions and demanded that educators be compensated appropriately."28 The emergence of teachers' organizations represented a significant development in labor history as well as educational history. The "American Federation of Teachers (AFT) formed in 1897 as the Chicago Teachers Federation (CTF) with the goal of increasing teachers' salaries and pensions," and "by 1916, a number of local unions had merged to form what is now the AFT."28 Women educators like Catherine Goggin and Margaret Haley, who "founded the CTF linked their work with the labor movement," played leadership roles in organizing teachers and demanding better compensation and working conditions.28 By the twentieth century, women teachers had achieved some success through collective action in improving their circumstances, though gender-based wage disparities and occupational segregation persisted.

Challenges, Controversies, and Opposition to Common Schools

The expansion of common schools encountered significant resistance from multiple quarters, reflecting fundamental disagreements about education's proper role and funding. One important analysis examining this controversy notes that "the movement for private school choice along with President Bush's attempt to 'break the mold' of the American public school system, recalls a sense of rugged" American individualism.5 However, the rhetoric of individual choice opposing common schools had deeper historical roots. According to one critical analysis of common school ideology, fundamental philosophical differences existed between those who supported common schools and their opponents, with the former viewing education as a public good and the latter as a private family responsibility.

Religious controversies represented perhaps the most explosive conflict surrounding common schools, particularly in urban areas with large immigrant populations. The battle over common schools in New York City beginning in 1840 illustrated the intensity of these conflicts. According to historical documentation, "the evolution of the debate over school funding into an anti-Catholic movement was established in the battle over the 'common schools' in New York City that began in 1840."20 At that time, "the New York City schools at that time were funded by the state through the Public School Society," which "was 'a benevolent association formed in 1805 to care for the instruction of children unable to attend religious or private schools'" and had "a primary goal of the Society was 'to inculcate the sublime truths of religion and morality contained in Holy Scriptures' and to assure that Bible exercises were included in the schools it controlled."20

The conflict escalated dramatically when Catholic communities sought to obtain public funding for Catholic schools. According to the historical record, "Catholic parishes had begun to develop their own Catholic schools in response" to the explicitly Protestant character of common schools, and subsequently "Catholic schools in New York City petitioned the common council for a share of the state school fund distributed through the Public School Society."20 The Public School Society's response was dismissive; "the Society answered with a message that resonates with today's rhetoric," claiming "by funding Catholic schools, money would be dissipated and that 'sectarian' Catholic education would replace the common schools."20 This rhetorical opposition to funding "sectarian" education while promoting ostensibly "nonsectarian" Protestant schools masked the deeply religious character of common school pedagogy.

Bishop John Hughes of New York, known as "Dagger John," emerged as the leading advocate for Catholic educational rights. According to historical accounts, Hughes "took a far more confrontational approach to the question of school funding than his predecessor," and "blasting the Public School Society for corrupting Catholic children, Hughes submitted a renewed petition demanding Catholics be given a portion of the state funds for schooling."20 The response to Hughes's petition revealed the anti-Catholic prejudices embedded in common school discourse. When a public debate occurred "for late October, 1840," where "Hughes represented the Catholic schools and spoke for three hours," the "Protestant response covered two days and dealt primarily in anti-Catholic vitriol rather than the issues at hand," with "Catholics represented as irreligious idol worshippers, bent on the murder of all Protestants and the subjugation of all democracies."20

The outcome of this conflict represented a partial victory for Catholic interests but did not substantially change Protestant control of public education. After gaining support "from Governor Seward (after a demonstration of Catholic strength at the voting booth)," a bill was "passed in the state legislature in 1842 which effectively ended the Public School Society's monopoly on New York City public education."20 However, "even under the new legislation, control of the public schools effectively remained in Protestant hands through the school boards," and "when protests were made that reading of the Bible be prohibited as 'sectarian,' a new board of education dominated by Protestants responded that the King James Bible was simply not a sectarian book."20 This history demonstrates how common schools, despite rhetorical claims to nonsectarianism, served as vehicles for Protestant cultural transmission and marginalized non-Protestant communities.
