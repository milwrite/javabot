The Metropolitan Museum of Art's Decorative Arts Departments and Collections: A Comprehensive Guide for Internship Applicants

This comprehensive report examines the Metropolitan Museum of Art's decorative arts departments, their extensive holdings spanning five centuries of European and American design, and the diverse internship opportunities available to aspiring museum professionals. The Met houses over 60,000 works of European decorative arts and approximately 20,000 American decorative arts objects across multiple curatorial departments, making it one of the world's most important repositories of design innovation and artistic craftsmanship. The institution offers multiple internship pathways tailored to students and recent graduates with interests in curatorial practice, conservation, education, and museum administration, with application deadlines in January and May 2026. Prospective applicants should understand the museum's selective evaluation process, which prioritizes demonstrated passion for the field, clear learning objectives, prior exposure to arts and culture, and commitment to building an inclusive museum community. This report synthesizes information from The Met's official departmental descriptions, internship program guidelines, recent exhibitions, and curatorial publications to provide a complete overview for potential interns seeking meaningful experience in decorative arts curation and preservation.

The Metropolitan Museum of Art: Institutional Overview and Collecting Mission

The Metropolitan Museum of Art stands as one of the world's foremost encyclopedic museums, housing a collection of more than 1.5 million objects spanning 17 curatorial departments that reflect over 5,000 years of human creativity20,36. Founded in 1870 by a group of businessmen, financiers, and leading artists who sought to bring art education to the American people, The Met has grown from its initial location at 681 Fifth Avenue to become the largest art museum in the United States33,36. The Museum's founding principles emphasized democratic access to art and the advancement of artistic education, principles that continue to guide institutional practice today. The Met's current Fifth Avenue facade and iconic Great Hall, designed by architect Richard Morris Hunt, opened to the public in December 1902 and represent the Beaux-Arts aesthetic that shaped American museum architecture33. Today, the Museum operates multiple locations, including the main Fifth Avenue building and The Cloisters in Upper Manhattan, with comprehensive exhibition spaces totaling approximately two million square feet. The institution's organizational structure reflects its encyclopedic mission, with 17 curatorial departments each overseeing specialized collections that enable scholars, students, and the general public to study artistic production across geographies and time periods.

The Met's approach to collection building emphasizes active stewardship of holdings while continuing to acquire artworks that strengthen the Museum's narrative capacity and support scholarly research20. More than one-third of the Museum's restricted endowment funds are dedicated to acquisitions, and all collecting departments accept numerous gifts of art annually20,20. This commitment to perpetual collecting has resulted in the acquisition of hundreds of works annually, with particular emphasis in recent years on expanding holdings in Native American art, works from Latin America and the American South, Judaica, and Islamic art from Africa and Indonesia20,20. The Museum's collecting strategies have historically been shaped by world events and evolving cultural values, demonstrating institutional responsiveness to contemporary understandings of artistic significance and historical importance. This active approach to collection development creates numerous opportunities for interns to participate in research, documentation, and curatorial decision-making processes that directly impact how The Met's story is told to diverse audiences.

The Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts: History, Holdings, and Significance

The Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts, established in 1907, represents one of The Met's oldest curatorial units and constitutes the direct descendant of the Museum's original Department of Decorative Arts1. The department's founding is intrinsically linked to J. Pierpont Morgan's monumental gift of the Hoentschel collection, comprising French seventeenth- and eighteenth-century paneling, furniture, decorative paintings, and gilt-bronze mounts1,34. Morgan's generosity was so transformative that it catalyzed the creation of the first object-based decorative arts department of its kind in the United States, establishing a model that subsequently influenced museum development across America1. Over the course of 60 years, the department's growth and evolution proved so substantial that it generated additional curatorial departments, including Arms and Armor (1912), Far Eastern Art (1915), Near Eastern Art (1932), Medieval Art (1934), American Art (1934), and Modern and Contemporary Art (1967)1. The department's official name has evolved multiple times—from Department of Decorative Arts to Department of Renaissance and Modern Art (1934), Renaissance and Post-Renaissance Art (1956), Western European Arts (1961), and finally Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts (1977 to present)—reflecting changing scholarly understandings of art-historical periodization and curatorial focus1.

Today, the Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts oversees responsibility for more than sixty thousand works forming The Met's expansive and historically significant collection1. These objects, made for and by Europeans, reflect artistic innovations, stylistic developments, global commerce, cultural exchanges, and collecting practices spanning the late fifteenth through the early twentieth centuries1. The departmental collection encompasses remarkable breadth across multiple media and object categories, including sculpture in various dimensions and mediums, woodwork and furniture, ceramics and glass, metalwork, goldsmiths work and jewelry, tapestries and textiles, horology and scientific instruments1. Objects within the collection range from domestic to devotional, functional to decorative, and scientific to whimsical, collectively telling a history of design innovation as Europeans responded to artistic stimuli imported from around the globe1. The department's collections demonstrate extraordinary craftsmanship and virtuosity across multiple materials, with works pushing and probing the capabilities of their media to create objects of breathtaking aesthetic power, awe-inspiring scale, or extraordinary intricacy1. Among the department's most renowned masterpieces are Tullio Lombardo's statue of Adam, Houdon's portrait bust of Diderot, Jean-Baptiste Carpeaux's group Ugolino and his Sons, and an extensive collection of sculptures by Rodin and Degas34,34.

The physical presentation of the Department's collections spans fifty-five galleries throughout The Met's building complex, with galleries numbered 500-556 and primarily located on the first floor1. Many of these galleries are enclosed medium-sized spaces that feature mixed displays of sculpture and decorative arts organized by region and date, while others are expansive environments designed to showcase monumental works1. The sky-lit, luminous Petrie Court houses monumental figurative sculpture of the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries, providing natural light and dramatic spatial framing for large-scale works1. The arcaded courtyard from the Castle of Vélez Blanco, a jewel of Spanish sixteenth century architecture, displays large-scale sixteenth century sculptures within an authentically reconstructed architectural setting1. The department has recently redesigned galleries with thematic features inspired by the objects within them, such as the Layered Narratives: The Northern Renaissance Gallery (Gallery 520), where the south wall contains an array of mounted stained-glass panels conjuring visions of sacred spaces, while the north wall suggests a "Kunstkammer" display like those assembled by sixteenth- and seventeenth-century collectors1. The Annie Laurie Aitken Galleries for British Decorative Arts (Galleries 509-516) reflect the visual exuberance, complex history, and bold entrepreneurial spirit characterizing British art and design from 1500 to 19001. While the majority of departmental holdings on display occupy Galleries 500-556, some works are installed throughout the Museum, in dialogue with artworks in galleries associated with the departments of Modern and Contemporary Art, European Paintings, American Art, Asian Art, Arms and Armor, Medieval Art, Musical Instruments, and Islamic Art1.

The American Wing: Decorative Arts and the Evolution of American Design

The American Wing comprises approximately 20,000 works of art by African American, Euro American, Latin American, and Native American men and women6,6,19. Visitors experience varied art, design, and culture from the mid-seventeenth to the mid-twentieth century, with some contemporary expressions, by a diverse array of artists from across North America, displayed across more than 75 galleries on three floors6,6. Since the American Wing's founding in 1924, this curatorial department has evolved its collecting to include artworks in many mediums by African American, Asian American, Euro-American, Latin American, and Native American makers, continually affirming increasingly inclusive definitions of American art and identity6,6. The dynamic holdings encompass painting, sculpture, drawing, furniture, textiles, regalia, ceramics, basketry, glass, silver, metalwork, jewelry, as well as historic interiors and architectural fragments, produced by highly trained and self-taught artists, both identified and unrecorded6,6. Monumental sculpture, stained glass, and architectural elements are installed in the Charles Engelhard Court, while silver, gold, glass, and ceramics are displayed on the courtyard balconies6,6.

A particularly significant aspect of the American Wing's presentation is the twenty historical interiors, or period rooms, that narrate the story of American domestic architecture and furnishings through three hundred years of domestic life6,6,24. These period rooms range from seventeenth-century Massachusetts to early twentieth-century Minnesota, providing visitors with intimate insights into the histories of these interiors and the lives of those who inhabited them6,6. The American Wing's 2024 centennial anniversary prompted a major reinstallation highlighting the department's history and ongoing evolution6,6. The Henry R. Luce Center for the Study of American Art functions as the American Wing's open-storage and installation space, featuring wide-ranging collections not on view in the main galleries, with objects arranged by material—painting, sculpture, furniture and woodwork, glass, ceramics, and metalwork24. The research opportunities afforded by this display make the Luce Center a focal point for education in the American Wing24. The Antonio Ratti Textile Study and Storage Center provides environmental conditions necessary for the long-term preservation of textile works of art at The Met, as well as study and research facilities for Museum staff and the general public1,6.

The decorative arts holdings within the American Wing reflect extraordinary technical mastery and stylistic innovation. Nineteenth-century American decorative arts demonstrate Federal, Empire, Gothic, Rococo, Renaissance, and Art Nouveau styles, with outstanding furniture makers represented including Duncan Phyfe, Charles-Honoré Lannuier, Joseph Meeks and Sons, and John Henry Belter9. Silversmithing is represented through works by Chaudron and Rasch, Fletcher and Gardiner, Gorham Manufacturing Company, and Tiffany and Company9. Glass manufacturing is exemplified through works from the Boston and Sandwich and New England Glass companies, while ceramics are represented by the Tucker Porcelain Company9. Decorative firms such as LePrince and Marcotte, Herter Brothers, and Louis Tiffany's Associated Artists demonstrate the sophistication of American decorative arts production during the nineteenth century9. Recent acquisitions highlight the American Wing's renewed commitment to strengthening and expanding collecting of works on paper by both well-known and historically understudied figures, including women and artists of color from diverse communities and across a broad geographic range27.

Internship Program Structure and Eligibility Requirements

The Metropolitan Museum of Art offers a comprehensive array of internship programs designed to accommodate students at different academic stages and career interests2,2. The institution distinguishes between multiple program types, each with specific eligibility criteria, duration, compensation structures, and learning outcomes. High school students have access to part-time, 10-week internships designed for current high schoolers2. Undergraduate and graduate students can pursue either fall and spring semester internships or summer internships, with both full-time and part-time options available2,28. Students enrolled in graduate-level conservation training programs within the United States can participate in full-time, 11-month conservation internships2,17. Recent college and university graduates have access to full-time, 9- or 12-month internships in specialized areas such as teen audience programs education, live arts, access and inclusion education, costume institute work, American decorative arts, digital content, Greek and Roman art, museum administration, and other fields2,10. Those who have completed a master's degree and PhD candidates are eligible for fellowship opportunities at The Met, which provide more advanced research and professional development opportunities2,2.

The Museum Seminar (MuSe) internship program represents the primary pathway for undergraduate and graduate students seeking comprehensive exposure to museum operations and professional development28,32. Fall and spring semester internships operate for 10 weeks on a part-time basis, with students committing 14 hours per week and receiving compensation of $24 per hour13. Summer internships extend for 10 weeks and offer both full-time and part-time options28. Full-time summer interns work 35 hours per week for $24 per hour and receive $1,500 toward housing or travel to New York City, while part-time summer interns work 14 hours per week for the same hourly rate28. Summer internship application deadlines occur on January 14, 2026, with notification by the end of April 2026, and internship periods running from June 2–August 7, 202628. Fall semester internships require applications by May 13, 2026, with notification in the second week of August and internship dates from the third week of September through the first week of December13. Spring semester internships have application deadlines on September 16, 2026, with notification in the second week of December and internship dates from the second week of February through the third week of April13.

The Tiffany & Co. Foundation Internship in American Decorative Arts represents a specialized 12-month long-term internship specifically designed for individuals with dedicated interest in American decorative arts10. This internship is awarded to an individual with special interest in American decorative arts and was established to provide invaluable training for the next generation of museum professionals in the decorative arts10. Interns develop knowledge and skills through hands-on projects researching and documenting the American Wing collection, special exhibitions, and additional departmental projects10. The Tiffany intern pursues an independent research project culminating in a small gallery installation10. Requirements include completion of a master's degree in a field related to American decorative arts by the beginning of the internship program in June, or completion of such a degree no more than one year prior to the application deadline10. Applicants must demonstrate strong dedication to American culture and American decorative arts from 1700–1930, with strong writing skills, ability to work independently, and enthusiasm for hands-on experience10. Compensation includes $26 per hour, $1,500 toward housing or travel to New York City, $3,000 stipend available for research and educational travel, and long-term interns receive benefits including health insurance10. The application deadline for the Tiffany internship is January 14, 2026, with notification by the end of April 2026, and internship dates from June 2, 2026–May 28, 202710.

Application Requirements, Selection Criteria, and Evaluation Rubrics

The Metropolitan Museum of Art employs a rigorous, multi-stage application review process designed to identify candidates who demonstrate not only strong academic preparation and relevant experience but also genuine passion for museums and commitment to contributing to an inclusive institutional culture13,16,28. Applications for undergraduate and graduate internships require submission of four core components: short essay responses to prompts specified on the application form, an unofficial academic transcript, a resume describing work or extracurricular experiences, and two letters of recommendation, one of which must come from a professor or instructor who knows the applicant in an academic context28. Students applying for positions in the Design Department must additionally submit a portfolio in PDF format or link to an online portfolio28. All application materials must be submitted by specified deadlines, with the Museum making clear that it will not accept applications, application materials, or recommendations submitted after the deadline10,28.

The application evaluation rubric employed by The Met assesses five primary criteria, each scored on a three-point scale of Excellent, Satisfactory, or Weak16. The first criterion evaluates clear and relevant goals for learning and professional development16. An excellent score requires applicants to clearly articulate concrete, achievable goals for their career or studies that are highly relevant to The Met's internship program and connect to a specific project or department16. Satisfactory scores are awarded when applicants mostly articulate concrete goals that are somewhat relevant to The Met's program though the goals may fall outside what is achievable within a specific project or department16. Weak scores result when applicants weakly or do not articulate goals for their career or studies, or when goals are not relevant to The Met's internship program or to a project or department16. The second criterion assesses potential for professional, academic, and personal growth16. An excellent score indicates that the applicant demonstrates strong enthusiasm for art and museums, and the internship opportunity would add new skills, knowledge, and experiences that would result in a turning point in their life, career, or academic pathway in museums or the arts16. Satisfactory scores reflect some enthusiasm for art and museums, with the internship building on existing skills and knowledge to propel the applicant further along a personal, career, or academic pathway16. Weak scores indicate little or no enthusiasm for art and museums, with no clear demonstration of how the internship would add or expand new skills, knowledge, and experiences16.

The third criterion addresses contribution to an inclusive Met community16. An excellent score requires that the applicant's education and experiences significantly reflect, engage, elevate, or positively contribute to the Museum's commitments to anti-racism and an inclusive community16. Satisfactory scores indicate that education and experiences somewhat reflect, engage, elevate, or positively contribute to these commitments16. Weak scores reflect that education and experiences do not reflect, engage, elevate, or positively contribute to The Met's commitments16. The fourth criterion evaluates preparedness for learning16. An excellent score reflects a high level of personal or professional responsibility and commitment, with consistently excellent academic performance16. Satisfactory scores indicate adequate levels of personal or professional responsibility with consistently satisfactory academic performance16. Weak scores reflect low levels of personal or professional responsibility and commitment with consistently weak academic performance16. Importantly, The Met emphasizes that reviewers look across all application materials to assess preparedness for learning, and that a few low grades will not eliminate an applicant from consideration16,32.

The fifth criterion addresses potential for exposure to art and cultural sector16. An excellent score is awarded when the applicant demonstrates few or no prior experiences with museums or cultural institutions, reflecting potential for transformation through the internship experience16. Satisfactory scores indicate some prior experiences in museums or cultural institutions but not a similar or analogous experience to an internship at The Met16. Weak scores result when applicants demonstrate substantial prior experiences at museums or cultural institutions, including internships similar to those offered at The Met, with reviewers questioning how The Met's program can offer something new and how the applicant would continue to grow from this experience16,32. This inverse scoring reflects The Met's institutional commitment to expanding access to museum careers among students with limited prior exposure to arts and culture due to systemic barriers or unequal access.

Application essays require careful attention and customization31,35,35. The first essay prompt asks why the applicant is interested in interning at The Met and what they would like to get out of two specific departments35. This essay demands deep research into departmental holdings, archives, special projects, and staff expertise35. Generic responses expressing admiration for The Met as an institution are insufficient; instead, applicants should identify specific collections, exhibitions, archival materials, or departmental methodologies that align with their scholarly interests35. The second essay prompt asks about a memorable experience the applicant had surrounding art or museums and how it impacted their worldview35. This essay should convey authentic connection to the field, potentially drawing on personal experiences that illuminate why the applicant feels called to museum work35. The third essay addresses diversity and inclusion, asking what about the applicant's background would contribute to the diverse workplace The Met seeks to build35. Applicants can address identity, lived experiences, communities served, or perspectives developed through teaching or volunteer work35.

Research Resources, Study Centers, and Learning Opportunities

The Metropolitan Museum of Art provides extraordinary research infrastructure supporting curatorial work, scholarly investigation, and public education. Thomas J. Watson Library, the Met's research library, contains more than one million volumes and extensive digital collections, constituting one of the world's most comprehensive art libraries22,30. While Watson Library currently undergoes renovation with expected reopening in spring 2026, the library team remains available online to assist research inquiries and provide limited scanning services30. Specialized libraries and study rooms include The Cloisters Library and Archives, collecting research material for the study of medieval art and related topics with approximately 15,000 volumes encompassing medieval architecture, painting, illuminated manuscripts, stained glass, sculpture, and decorative arts22. The Irene Lewisohn Costume Reference Library contains 30,000 books and periodicals plus over 1,500 designer files pertaining to fashion history and costume from around the world22. The Robert Goldwater Library is a noncirculating research facility dedicated to documentation of visual arts of sub-Saharan Africa, Pacific Islands, and Native and Precolumbian America22. The Joyce F. Menschel Photography Library contains more than 10,000 volumes relating to photography history and The Met's photograph collection22. The Onassis Library for Hellenic and Roman Art houses 17,000 volumes for study of Greek and Roman art, specializing in early archaeological literature, exhibition and auction catalogues, sculpture, and vase painting22.

The Antonio Ratti Textile Center provides environmental conditions necessary for long-term preservation of textile works of art at The Met, as well as study and research facilities for Museum staff and the general public1,11,22,24. The Henry R. Luce Center for the Study of American Art functions as the American Wing's open-storage installation space, featuring collections not on display in main galleries, with objects arranged by material and providing research opportunities that make the Luce Center a focal point for education22,24. The Study Room for Drawings and Prints allows qualified visitors to examine most works on paper not on display, along with reference library resources on print and drawing history22. The Study Room for Photographs contains approximately 75,000 photographs spanning the medium's entire history, with almost all eligible for firsthand inspection22. The Leonard A. Lauder Research Center for Modern Art serves as a catalyst for scholarship on modernism across media, disciplines, and geographies22. The Bluff Collaborative for Research on Dada and Surrealism coordinates scholarly investigation of these artistic movements22.

The Met's research initiatives extend beyond traditional study centers. The provenance research team works with curators, conservators, scientists, and internal and external partners in conducting systematic review of the Museum's collection provenance and ensuring that all objects entering the collection meet strict collecting policies22. Archaeological research programs ground research partnerships and upcoming exhibitions, as exemplified by archaeological research in Sardinia, Crete, and the Cyclades undertaken during 2024–2025 by the Department of Greek and Roman Art4. The Museum maintains comprehensive digital collections through the Thomas J. Watson Digital Collection, providing access to rare and unique research materials including auction catalogues, Met publications, photographic albums, ephemeral materials, illustrated books, and archival collections22. MetPublications online portal features over 1,700 titles, including books, guides, Bulletins, and Journals from the past six decades, with over 1,400 out-of-print titles fully available for online reading, searching, and downloading as PDFs38,39.

Decorative Arts Conservation and Technical Study

The Department of Objects Conservation plays a vital role in maintaining The Met's decorative arts collections in stable condition for handling, storage, transport, exhibition, and preservation for future generations43. Conservators engage with physical properties of works of art through remedial treatment, preventive measures, and evaluation for display, publication, loan, or acquisition43. Initial examination of each work involves investigation and documentation of materials and manufacture, alterations undergone, and current state of preservation43. Conservators employ the human eye as the most important examination tool, often aided by magnification and supplemented with specialized techniques including radiography, multiband imaging, reflectance transformation imaging, polarized and ultraviolet microscopy, and reflected light microscopy43. Preventive conservation activities address ambient light levels, temperature, relative humidity, pest mitigation, storage conditions, and exhibition materials to minimize deterioration43.

Specialists within Objects Conservation focus on specific decorative arts categories. Mechthild Baumeister, who has worked at The Met since 1988, specializes in examination and treatment of furniture, wooden objects, and historic interiors in the Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts, and recently supervised conservation and reinstallation of historic interiors, furniture, and wooden artifacts in the new British galleries29. Anne Grady, who officially joined the Museum in 2016, is responsible for post-Renaissance European and American decorative arts, having previously worked on sculpture and design objects at the Museum of Modern Art and Los Angeles County Museum of Art29. Marina Kastan is responsible for conservation of leather and upholstered artworks in several curatorial departments, including the American Wing and European Sculpture and Decorative Arts, with research focusing on complex composite objects that are simultaneously aesthetic and functional29. Vicki Parry, who joined The Met in 2002, is responsible for conservation of ceramics and stone objects in the Department of Asian Art and also conserves decorative arts from various materials such as cloisonné enamel and amber29. Pascale Patris, who joined Objects Conservation in 1994, specializes in research and treatment of painted and gilded wood surfaces of decorative arts from medieval Europe through Italian Renaissance and Baroque to nineteenth-century Gilded Age America29. These conservation specialists engage in both remedial treatments and scholarly research that advance understanding of materials, manufacturing techniques, deterioration processes, and conservation methodologies43.

Notable Collections, Major Acquisitions, and Curatorial Leadership

The Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts distinguishes itself through ownership of extraordinary masterworks and thematic collections that illuminate multiple dimensions of European cultural production. The department maintains distinguished works of Italian Renaissance sculpture, eighteenth-century French sculpture, and an extensive collection of sculptures by Rodin and Degas34,34. Best-known masterpieces in marble include Tullio Lombardo's statue of Adam, Houdon's portrait bust of Diderot, and Jean-Baptiste Carpeaux's group Ugolino and his Sons34,34. Major areas of the collection encompass French and English furniture and silver, Italian bronzes, goldsmithwork, maiolica and glass, and French and German porcelain34,34. The Wrightsman Collection represents a seminal gift to The Met, transforming the institution's holdings of decorative arts7,49. Charles and Jayne Wrightsman's magnificent gifts include French furniture, gilt-bronze works, porcelains, and architectural elements from famous eighteenth-century French houses7. The Wrightsman Galleries display splendid examples of French decorative arts and several salons from famous eighteenth-century French houses34,34. Meissen and Sèvres porcelains greatly amplify the Metropolitan's holdings, among the world's most important, with acquisitions including a flock of Meissen birds acquired in 19767.

The Untermyer Collection has transformed The Metropolitan's collection of English decorative arts into one of the most important outside England3. Judge Irwin Untermyer, by gift and bequest, gave to the Museum one of the finest and largest collections of English decorative arts dating from the sixteenth century to the end of the eighteenth, as well as an exceptional collection of Continental porcelain and bronze sculpture3. Judge Untermyer was the first in the United States to collect English needlework, and beginning in the early 1940s was among the first in the country to collect Meissen porcelains3. His later acquisitions included outstanding examples of English furniture and English silver, with careful attention to complementing rather than duplicating existing Museum holdings3. The Hoentschel Collection, originally gifted by J. Pierpont Morgan, comprises French seventeenth- and eighteenth-century paneling, furniture, decorative paintings, and gilt-bronze mounts that catalyzed creation of the department itself1,48.

The curatorial team leading the Department of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts comprises leading experts whose research advances scholarly understanding of decorative arts production and conservation. Sarah E. Lawrence, the Iris and B. Gerald Cantor Curator in Charge of European Sculpture and Decorative Arts, oversees departmental administration and curatorial direction44. Denise Allen, who joined the department in 2014, specializes in Italian Renaissance sculpture with focus on Benvenuto Cellini, having previously curated exhibitions on bronze statuettes at the Frick Collection15. Wolf Burchard, curator of The Met's British Galleries since joining ESDA in 2019, previously served as Furniture Research Curator at the National Trust and Curatorial Assistant at the Royal Collection15. Elizabeth Cleland, working at The Met since 2004, is responsible for post-medieval European textiles15. Daniëlle Kisluk-Grosheide is responsible for French and Dutch decorative arts15. Wolfram Koeppe, who joined The Met in 1992, lectures, teaches, and is involved in media programs and development efforts15. Iris Moon, who joined in 2017, is responsible for European ceramics and glass and is currently organizing an exhibition on Chinoiserie, women, and the porcelain imaginary15. Elyse Nelson oversees the eighteenth- and nineteenth-century European sculpture collection15.

The American Wing's curatorial team similarly represents deep expertise in American decorative arts and design. Sylvia Yount, Lawrence A. Fleischman Curator in Charge, oversees administrative and curatorial direction and has prioritized broadening the department's collecting to be more inclusive of underrepresented artists19. Alyce Perry Englund, Curator of American Decorative Arts, oversees seventeenth to early nineteenth-century furniture and Shaker collections19. Medill Higgins Harvey oversees American silver, jewelry, and other metalwork as well as mid-nineteenth-century furniture19. Adrienne Spinozzi, who joined the American Wing in 2007, oversees American redware, stoneware, and art pottery collections and has curated exhibitions examining nineteenth-century stoneware and the contributions of enslaved potters19. These curators' scholarship and exhibitions shape how decorative arts are presented to public audiences and understood within broader art-historical narratives.
