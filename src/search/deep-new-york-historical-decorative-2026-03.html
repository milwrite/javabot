The New York Historical Society's Decorative Arts Department and Collections: A Comprehensive Overview for Prospective Interns

The New York Historical Society stands as one of America's most significant cultural institutions, housing an extraordinary collection of decorative arts that spans centuries of aesthetic innovation and cultural production. Founded in 1804, the institution has systematically assembled one of the world's most comprehensive collections of American decorative arts, encompassing everything from Tiffany glass to Elie Nadelman's folk art assemblage to contemporary artisan works. For prospective interns seeking to develop expertise in museum collections management, decorative arts curation, and material culture studies, the New York Historical offers unparalleled opportunities to engage directly with masterworks while contributing meaningfully to scholarly research and exhibition development. This report provides a detailed examination of the institution's decorative arts department, its celebrated collections, current exhibition programming, available internship pathways, and the professional development opportunities available to emerging museum professionals.

Historical Foundations and Institutional Mission of the New York Historical

The New York Historical was established by eleven civic-minded founders who had witnessed the tumultuous years of the American Revolution and the British occupation of New York1,1. These founders—John Pintard, William Linn, John N. Abeel, Samuel Bayard, David Hosack, Anthony Bleecker, Samuel Miller, John M. Mason, DeWitt Clinton, Peter G. Stuyvesant, and Egbert Benson—were motivated by a conviction that their contemporaries needed to take deliberate action to preserve evidence of their historical moment, fearing that "dust and obscurity" would be the fate of accounts and artifacts if left in the hands of private individuals1,1. The founders articulated a foundational principle that continues to guide the institution today: "Without the aid of original records and authentic documents," they asserted, "history will be nothing more than a well-combined series of ingenious conjectures and amusing fables."1,1 This commitment to preserving authentic material culture rather than relying on interpretation alone has shaped every aspect of the New York Historical's development, particularly in its approach to collecting and exhibiting decorative arts.

Since its founding over two centuries ago, the New York Historical has evolved into what it characterizes as "a museum of museums and a collection of collections," serving as a leading cultural institution that documents over four hundred years of American history through a peerless collection of art, documents, and artifacts1,1. The institution's mission extends beyond mere preservation to encompass groundbreaking exhibitions, acclaimed educational programs for teachers and students nationwide, and thought-provoking conversations among leading scholars, journalists, and thinkers about the past, present, and future of the American experiment1,1. Under the current leadership of President and CEO Louise Mirrer, the institution has made a deliberate commitment to elevating the perspectives and scholarship that define the United States' democratic heritage while challenging visitors to shape ongoing history for the better1,1. The decorative arts department operates within this broader institutional context, serving as a crucial vehicle for understanding how material culture reflects and shapes American identity across different periods, communities, and social groups.

Scope and Significance of the Decorative Arts Collections

The New York Historical's decorative arts holdings represent one of the most encyclopedic and carefully curated assemblages of American material culture in any museum collection. The institution's art holdings comprise more than 1.6 million works in total, with the decorative arts collection constituting a substantial and extraordinarily significant portion of this vast assemblage4. What distinguishes the New York Historical's decorative arts holdings from other major museum collections is not merely the quantity of objects—though the collection is undoubtedly vast—but rather the institutional commitment to understanding decorative arts as documents of cultural history, technological innovation, and aesthetic aspiration. The decorative arts department approaches objects not as isolated beautiful things but as material evidence that illuminates how people lived, worked, believed, and imagined their futures.

Among the most celebrated elements of the New York Historical's decorative arts collection are its unparalleled holdings in Tiffany glass and metalwork4,13. The institution houses one of the largest and most encyclopedic Tiffany lamp collections in the world, featuring one hundred illuminated lamps displayed within a dramatically lit gallery space on the fourth floor of the museum13. This collection includes multiple examples of the iconic Dragonfly shade, a unique Dogwood floor lamp dating to approximately 1900-1906, a Wisteria table lamp from around 1901, and a rare, elaborate Cobweb shade mounted on a Narcissus mosaic base dating to approximately 1902, among numerous other exemplary pieces13. The significance of the Tiffany collection extends beyond the aesthetic appeal of these luminous objects to encompass crucial historical narratives about design, labor, gender, and technological innovation in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

Perhaps equally important to the museum's understanding of its Tiffany collection is its recent scholarship revealing the previously unacknowledged contributions of women designers and artisans to the creation of these celebrated works. The New York Historical now emphasizes the role of Clara Driscoll, who served as head of the Women's Glass Cutting Department at Tiffany Studios from 1892 to 1909 and has been identified as the designer of many of the firm's most celebrated leaded glass shades13. Known informally as the "Tiffany Girls," Driscoll and her staff labored in anonymity but were exceptionally well compensated, with Driscoll receiving a weekly salary of thirty-five dollars, equivalent to that of Tiffany's male designers and reflecting his regard for her considerable abilities13. This reframing of the Tiffany collection to center the contributions of previously invisible women workers represents the kind of interpretive scholarship that the New York Historical encourages among its interns and staff members.

The decorative arts collection also encompasses an extensive assemblage of American furniture spanning the colonial period through the twentieth century, including labeled examples by leading New York cabinetmakers such as Thomas Burling, Michael Allison, Duncan Phyfe, Joseph Meeks and Sons, and others who shaped the city's distinctive furniture-making traditions4. In the realm of sculpture, the New York Historical maintains an encyclopedic collection of more than eight hundred works documenting the full catalog of representational sculpture in America from the colonial period to the present day4. The collection includes important examples of Hudson River School paintings, iconic genre and history paintings, a vast range of American portraits, and all 435 of John James Audubon's extant preparatory watercolors for The Birds of America4.

The Nadelman Folk Art Collection and Contemporary Collecting Practices

Among the most distinctive and culturally significant holdings in the New York Historical's decorative arts department is the legendary folk art collection of sculptor Elie Nadelman and his wife Viola Spiess Flannery. Elie Nadelman was an avant-garde sculptor recognized internationally for his elegant, modernist works, but his equally pioneering passion involved collecting American and European folk art19,19. Influenced by the "peasant arts" of his native Poland and other European countries, Nadelman began collecting after immigrating to New York City in 191419,19. He met and married the wealthy and cosmopolitan Viola Spiess Flannery in 1919, and together they embarked on an all-consuming passion for collecting American and European folk art, assembling an impressive trove of approximately fifteen thousand objects19,19.

The New York Historical purchased the Nadelman collection in 1937, and the institution has devoted substantial curatorial resources to understanding, preserving, and interpreting this extraordinary assemblage19,19. The collection includes furniture, household accessories such as lamps and candlesticks, textiles, glass, ceramic objects, paintings, toys, weather vanes, sculptural woodcarvings, and chalkware, representing a strikingly comprehensive documentation of folk artistic practices across regions and periods4,19. The first major examination of the collection, presented at the New York Historical, showcased more than two hundred objects displayed to evoke the couple's groundbreaking Museum of Folk and Peasant Arts in Riverdale, New York19,19. The exhibition was curated by Margaret K. Hofer, Museum Director, and Roberta J.M. Olson, Curator of Drawings, and was accompanied by a fully illustrated catalogue titled Making It Modern: The Folk Art Collection of Elie and Viola Nadelman.

The significance of the Nadelman collection for interns and emerging professionals lies not only in the objects themselves but in what the collection reveals about curatorial practice and aesthetic judgment. The Nadelman collection demonstrates how collectors and curators can elevate vernacular and folk traditions to scholarly prominence, challenging conventional hierarchies of taste that have traditionally privileged fine art over functional or decorative forms. Interns who engage with the Nadelman collection develop nuanced understanding of how institutional interpretation shapes public understanding of material culture and how seemingly marginal or forgotten artistic traditions can be recovered and celebrated through museum practice.

Contemporary Decorative Arts Exhibitions and Curatorial Programming

The New York Historical's approach to decorative arts extends well beyond historical collecting to encompass vibrant programming that interprets contemporary and recent artistic production. A prominent example is the current exhibition Enchanting Imagination: The Objets d'Art of André Chervin and Carvin French Jewelers, curated by Debra Schmidt Bach, New York Historical's curator of decorative arts and special exhibitions6. This dazzling exhibition presents meticulously created objets d'art from André Chervin with his New York atelier, Carvin French, representing one of the most acclaimed makers of handcrafted fine jewelry in the world. Though Chervin is revered by jewelry experts and professionals, his name has remained largely unknown to the general public, as anonymity was essential to the firm's success—each jewelry retailer sold the firm's masterworks under their own brand names.

The exhibition highlights Chervin's artistic extension beyond jewelry into the creation of unique, precious objets d'art fashioned in gold and silver with gems such as rubies, diamonds, and sapphires, as well as masterfully carved semi-precious stones including jadeite jade, lapis lazuli, amethyst, and rock crystal quartz. Over six decades, Carvin French has manufactured thousands of magnificent pieces for distinguished shops including Tiffany & Company, Cartier, Bulgari, and Verdura. The exhibition features select pieces of Carvin French jewelry, including stunning one-of-a-kind brooches such as an extraordinary butterfly brooch scintillating with fancy vivid yellow diamonds framed in blackened steel, made for collector Ralph Esmerian. The main focus of the exhibition centers on Chervin's artistic extension beyond jewelry and his creation of bejeweled objets d'art, each evolving over the course of five to twenty-five years, with most measuring merely three to eight inches in height. Highlights include the poignant sculptural lamp "My Heavy Heart," a boudoir lamp that incorporates a large, shimmering, citrine heart poised atop an 18 karat gold wheelbarrow overflowing with colored diamond flowers.

This exhibition exemplifies the kind of contemporary decorative arts programming that attracts scholars and interns to the New York Historical. The institution's commitment to presenting work by lesser-known or historically overlooked makers and to recovering previously invisible creative labor—whether the "Tiffany Girls" or Carvin French's anonymous artisans—demonstrates how decorative arts curating can serve broader institutional goals of inclusivity and historical revision. Interns working on exhibitions such as Enchanting Imagination gain practical experience in research, object handling, exhibition interpretation, and public engagement while contributing to scholarly conversations about craft, luxury, and artistic innovation.

Curatorial Leadership and Professional Structure in the Decorative Arts Department

The decorative arts department at the New York Historical operates within a clearly structured curatorial hierarchy that provides multiple entry points for interns and emerging professionals. The department is led by experienced curators who have demonstrated expertise in material culture, design history, and museum practice. Debra Schmidt Bach, the curator of decorative arts and special exhibitions, has curated landmark exhibitions including Enchanting Imagination, providing mentorship to students and early-career professionals6. The curatorial team works collaboratively across departmental lines, integrating decorative arts scholarship into broader institutional priorities around inclusive interpretation and public engagement.

In January 2024, the New York Historical announced a significant promotion that restructured curatorial leadership at the institution. Wendy Nālani E. Ikemoto, PhD, a Native Hawaiian scholar who arrived at the institution in 2018 as senior curator of American art, was appointed vice president and chief curator, becoming the first Indigenous curator to lead the curatorial department of a major American historical institution31. Dr. Ikemoto received a BA from Stanford University and an MA and PhD from Harvard University, and has overseen the museum's collections of paintings, sculpture, and drawings while spearheading major acquisitions31. She also served in the inaugural cohort of faculty helping to develop the Master of Arts in Museum Studies program, a collaboration between the New York Historical and the CUNY School of Professional Studies designed to diversify curatorial ranks at American museums31.

Alongside Dr. Ikemoto's promotion, Catriona Schwartz was named director of public programs, Lily Wong was appointed curator of history exhibitions with over ten years of experience managing and curating exhibitions and installations, and Allison Robinson, PhD, was named associate curator of exhibitions31. Dr. Robinson is a historian of United States and American material culture who first joined the New York Historical as a Mellon postdoctoral fellow in women's history and public history in 202131. This leadership team demonstrates the institution's commitment to nurturing diverse talent from within and to creating career pathways for emerging professionals who demonstrate scholarly rigor and curatorial vision.

Internship Opportunities in the Collections and Decorative Arts Departments

The New York Historical offers multiple internship pathways for students and emerging professionals interested in decorative arts curation, collections management, and museum operations. The most relevant internship track for students focused on decorative arts is the Collections Internship, which provides intensive, paid experience in collections management and database maintenance. According to the most current information available, the Summer 2025 Collections internship runs from June 9 through August 15, 2025, offering a part-time program of up to 180 hours at an hourly rate of $17.003,3,3,3. The fall 2025 program runs from October 6 through December 12, with a part-time commitment of 120 hours at the same hourly rate44. Spring 2025 Collections internships run from February 10 through April 18, 2025, also structured as part-time programs of 120 hours at $16.00 per hour.

The Collections Internship is specifically designed for students enrolled in Art History, History, Museum Studies, or Studio programs with a demonstrated interest in museum collections management and registration10,3,44. Interns in this position assist with the management of new collections acquisitions and incoming loans for exhibitions, including preparation of checklists, correspondence, and inventories10,3,44. A core component of the internship involves learning how to properly house, record, catalogue, and organize collections items, understanding the physical and environmental requirements for preserving diverse materials10,3,44. Interns also gain substantial experience with The Museum System (TMS), the institution's collections database, uploading new images, assisting in standardizing data, and cataloging recent acquisitions10,3,44. Through this work, interns develop practical competencies in collections documentation while contributing directly to the institution's ability to manage and provide access to its vast assemblage of objects.

The Collections Internship requires excellent organizational and computer skills, as well as meticulous attention to detail10,3,44. Application materials typically include a cover letter indicating interest in the position and department, a resume, a 5-10 page writing sample, and two letters of recommendation, with at least one from a professor10,3,44. All applications must be complete and submitted by the stated deadline, and applicants selected for consideration will be contacted for an interview10,3,44. Interns who wish to receive academic credit for their work must submit written confirmation from their college stating that they will receive credit for hours completed upon hiring10,3,44.

Beyond the Collections Internship, the New York Historical offers specialized internship opportunities in history exhibitions and curatorial research. The History Exhibits Internship, which has appeared in recent iterations focused on topics such as LGBTQ+ life during the Harlem Renaissance, provides undergraduate and graduate students with the opportunity to assist the Museum Department's curatorial team in research for upcoming exhibitions5,5. The internship involves research with primary and secondary sources, preparation of research reports on specific topics, and integration into the exhibition planning team, allowing interns to collaborate closely with curators under supervision of the Curator of History Exhibitions while gaining valuable experience in research and exhibition planning5,5. This internship pathway is particularly valuable for students interested in understanding how decorative arts objects contribute to broader historical narratives and how material culture can be mobilized to address contemporary social and political questions.

Application Requirements and Professional Preparation

For prospective interns seeking to work in the New York Historical's decorative arts or collections departments, careful attention to application materials is essential. The institution emphasizes that complete applications, including all required letters of recommendation, are mandatory for consideration10,3,44. The writing sample, typically 5-10 pages, should demonstrate clear thinking about historical subjects, competence in research and analysis, and ability to communicate findings in accessible prose. Students should select a writing sample that showcases their strongest work—ideally an essay that demonstrates engagement with material culture, museum practice, or related scholarly topics.

The cover letter offers an important opportunity for prospective interns to articulate their specific interests in decorative arts and to convey enthusiasm for working at the New York Historical in particular. Rather than submitting a generic statement, applicants strengthen their candidacy by demonstrating familiarity with the institution's collections, current exhibitions, and scholarly priorities. For example, mentioning specific Tiffany objects in the collection, expressing interest in the Nadelman folk art collection, or referencing recent curatorial appointments and exhibitions demonstrates serious engagement with the institution's mission and work. Letters of recommendation should come from professors or advisors who can speak to the applicant's intellectual capabilities, organizational skills, attention to detail, and ability to work collaboratively with colleagues.

Prospective interns should be aware that the New York Historical has adopted a mandatory vaccination policy requiring interns scheduled to work on-site to provide proof of vaccination ahead of the start date and to follow health and safety measures put into place by the institution. The institution positions this requirement as necessary to protect the overall health and safety of the New York Historical community.

Educational Programming and Professional Development

Beyond the structured internship programs, the New York Historical offers extensive opportunities for professional development and education in decorative arts and museum studies. The Master of Arts in Museum Studies, offered through a collaboration between the New York Historical and the CUNY School of Professional Studies, provides students with comprehensive knowledge, skills, and preparation to excel as professionals in the museum community. The online degree program gives students flexibility to take classes while working and pursuing other obligations, while the practicum component at the New York Historical offers students the opportunity to use the institution's rich and varied resources to gain on-site experience in curation, collections management, and museum operations. The program aims explicitly to diversify the museum workforce and to address the needs of an increasingly diverse and engaged museum-going public. Scholarship funds are available to candidates who qualify, with support generously provided by Dr. Agnes Hsu-Tang and Oscar Tang, in honor of Dr. Louise Mirrer.

The institution also offers fellowship opportunities for scholars at various stages of their careers, including the prestigious Mellon Foundation Postdoctoral Fellowship in Women's History and Public History27. These fellowships provide scholars with deep access to the institution's collections and an intellectual community to develop new research and publications27. Fellows work from a dedicated study center on the Edith & Frances Mulhall Achilles Mezzanine and are encouraged to explore the collections, take advantage of the full scope of the Library and Museum's resources, and share their research during their tenure through informal talks and blog posts27.

Conservation and Preservation Practices

The New York Historical's commitment to decorative arts extends beyond exhibition and interpretation to encompass sophisticated conservation and preservation practices. The institution is currently constructing a state-of-the-art conservation studio as part of its major expansion project, which will house a professional team of four museum and library conservators using advanced technology to preserve rare and precious documents, decorative arts objects, paintings, and other artworks28. This conservation facility, designed by Samuel Anderson, widely considered the preeminent architect of conservation studios, will provide dedicated space for treating and restoring items from the Patricia D. Klingenstein Library and the Museum Division28.

For interns and emerging professionals interested in conservation practices, the New York Historical offers behind-the-scenes programming such as "A Behind-the-Scenes Look at Conservation," which provides visitors and prospective interns with insight into the complex work of preserving and treating objects18. Through such programming, the institution demystifies conservation work and encourages interest in this crucial dimension of museum practice. Interns working in collections management develop working knowledge of conservation priorities and learn how institutional decisions about storage, display, and handling affect the long-term preservation of objects.

The New Wing and Future Directions for Decorative Arts Programming

The New York Historical is currently undertaking a transformative expansion with the construction of a seventy thousand square foot new wing adjacent to its longtime headquarters at 170 Central Park West28. This major campaign, titled "Campaign for the Future of History," aims to ensure the future of the institution as it approaches the nation's 250th anniversary in 202628. The new wing will house multiple specialized centers and galleries, including the American LGBTQ+ Museum, the Patricia D. Klingenstein Library storage and research facilities, expanded classroom spaces, and the Stuart and Jane Weitzman Shoe Museum28. The Klingenstein Family Gallery will serve as a stunning first-floor main gallery functioning as a flexible space for various events and changing exhibitions28.

This expansion carries significant implications for decorative arts programming and curatorial practice at the New York Historical. The creation of dedicated gallery spaces and expanded storage facilities will enable the institution to display and preserve its extensive decorative arts holdings more comprehensively and to mount larger, more ambitious exhibitions interpreting material culture across different historical periods and social contexts. The new conservation studio will position the institution as a leader in the preservation and scientific study of decorative arts objects, potentially attracting conservation fellows and interns interested in advanced training in this specialized field. For prospective interns, the expansion represents an institution at an inflection point, expanding its capacity to undertake ambitious scholarly and curatorial projects while deepening its commitment to inclusive interpretation and broad public access.

The Tiffany Lamp Gallery as Model Exhibition Practice

The Gallery of Tiffany Lamps on the fourth floor deserves extended attention as a model of how the New York Historical interprets decorative arts objects to illuminate broader historical narratives. The gallery features one hundred illuminated Tiffany lamps displayed within a dramatically lit jewel-like space, creating a visceral encounter with the objects that emphasizes their aesthetic power and luminous beauty13. However, the exhibition's interpretive strategy extends far beyond aesthetic appreciation to encompass material innovation, labor history, gender history, and the cultural meanings of luxury in America at the turn of the twentieth century.

The interpretive materials accompanying the Tiffany lamp collection emphasize the hidden history behind the lamps and the contributions of women in their creation13. The exhibition notes that while Louis C. Tiffany (1848-1933) was the artistic genius behind Tiffany Studios, he was not the exclusive designer of its lamps, windows, and luxury objects13. Clara Driscoll and the "Tiffany Girls" who worked in the Women's Glass Cutting Department labored in relative invisibility despite the centrality of their creative contributions to the firm's aesthetic achievements13. Interactive elements in the gallery include a hands-on "Design-a-Lamp" experience and a diorama illustrating the rise of electrification, connecting decorative arts objects to broader technological and social change13. Kiosks share personal stories of individual Tiffany Girls, including dramatic readings from Clara Driscoll's letters, sources of their design inspiration, and details on the manufacturing process13.

For interns, engagement with the Tiffany lamp exhibition provides concrete example of how decorative arts curators can use material culture to tell complex stories about labor, gender, technology, and social history. Interns working on research projects related to the collection learn to move beyond aesthetic analysis to historical investigation, asking questions about who made objects, under what conditions, for whom, and what values and assumptions different objects embodied. This interpretive approach represents the cutting edge of decorative arts museum practice and the kind of sophisticated thinking the New York Historical cultivates among its curatorial staff and interns.

Objects Tell Stories: Thematic Exhibition Programming

The Objects Tell Stories exhibition in the North Gallery on the fourth floor provides another exemplary model of decorative arts curatorial practice at the New York Historical23. Themed displays present a variety of topics—such as slavery, war, infrastructure, childhood, recreation, and the COVID-19 pandemic—offering unexpected and surprising perspectives on collection highlights23. The exhibition features emblematic historical artifacts that chart key moments in American history, including a copper globe from 1542 detailing Giovanni da Verrazzano's exploration of the New York area, a draft wheel used in the lottery that sparked the Draft Riots in Civil War-torn New York in July 1863, and a silver subway controller handle used by Mayor George McClellan to drive the first subway car on its maiden voyage from City Hall in 190423.

Among the diverse objects displayed are George Washington's camp cot from Valley Forge, a Venetian blind retrieved from St. Paul's Churchyard in the days after September 11, 2001, stained glass dating back to 1650 from the time of New Amsterdam, a portrait of Seneca Chief Sagoyewatha, the massive painting Return of the 69th (Irish) Regiment, N.Y.S.M. from the Seat of War from 1862, a woman's safety bicycle constructed in 1900 which made riding easier in long skirts, and the only full-size model of Lincoln's head designed by Daniel Chester French for the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C.23 Each of these decorative and functional objects is interpreted to illuminate larger historical narratives about how people lived, what they valued, how they organized their social relationships, and how they understood their place in the broader American story.

Interns working on Objects Tell Stories projects learn to conduct detailed object research, to locate objects within appropriate historical contexts, and to compose interpretive materials that make complex history accessible to general museum visitors. The exhibition demonstrates how the New York Historical's decorative arts collection is not confined to objects traditionally classified as "decorative arts" but encompasses the full range of material culture that documents American life and experience. This expansive approach to decorative arts enlists interns in a democratizing project that elevates everyday objects and the experiences of ordinary people to equal status with fine art and the lives of the famous and powerful.

The Institutional Context: Collections and Research Library

The New York Historical's decorative arts collections exist within the context of a much larger institutional assemblage that includes an extensive research library and manuscript collections. The Patricia D. Klingenstein Library contains more than three million books, pamphlets, maps, atlases, newspapers, broadsides, music sheets, manuscripts, prints, photographs, and architectural drawings4. Among its collections are far-ranging materials relating to the founding and early history of the nation, including the first documentary evidence of the phrase "United States of America," one of the best collections of 18th-century newspapers in the United States, an outstanding collection of materials documenting slavery and Reconstruction, and an exceptional collection of Civil War material4. The manuscript collections contain over twenty thousand linear feet of archival materials including family papers and organizational and business records that document the lives of important New Yorkers and Americans as well as average citizens.

For interns interested in decorative arts in particular, these library and archival resources provide crucial context for understanding objects in their historical moment. Research into the papers of prominent collectors, the business records of furniture makers and merchants, the family correspondence of people who owned and used decorative arts objects, and newspaper accounts of exhibitions and acquisitions can all illuminate the meanings and values associated with decorative objects in specific historical contexts. Interns pursuing research projects in decorative arts are encouraged to integrate library research with direct engagement with objects, understanding that decorative arts scholarship is strengthened by attention to written as well as material sources.

Curatorial Assistant and Advanced Professional Positions

For interns who complete their initial internship and demonstrate exceptional aptitude and commitment, the New York Historical offers full-time professional positions that provide pathways to sustained careers in curatorial work. The Curatorial Assistant position, a full-time role supporting the Museum Department at $52,000 annually, exemplifies the kinds of professional opportunities available to individuals who have developed expertise and demonstrated their value to the institution47. The Curatorial Assistant supports the activities of the Museum Department, including exhibitions, acquisitions, and collection projects, working under supervision of the Vice President & Chief Curator47.

Essential job duties for the Curatorial Assistant include maintaining and updating exhibition checklists, editing and proofreading exhibition text and graphic files for the Chief Curator's review, working with the Rights Clearance Specialist to request images and permissions from outside sources, managing image files for exhibitions and publications, ordering installation photography, and providing administrative support for exhibitions and other department projects47. The position also involves providing administrative support for the organization of Board Exhibitions Committee and Collections Committee meetings, drafting loan letters, responding to collection- and exhibition-related inquiries from the general public, and undertaking research and curatorial coordination projects as assigned47.

Requirements for the Curatorial Assistant position include a BA in History, Art History, or a related field, knowledge of American art history specifically in the fine arts, two years of museum employment or related internship experience, superior writing and editing skills, and high organizational ability with attention to detail47. The position requires the ability to multitask and juggle multiple projects in a dynamic and fast-paced environment, proficiency with Microsoft Word, Excel, and Google Suite programs, and familiarity with museum collection databases such as TMS is a plus47. The New York Historical provides generous benefits for eligible employees, including paid time off, comprehensive health insurance, retirement plan contributions, flexible spending accounts, an Employee Assistance Program, and discounts at the NYHistory Store and on-site dining venues47.

Specialized Curatorial Fellowships and Advanced Research Positions

For scholars and emerging professionals further along in their careers, the New York Historical offers specialized curatorial fellowships that provide opportunities for intensive research and scholarship. The recent Curatorial Fellow in Native History position, funded by the Terra Foundation and focused on developing an exhibition about Gayëtwahgeh (Cornplanter), Sagoyewatha (Red Jacket), and early North American visual and material culture of diplomacy, exemplifies the institution's commitment to addressing previously marginalized historical narratives through its decorative arts and material culture collections16. The fellow in this position conducted in-depth research on objects in the preliminary checklist, explored potential additions including loans from other institutions and individuals, identified contemporary Indigenous creatives for involvement, and conducted outreach with Haudenosaunee communities, particularly the Seneca Nation16. The position carried a salary of $60,000 plus benefits, and fellows worked closely with the Vice President & Chief Curator, Assistant Curator of Decorative Arts and Material Culture, and Assistant Curator of History Exhibitions and Academic Engagement16.

These curatorial fellowship positions represent the institution's commitment to advancing scholarship on decorative arts from diverse perspectives and to bringing underrepresented communities' voices into interpretations of material culture. For interns who successfully complete initial internships and develop stronger scholarly credentials, these fellowship opportunities provide pathways to deeper engagement with the institution's collections and to contributing to major research initiatives.

Conclusion: Professional Pathways and Institutional Commitment

The New York Historical Society's decorative arts department represents one of the most intellectually vibrant and professionally rewarding environments for emerging museum professionals interested in material culture, curatorial practice, and historical interpretation. The institution's extensive collections—encompassing Tiffany glass, the Nadelman folk art assemblage, American furniture, sculpture, and contemporary decorative arts by artists such as André Chervin—provide students and early-career professionals with incomparable opportunities to engage with masterworks while contributing meaningfully to curatorial research, exhibition development, and public interpretation. The structured internship programs, ranging from the Collections Internship focused on database management and preservation to the History Exhibits Internship emphasizing curatorial research and exhibition planning, offer multiple entry points for students at different levels of experience and with varying interests within the museum field.
