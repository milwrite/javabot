The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation: A Comprehensive Examination of Institutional Purpose, the Humanities in Place Program, and Place-Based Cultural Heritage Initiatives

The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation has emerged as the nation's largest private supporter of the arts and humanities, representing a distinctive philanthropic approach that prioritizes cultural expression, historical preservation, and equitable access to knowledge across diverse American communities11,28,22. Founded in 1969 with initial assets of $273 million and currently maintaining an endowment of approximately $7.7 billion as of the end of 2024, the Foundation has distributed nearly $9.1 billion in grants since its inception, focusing its institutional resources on building what its leadership describes as "just communities enriched by meaning and empowered by critical thinking"4,4,28. Under the recent stewardship of President Elizabeth Alexander, the Foundation has undergone a significant strategic evolution that increasingly centers social justice within its grantmaking operations, a philosophical shift that fundamentally shapes how the institution approaches place-based cultural heritage work, the built environment, and humanistic inquiry across the United States.

The Institutional Evolution and Mission of the Mellon Foundation

The Mellon Foundation's contemporary identity and operational philosophy represent the synthesis of over fifty years of philanthropic commitment to humanities and cultural sectors. The organization was created in 1969 through the consolidation of the Avalon Foundation and the Old Dominion Foundation, bringing together the assets and legacies of multiple philanthropic traditions into a single institution4,4,4. The foundational assets were supplemented substantially by funds from the estate of Ailsa Mellon Bruce, whose contributions allowed the Foundation to grow from its initial $273 million to $700 million by 1970, and eventually to $880 million by 1980, the final year the Foundation received estate payouts4,4. This gradual accumulation of resources, combined with careful long-term investment strategies, has allowed Mellon to maintain substantial grantmaking capacity throughout multiple economic cycles and to increase its annual giving from $417.7 million in 2021 to more than $540 million in 20244,10,17,4.

The Foundation's contemporary mission reflects a fundamental belief that "the arts and humanities are where we express our complex humanity, and that everyone deserves the beauty and empowerment to be found there"28,11,38. This philosophical grounding emerged from decades of work in cultural institutions, higher education, and archival preservation, but has been substantially refined in recent years to explicitly incorporate social justice principles. As Elizabeth Alexander, who assumed the presidency of the Foundation, articulated in strategic communications, the Foundation's refined mission now focuses on building "just communities enriched by meaning and guided by critical thinking, where ideas and imagination can thrive"39. This strategic evolution, endorsed by the Foundation's board of trustees, represents what the organization describes as a "major strategic transformation" that prioritizes historically underserved and overlooked communities in all of its grantmaking operations39.

The Foundation's operational structure reflects its commitment to deep institutional immersion in the fields it supports. The organization employs approximately 160 staff members who work across four core grantmaking areas and multiple presidential initiatives, with program officers conducting substantial field research and relationship-building before extending grant invitations to most organizations28,38. This approach, which the Foundation describes as seeking to "get to know innovative organizations, scholars, and creators" before inviting formal proposals, differs substantially from purely competitive grant processes and reflects a philosophy that philanthropic impact depends on sustained engagement with field practitioners1,2,6,19,25,6,29,29,29,6,29,22.

Core Grantmaking Areas and Philosophical Foundations

The Mellon Foundation structures its philanthropic work through four integrated grantmaking areas, each reflecting distinct but complementary approaches to supporting knowledge production, cultural expression, and public engagement25,29,29,29,22. The Higher Learning program supports colleges, universities, and other organizations pursuing "new directions in humanistic study in a range of learning environments," with particular emphasis on rural universities, tribal colleges, historically Black colleges and universities (HBCUs), and institutions serving nontraditional and incarcerated students6,6,6,29. The Public Knowledge program focuses on archives, libraries, and organizations engaged in "creating, preserving, and making accessible cultural and scholarly records" that strengthen the cultural record and increase equitable access to deep knowledge19,21,25,29,29. The Arts and Culture program celebrates artistic practice, supporting "cultural organizations that are driving models of visionary leadership," institutions creating new forms of expression, artistic archives, and research that informs the creative sector2,2,23,2,2,29,29,22. The Humanities in Place program, the subject of particular focus in this research, brings "a variety of histories and voices into public, media, and memorial spaces, widening the range of complex public storytelling" by working with historic and memorial spaces, museums, and public experience conveners1,1,1,39.

Beyond these core areas, the Foundation maintains several signature Presidential Initiatives that represent long-term institutional commitments to particular social justice concerns. The Monuments Project, launched in 2020 with an initial $250 million commitment that was doubled to $500 million in 2023, represents the Foundation's most ambitious contemporary initiative, aimed at "transforming the nation's commemorative landscape to ensure our collective histories are more completely and accurately represented"13,16,17,10,10,36. The Imagining Freedom initiative, a $125 million commitment, supports artistic and humanistic work centered on the voices of people impacted by the U.S. criminal legal system, recognizing that "nearly half of American adults have a relative who has spent time in jail or prison"24,27. These initiatives demonstrate the Foundation's belief that cultural work and historical memory-making serve essential functions in pursuing broader social justice goals11,11,11.

The Foundation's financial architecture reflects its commitment to sustainability and perpetual grantmaking. The organization maintains a diversified investment portfolio across five asset classes designed to "preserve and increase its purchasing power, enabling Mellon to provide grants in perpetuity"4,4,4. In 2020, the Foundation issued $300 million in social bonds, a structure that allowed it to increase emergency grantmaking during the COVID-19 pandemic by $200 million above its originally planned grantmaking, demonstrating the Foundation's capacity to respond rapidly to field crises4,4,4. This financial flexibility proved crucial during the COVID-19 emergency, when the Foundation conceived and funded Creatives Rebuild New York (CRNY), an artist relief program that the organization describes as "largely unprecedented in philanthropy" in both its financial scale and scope10,17,10,10.

The Humanities in Place Program: Conceptual Framework and Operational Approach

The Humanities in Place program represents one of the Foundation's most innovative and place-centric grantmaking areas, launched in 2020 as the first newly established major program area at Mellon in more than thirty years33,1,1. The program's conceptual framework rests on a sophisticated understanding that "place" is not merely a geographical location but rather "a concept inextricably linked to language, culture, history, art, and knowledge production" that "informs the rituals and daily practices of community and can provide a foundation for individual and shared identity"33,36. This understanding drives the program's commitment to funding "place keepers, artists, institutions, and storytellers who shine a spotlight on historically overlooked sites, and the communities and stories that surround them"1,1,1.

The program directly addresses what Foundation leadership identifies as a critical contemporary need: for communities whose stories and identities have been "under-told, lost, threatened, or persecuted," the work of placemaking—understood as participatory processes to shape public places—and place-keeping, understood as "active and ongoing care for spaces," becomes especially vital33,36. Justin Garrett Moore, the program's director, articulates the program's human-centered philosophy concisely: "Humanities in Place is about human connection. The work you are doing is calling people in to learn about, and connect with, each other"1,1,1. This conception of place-based work as fundamentally relational and community-centered shapes both the types of organizations the program funds and the methodologies it encourages.

From 2021 to 2023, the Humanities in Place program made 176 grants supporting 158 distinct projects across multiple communities, representing 86 cities and reflecting "a broad" geographic and cultural reach33. The program's annual grantmaking budget reached $41.5 million in 2023, representing a substantial institutional commitment to place-based work33. This financial scale enables the program to support diverse types of initiatives ranging from small community-based projects to larger institutional efforts. The program accepts applications on a rolling basis, though like most Mellon grantmaking, most grants are made through invitation-only processes after program staff have conducted relationship-building and field research1,5,1,1.

Humanities in Place Grant Categories and Supported Activities

The Humanities in Place program supports three primary categories of work through grants to eligible recipients1,1,1. The program funds "innovative programming, advanced by community groups, that seeks to explore and share cultural heritage for broad public benefit," recognizing that community-based organizations often hold deep knowledge about local histories and cultural practices that extend beyond what institutional archives preserve. The program also supports "initiatives that focus on often-overlooked communities or regions to broaden our understanding of American and Indigenous histories," explicitly prioritizing work that addresses historical marginalization and underrepresentation. Additionally, the program funds "cultural heritage sites, including museums and public spaces, that promote greater access and exchange of stories," recognizing that the physical infrastructure through which communities engage with history shapes both what histories are accessible and how they are understood1,1.

Specific examples of Humanities in Place funding reveal the program's diverse approach to place-based cultural heritage. The Community Foundation of the Lowcountry received support for a project titled "Country Onions, Sweet Potatoes, and Salt: The Unsung Ingredients of Southern Food," which explores the often-overlooked histories embedded in food traditions1,22,31. The National Public Housing Museum in Chicago, which opened in 2025 with Mellon support, represents an institutional effort to change narratives around public housing and urban history1,1. Projects span geographically from Alaska to Guam and across the borderlands to the Midwest, reflecting the program's commitment to supporting work across diverse American communities1,1.

The program's emphasis on place-keeping—ongoing, active care for cultural spaces—emerged from recognition that place-based work extends beyond initial project creation. Program staff identified that communities require sustained support for maintaining relationships with physical spaces, preserving documentation of community practices, and facilitating ongoing public engagement with places of historical and cultural significance. This framework shapes how the program evaluates potential grantees and the duration of grants it makes, often supporting multi-year projects that allow for sustained community engagement and relationship-building33,1.

Place-Based Cultural Heritage: Projects, Communities, and Impact

The Foundation's commitment to place-based cultural heritage manifests across numerous specific projects that demonstrate the conceptual and practical dimensions of the program. The Kootéeyaa Deiyí Totem Pole Trail project in Juneau, Alaska, supported in 2021, exemplifies how Mellon funding activates both cultural heritage and public spaces simultaneously31,31. The project commissioned Native artists in several southeast Alaska communities to create new totem poles honoring "Alaska Native presence and persistence along the public waterfront and the region's port of entry"31,31. This initiative represents not merely cultural preservation but active cultural creation by Native artists, acknowledging contemporary indigenous artistic practice while honoring historical traditions. The totem poles function simultaneously as public art, community markers, and educational resources, inviting both community members and visitors to engage with Alaska Native cultures in public spaces that had previously lacked such representation.

The University of Montana's initiative titled "Putting Indigenous Stories Back in the Picture" exemplifies how Humanities in Place funding supports place-based work within higher education institutions35. With Mellon support, the university developed a three-year project working to "provide a new model to incorporate Indigenous knowledge, histories, and perspectives into higher education and the university's humanities efforts, and to help preserve that knowledge." The project responded to a specific place-based need: Montana's state constitution includes a mandate to study and preserve Native American histories and knowledge, yet the university lacked sufficient infrastructure and expertise to fulfill this mandate. The project engaged all twelve of the state's federally recognized tribes, developed an Indigenous scholar-in-residence program in which "tribal elders and knowledge keepers visit the university and work with faculty and students to share and capture their stories and expertise," and worked to produce "culturally responsive and IEFA-compatible content" for the university's humanities curriculum35. This project demonstrates how place-based work operates at the intersection of institutional capacity-building, community collaboration, and knowledge preservation.

The NATURA Exhibition in Puerto Rico's El Yunque National Forest represents another exemplary place-based project, with Mellon funding supporting "art installations placed along a hiking trail" that "opened pathways for people to explore the links between art and nature"31,31. Specific installations, such as "Memoriam" by artists Jaime and Javier Suárez and "El Portal del Yunque: Presence and Lichen" by Ivelisse Jiménez, engaged visitors in unexpected perspectives on creativity and local ecosystems17,31,31. These projects transform natural spaces into sites of artistic and intellectual engagement, making visible the connections between environmental history, artistic practice, and community identity.

The Ireichō, or "Sacred Book of Names," supported by Mellon in March 2022, represents a distinct but related form of place-based cultural heritage work31,31. The project compiled the names of all 125,000 people of Japanese ancestry who were incarcerated by the U.S. government during World War II in digital and sacred form, creating what supporters describe as "a first-of-its kind book" that "invites all visitors to see and honor the generations-long impact of that collective experience"11,11,31,31. The project transforms individual names and experiences into a collective memorial, functioning simultaneously as historical documentation, cultural memory work, and community healing practice. The Ireichō demonstrates how place-based heritage work operates not only through geographically specific locations but also through gathering and honoring names and stories that inscribe otherwise marginalized communities into the cultural record.

The Built Environment, Placemaking, and Community-Engaged Design

The Foundation's engagement with the built environment—the physical spaces through which communities experience culture, history, and shared life—represents an increasingly central dimension of place-based work. The UC San Diego Community Stations program, supported through Mellon's Arts and Culture and other programs, exemplifies how the Foundation funds projects that merge "architecture and urbanism, political theory and urban policy, as well as visual arts and the humanities" to address pressing community needs along the California-Mexico border20,17. The program operates four "community stations" that function as "laboratories for thinking of alternative programming," co-designed with community members and built with materials responsive to both environmental and economic considerations20,17. These stations integrate multiple functions—public gathering spaces, educational sites, housing development, food security infrastructure—into unified places that serve as models for community-engaged urbanism and "slow" development practices grounded in sustained collaboration rather than top-down imposition.

The National Public Housing Museum in Chicago represents another significant place-based project supported through Humanities in Place funding1,1. The museum opens a formerly overlooked cultural heritage site—public housing—to public historical interpretation and engagement, inviting visitors to encounter the histories and communities of public housing residents. This project exemplifies how Humanities in Place funding supports institutional efforts to broaden which communities and places are recognized as culturally significant and worthy of public commemoration and historical study.

The built environment also functions in the Foundation's work as an archive of and witness to historical processes. The National Audio-Visual Conservation Center in Culpeper, Virginia, supported through Mellon funding, provides "underground storage for what will be the most comprehensive collection of audio and visual media materials in the world"31,31. This physical facility preserves the material traces through which communities across time have documented and communicated their experiences, making the built environment itself a preservation infrastructure essential to cultural memory.

The restoration of the Robert T. Coles house in Buffalo, New York, supported through Mellon funding, represents yet another approach to the built environment and cultural heritage31,31. Robert T. Coles was the first Black architect in the Northeast to own his own practice, and his mid-century modern home stands as both an architectural achievement and a witness to African American professional achievement in a historically segregated profession. The restoration project combines architectural preservation with historical documentation and public interpretation, recognizing that buildings themselves are historical documents worthy of care and study.

The Monuments Project: A $500 Million Initiative in Place-Based Commemoration

The Monuments Project, while technically distinct from the Humanities in Place program, represents the Foundation's most ambitious contemporary engagement with place-based cultural heritage, built environment transformation, and the politics of public memory. Launched in 2020 with an initial $250 million commitment, and doubled to $500 million in 2023, the Monuments Project operates according to the conviction that "monuments and memorials powerfully shape our understanding of our country's past, and determine which narratives we honor and celebrate in the American story"13,16,17,10,10,36. The Foundation's leadership articulates the project's urgency by noting that "our country's current monument landscape offers an incomplete—and even inaccurate—picture of the nation's complex and diverse history," and that "this failure to represent our multiplicity impacts how we perceive, distribute, and demonstrate power in the US"13,16.

The Monuments Project operates across four defined areas of activity that reveal its comprehensive approach to transforming the commemorative landscape. First, the project "funds new monuments (broadly defined), memorials, and historic storytelling spaces," recognizing that expansion of the commemorative landscape requires not only contextualizing existing monuments but creating new sites of memory and meaning13,16,10,36. Second, it "supports reparative, community-driven actions around existing monuments, including contextualization, relocation, removal, and community visioning and engagement for sites of removed or relocated monuments," acknowledging that transforming the commemorative landscape involves complex processes of interpretation, debate, and decision-making13,16,10,36. Third, it "facilitates the production of storytelling and engaged scholarship (e.g., public and oral history, podcasts, websites, and digital technologies) that inform public understanding of how commemorative landscapes communicate, shape, and teach our history"13,16,10,36. Fourth, it "advances research that informs and supports practitioners, policymakers, and others working to transform the commemorative landscape"13,16,10,36.

Specific Monuments Project grants reveal the initiative's diverse methodologies and scale. The Emmett Till Interpretive Center in Sumner, Mississippi, received $691,750 in operational funding to support "racial healing efforts that include historic preservation, community building activities in the Mississippi Delta, and a year-long strategic planning process to coordinate the preservation of the Mamie and Emmett Till story across the Mississippi Delta and in Chicago"14. The Los Angeles County Museum of Art (LACMA) received $1.2 million over three years to support "Monumental Perspectives," a project developing "augmented-reality monuments and murals led by local artists and technologists" that "celebrate the region's diversity and use AR technology to reflect the inclusive perspectives from local communities that are often untold"14. MASS Design Group received $500,000 over two years to support a "Public Memory and Memorial Lab" providing "direct support to organizations on projects that engage memorialization, collective memory, and truth-telling"14. Prospect New Orleans received $2 million over two years to support an exhibition highlighting "monuments and their power to both create and complicate history, as well as new public works that engage how the city—still home to ten Confederate monuments—commemorates history"14. The Social and Public Art Resource Center received $5 million over three years to support "the preservation, activation, and expansion of one of the country's largest monuments to interracial harmony" through Judy Baca's Great Wall of Los Angeles, advancing "the work on the mural while also developing digital techniques and resources for future artists and enhance community engagement"14.

These grants demonstrate the Monuments Project's sophisticated understanding that transforming the commemorative landscape requires simultaneous work in multiple registers: creating new memorial spaces, contextualizing and potentially removing existing monuments, supporting community visioning processes, and generating scholarship and technological resources that help communities imagine and implement alternative ways of commemorating shared history. The Monuments Project has reached the halfway point of its $500 million commitment, having fortified support in 2024 "for communities across the country that are engaged in public memory work," including the Forecast Public Art for Midwest Memory project, the Oregon Remembrance Project, and the Bureau of Indian Affairs and National Native American Boarding School Healing Coalition's Road to Healing oral history project17,10,10,10.

Presidential Initiatives: Imagining Freedom and Social Justice Commitments

The Foundation's Presidential Initiatives, of which the Monuments Project is one, represent the organization's most direct engagement with social justice work through arts and humanities funding. The Imagining Freedom initiative, representing a $125 million multiyear commitment announced by the Foundation in 2018 and deepened in subsequent years, supports "artistic, cultural, and humanistic work that centers the voices and knowledge of people directly affected by the carceral system"24,27. As Foundation President Elizabeth Alexander articulates in describing the initiative: "The arts and humanities uniquely and powerfully counter some of the most enduring, far-reaching, and least seen impacts of mass incarceration in our country and on its individuals and communities"27.

Imagining Freedom supports work in three interconnected areas. First, it "advances the participation of system-impacted people in intellectual and creative communities" through fellowships and residencies that "cultivate their practices, grow their leadership capacity, and build supportive community—regardless of whether their works focus on the criminal legal system"24,27. Second, it "supports the creation and circulation of exemplary scholarly, archival, artistic storytelling, and other cultural work, that examines the experience of people who have been impacted by carceral systems, as well as the history and present of the systems themselves"24,27. This might include "research collaborations beyond and across academic institutions," "development and recognition opportunities for emergent scholars," "community-based and scholarly archival efforts," and "select storytelling projects with demonstrable potential to reach large audiences and shift narratives around incarceration"24,27. Third, it "broadens access to literature, library resources, and humanities-based learning communities within carceral settings to counter the intellectual and cultural deprivation caused by incarceration," supporting partnerships "with public libraries and other entities that provide broad access to books and research materials within prisons, as well as inside/outside collaborations involving communities of rigorous ongoing learning"24,27.

Specific Imagining Freedom grantees include Freedom Reads, a project led by poet and lawyer Reginald Dwayne Betts that has received over $5 million in Mellon funding since its launch in June 2020 and is "putting 500-book libraries in every prison in the United States"27. The New York Public Library received funding to expand "reference-by-mail services for incarcerated people," and the San Francisco Public Library (in partnership with the American Library Association) received funding for cataloging and growing library services available in prison27. The Million Dollar Hoods project at UCLA, led by historian Professor Kelly Lytle Hernandez, received funding to "archive historical documents, oral histories, and ephemera in order to chronicle the many impacts of policing and mass incarceration in Los Angeles"27. These initiatives demonstrate the Foundation's conviction that the arts and humanities serve essential functions in disrupting carceral systems and acknowledging the full humanity of people touched by mass incarceration.

Foundation Operations and Grantmaking Processes

The Foundation's operational structure reflects its philosophy of deep engagement with the fields it supports. The organization maintains a staff of approximately 160 employees who work across grantmaking areas and administrative functions, and the Foundation is committed to being "deeply immersed in the fields in which we invest"28,38. Most Mellon grants are made through invitation-only processes in which program staff conduct substantial relationship-building and field research before inviting organizations to submit formal proposals1,2,6,19,25,6,29,29,29,6,29,22. As Foundation materials explain: "Our staff seek out and get to know innovative organizations, scholars, and creators before inviting them to submit a proposal for funding"1,2,6,19,25,6,29,29,29,6,29,22. This approach reflects the Foundation's belief that philanthropic impact depends on sustained engagement with field practitioners and deep understanding of organizational capacity, community relationships, and project viability.

When organizations do submit proposals to Mellon, the application process requires comprehensive documentation of project scope, rationale, activities, budget, and outcomes. Humanities in Place proposal guidelines, for example, require "four pieces" including "information on the proposed grant and organization," "a proposal narrative document that provides detailed information on the proposed work," "supplemental documents," and "a budget and budget description outlining how funds would be spent"5. The proposal narrative typically addresses several core questions: "What need or issue does your proposed work aim to address? How are you positioned to do this work? What are the goals of the proposed work during the grant period? What activities would Mellon funding support?"5. The Foundation's expectations regarding budget detail reflect its commitment to ensuring that grant funds are used effectively and accountably, with specific guidance about allowable expenditures ranging from "course releases for participating faculty" and "course development funds" to "undergraduate research fellowships/stipends" and "funds to support costs associated with workshops and reading, discussion, and/or action groups"5,12.

The Foundation operates an invitation-based model for most grantmaking, though it occasionally releases open calls for proposals on specific topics. The Higher Learning 2026 Open Call for Concepts, for example, represents one of these targeted opportunities, inviting institutions of higher education to submit applications for research and/or curricular projects focused on two specific areas: "Unruly Intelligences" (exploring the humanities' role in understanding artificial and other intelligences) and "Normalization and Its Discontents" (examining the paradoxical concept of normalcy and its impact on society)7,8,12,30. This open call commits Mellon to providing $10 million through the Higher Learning program, with individual awards ranging from $250,000 to $500,000 for projects of up to four years' duration7,8,12. The application process requires institutions to register by December 1, 2025, submit concept notes with proposed budgets by February 17, 2026, and provide supporting documentation including endorsement letters from institutional leadership and brief curriculum vitae for principal investigators7,8,12.

The Foundation's indirect cost policies reflect its commitment to supporting organizational capacity while ensuring fiscal responsibility. "Generally, the Foundation will fund indirect costs at a rate of 20% of direct costs for all spendable project support grants, with certain exceptions," though different rates may be recommended for regranting, construction, or other specialized project types5,12. This policy recognizes that organizations require resources for administrative functions and facility operations while ensuring that the majority of grant funds support programmatic activities directly relevant to project goals.

Recent Strategic Initiatives and Evolving Priorities

In 2024 and early 2025, the Foundation announced and began implementing several significant strategic initiatives that reflect its evolving understanding of how arts and humanities funding can address contemporary social challenges. The Literary Arts Fund, announced in January 2026 as "a coalition of funders driving a $50 million initiative to support our country's not-for-profit literary presses and organizations," was launched under Mellon's leadership with the conviction that such organizations are "vital to broadening the reach of American literature and a fundamental part of our cultural heritage"11,11,11. The Foundation also launched a Jazz initiative with $35 million in total commitment, described as "an effort to celebrate musicians and preserve this beautifully original American art form"11,11,11. More recently, Mellon announced $6.5 million in grants dedicated to expanding jazz scholarship through funding for the Jazz Study Group, the Association for the Advancement of Creative Musicians, the Jazz Institute of Chicago, and The World Stage in Los Angeles26.

The Foundation also launched the Frontera Culture Fund through its Arts and Culture program area, "advancing the artistic visions driven by cross-cultural and transnational communities sharing both common objectives and complex challenges in the US-Mexico borderlands"17,17,10,10,10. The Humanities Internship Program represents another significant initiative, providing "paid career development opportunities" for college students engaged in humanities internships, reflecting the Foundation's belief that "everyone deserves meaningful access to the veracity, beauty, imagination, and continual revelation that the arts and humanities provide"11,11,11,31,31. The Foundation also made grants to establish Public Memory Labs at local libraries throughout the country, creating "centers for people across America to access equipment and services to aid in digitizing personal and family history collections, such as photos and home movies," often serving "community members who do not otherwise have the resources to bridge the digital divide or whose cultural histories have been underrepresented in American archives"17,17,10,10,10.

These initiatives collectively demonstrate the Foundation's evolving understanding of how arts and humanities funding can address contemporary challenges including economic inequality, cultural erasure, and limitations in public access to knowledge and cultural resources. The Foundation's approach, which President Elizabeth Alexander describes as "the roving spotlight of philanthropy," seeks to move funding "dynamically and inclusively across the fields we support" in recognition that "for too long funding for the arts and humanities—if it existed at all—was concentrated at a small number of institutions to privilege a narrow set of topics and perspectives"11,11,11.

Mellon Mays Undergraduate Fellowship Program and Pathways to Academic Careers

One of the Foundation's longest-standing programs, the Mellon Mays Undergraduate Fellowship (MMUF) Program, demonstrates Mellon's sustained commitment to expanding who enters and succeeds in humanities scholarship and academic careers. The MMUF operates across more than one hundred member institutions, including prestigious research universities, liberal arts colleges, and historically Black colleges and universities, providing mentoring and financial support to undergraduate fellows from rising junior classes as they "prepare for entry into PhD programs and eventual careers as scholars and faculty members"9,6. This program reflects the Foundation's conviction that expanding the diversity of humanities scholarship requires deliberate institutional intervention to support students from underrepresented communities through multiple stages of academic preparation and career development. By creating institutional relationships and peer cohorts, the MMUF aims to address documented barriers to graduate school entry and academic career success for students of color and first-generation college students.

Assessment, Accountability, and Grantee Relationships

The Foundation's commitment to understanding its impact and learning from grantees reflects its evolving approach to philanthropic accountability. The Foundation commissioned a comprehensive report titled "What's in a Place? More than Geography," published in 2024, which reflected on grantmaking through the Humanities in Place program from 2021 through 2023 and sought to "articulate how the program is taking shape within a much broader place-based ecosystem—one that includes practitioners, conservationists, grassroots organizers, scholars, activists, and other funders"33. The report, produced in partnership with consultant Lynn Ross and West Wing Writers and including input from Mellon grantees through surveys and interviews, addressed fundamental questions: "What do we mean when we say 'humanities in place'? What is this work? Where and how does it happen? Who leads it? And why does it matter?"33.

The Foundation's participation in the Grantee Perception Report (GPR), developed by the Center for Effective Philanthropy and administered by more than 300 foundations, demonstrates its commitment to gathering feedback from grantees on topics including impact on fields and communities, application and reporting processes, and grantee communications32.
