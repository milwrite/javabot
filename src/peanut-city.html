Peanut City

The rain in Peanut City didn't wash the streets clean; it just made the grime glisten. Like a polished shell. I was nursing a lukewarm drink at The Shell Breaker when she walked in. Tall, leggy, smelling of roasted cashews and cheap danger.

"Are you Sportello?" she asked. Her voice was smooth, like creamy peanut butter, but her eyes were hardâ€”salted and roasted.

"I don't take cases involving legumes," I grunted, staring at the neon reflection in my glass. The sign outside buzzed, a fly trapped in the zapper of urban decay.

"This isn't about legumes, detective. It's about the Salt. The Big Salt."

That got my attention. In Peanut City, salt was worth more than gold. More than blood. It was the only thing that kept the flavor of life from turning rancid.

"Talk," I said, turning to face her.

"The Mayor's private stash is gone. The premium sea salt flakes. Vanished from the high-security pantry." She leaned in closer, the shadows dancing across her face. "They say it was an inside job. Someone cracked the safe.

"If the squirrels are involved, I'm out. I don't do business with bushy-tailed anarchists."

"No squirrels," she whispered, her voice dropping an octave. "It was the Elephants."

The Elephants. The heavy mob. They never forgot a debt, and they never forgot a nut. They controlled the docks, the shipments, the very ground we walked on.

I slammed my glass down. "Double my rate," I said. "And keep the shells."

She nodded, sliding a manila folder across the sticky table. I grabbed my coat. The night was young, and the city was hungry. Time to see if the Elephants liked their peanuts salty.
